Harmony

-Harmony, in music, the sound of two or more notes heard simultaneously. In practice, this broad definition can also include some instances of notes sounded one after the other. If the consecutively sounded notes call to mind the notes of a familiar chord (a group of notes sounded together), the ear creates its own simultaneity in the same way that the eye perceives movement in a motion picture. In a narrower sense, harmony refers to the extensively developed system of chords and the rules that allow or forbid relations between chords that characterizes Western music.

7. how the principles of art (rythm/movement,balance,emphasis,proportion,harmony,unity,variety) are made use of by the photographer as an artist


Photographers as an artist, use their subject to invoke an emotion to their viewers and to convey their expressions. These art principles can be seen through the movement of the eyes and the over all composition of the image.

The principle of Rhythm, for example, forces the viewer's eyes to move around the image with strategic placement of different subjects or by showing a pattern which creates movement within the frame. One good example of this is a photo of a roof with tiles laid out on top of each other.

Balance, on the other hand, shows a juxtaposition or placement of contrasting images together in a photograph that makes the subject look equal in weight as the rest of the details in the frame. This is most commonly used when taking portrait photos. One good example of this is a photograph of a flower, at the dead center of the image with the grass horizon behind it.

Emphasis in photography lets the photographer showcase the subject as the main component of the photo where the viewer's eyes will directly look at. This can be achieved by proper framing or color-grading of the photo where the main subject can appear bigger or more vibrant than the rest of the composition. This technique can also be found in the principle of Proportion where the photographer uses the size of a subject to make it look more or less important than the rest of the composition. Proper framing of the proportion of a subject can also give the photograph an unrealistic illusion, which gives the viewer an abstract sense of the image.

The principles of Harmony and Unity may both seem similar but they are differentiated by the composition of the image. Harmony gives the photograph a more holistic and simplistic feel. This can be done with use of  subjects with hues that blend well together, like a forest of trees or the horizon where the blue sky meets the blue sea. Unity on the other hand, can be attained with creating a composition where each part of the photograph can stand good alone but looks better when placed together. Lastly, the principle of Variety, as the name suggests, is having different components in your composition to create a stunning image. It can be as simple as photographing a colorful house with a majestic blue sky as the backdrop or photographing different kinds of screws and nails, showing their different texture and sizes.
