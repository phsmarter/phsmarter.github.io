3. the unicorn in the Garden create a critique essaypa answer po.​


"The Unicorn in the Garden" is a short story written by James Thurber, first published in The New Yorker in 1939. The story has been widely read and analyzed, and is considered a classic of American literature. The story is a satirical commentary on the nature of reality and the way that people often interpret the world around them.

The story begins with a man telling his wife that he has seen a unicorn in their garden. She dismisses his claim as absurd, but he persists in his belief that the unicorn exists. When the man's psychiatrist arrives, he diagnoses the man with a mental illness and takes him away to a mental institution. The story ends with the wife discovering the unicorn in their garden, and the psychiatrist being shown to be wrong.

The story can be interpreted in a number of different ways, but one of the main themes is the way that people can be blinded by their own beliefs and prejudices. The wife, for example, is so convinced that her husband is wrong that she refuses to consider the possibility that he might be right. Similarly, the psychiatrist is so convinced of his own expertise that he dismisses the man's claims as delusional.

The story can also be seen as a commentary on the nature of reality itself. The unicorn is an imaginary creature, and yet it is able to exist in the man's mind and, ultimately, in the garden itself. This suggests that reality is not necessarily objective, but is instead shaped by our own perceptions and beliefs.

Overall, "The Unicorn in the Garden" is a thought-provoking and engaging story that raises important questions about the nature of reality, perception, and belief. Its satirical tone and clever plot twists make it a classic of American literature, and a valuable piece of reading for anyone interested in exploring the complexities of the human mind

17. lady and the unicorn tapestry era​


Answer:

It is the modern title given to a series of six tapestries created in the style of mille-fleurs (meaning: "thousand flowers") and woven in Flanders from wool and silk, from designs ("cartoons") drawn in Paris around 1500. The set, on display in the Musée de Cluny in Paris, is often considered one of the greatest works of art of the Middle Ages in Europe.

Five of the tapestries are commonly interpreted as depicting the five senses – taste, hearing, sight, smell, and touch. The sixth displays the words "À mon seul désir". The tapestry's meaning is obscure, but has been interpreted as representing love or understanding. Each of the six tapestries depicts a noble lady with the unicorn on her left and a lion on her right; some include a monkey in the scene.
