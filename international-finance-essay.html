International Finance: Benefits

Some of the benefits of international finance are:

Access to capital markets across the world enables a country to borrow during tough times and lend during good times.

It promotes domestic investment and growth through capital import.

Worldwide cash flows can exert a corrective force against bad government policies.

It prevents excessive domestic regulation through global financial institutions.

International finance leads to healthy competition and, hence, a more effective banking system.

It provides information on the vital areas of investments and leads to effective capital allocation.

19. ESSAY;What is working capital and explain its importance in managing the business' finances.​


Answer:

Working capital is a measure of a company's liquidity, efficiency, and short-term financial health. It represents the amount of money that a business has available to meet its short-term obligations, such as paying bills, salaries, and other expenses that are due within one year.

Working capital is important for managing a business's finances because it helps the company to maintain a healthy balance between its short-term assets and liabilities. If a company has a positive working capital, it means that it has enough resources to meet its current obligations, which is crucial for maintaining a good reputation and keeping the business running smoothly. On the other hand, if a company has negative working capital, it may struggle to pay its bills and meet its financial obligations, which can lead to financial problems and even bankruptcy.

There are several factors that can affect a company's working capital, including its level of debt, the efficiency of its operations, and the timing of its cash inflows and outflows. To manage its working capital effectively, a company may need to carefully monitor its current assets and liabilities, optimize its cash management processes, and make strategic decisions about how to allocate its resources.

In summary, working capital is a key measure of a company's financial health and is essential for managing its finances effectively. By keeping a close eye on its working capital, a business can ensure that it has the resources it needs to meet its short-term obligations and maintain a strong financial foundation.

25. Discuss the history and evolution of international financial institutions such as the International Monetary Fund, the World Bank, and the European Monetary System. How have these institutions impacted the global economic system, and what are their current roles in international trade and finance?​


Answer:

The International Monetary Fund (IMF), the World Bank, and the European Monetary System (EMS) are international financial institutions that were created after World War II to help manage the post-war global economic system. These institutions have played a significant role in shaping the global economy, supporting economic development, and maintaining international financial stability.

The IMF was established in 1944 to promote international monetary cooperation and provide member countries with financial assistance in times of balance of payments problems. The IMF’s main goals are to stabilize exchange rates, facilitate the balanced growth of international trade, and promote economic growth and employment.

The World Bank was established in 1944 as the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD) to help rebuild war-torn countries. It is now a group of five organizations that provide financial and technical assistance to developing countries to reduce poverty and promote sustainable economic growth.

The EMS was established in 1979 to promote monetary stability among EU member states by reducing fluctuations in exchange rates. The EMS was replaced by the European Monetary Union and the euro currency in 1999.

Over the years, these institutions have played a crucial role in shaping the global economic system. They have helped stabilize currencies, provided financial assistance to developing countries, and promoted economic growth and development. They have also played an important role in debt relief and restructuring, provided technical assistance and knowledge-sharing, and supported economic and social reforms.

The IMF and the World Bank have been criticized for promoting policies that prioritize the interests of developed countries over those of developing countries. Critics argue that these institutions have imposed strict conditions on the loans they provide, leading to austerity programs and reduced public spending on social welfare programs.

Despite the criticisms, the IMF, the World Bank, and the EMS (now the European Central Bank) continue to play a significant role in international trade and finance. The IMF regularly provides financial assistance to member countries facing balance of payments problems, while the World Bank provides loans and technical assistance to support economic growth and poverty reduction in developing countries.

The European Central Bank, meanwhile, plays a crucial role in promoting monetary stability and supporting economic growth in the European Union. These institutions are expected to continue playing an important role in shaping the global economic system for years to come.
