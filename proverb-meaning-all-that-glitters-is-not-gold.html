A proverb (from Latin: proverbium) is a simple, concrete, traditional saying that expresses a perceived truth based on common sense or experience. Proverbs are often metaphorical and use formulaic language. Collectively, they form a genre of folklore.

Some proverbs exist in more than one language because people borrow them from languages and cultures similar to theirs. In the West, the Bible (including, but not limited to the Book of Proverbs) and medieval Latin (aided by the work of Erasmus) have played a considerable role in distributing proverbs. Not all Biblical proverbs, however, were distributed to the same extent: one scholar has gathered evidence to show that cultures in which the Bible is the "major spiritual book contain between three hundred and five hundred proverbs that stem from the Bible,"[1] whereas another shows that, of the 106 most common and widespread proverbs across Europe, eleven are from the Bible.[2] However, almost every culture has its own unique proverbs.

22. II. Can you explain what these proverbs mean? 1. "All that glitters is not gold." 2. "A spark can start a fire that can burn the whole town." 3. "The old horse in the barn still yearns to run." 4. "A well-dressed monkey is still a monkey." 5. "Give a man a fish and you feed him for a day. Teach a man to fish and you feed him for a lifetime."​


Answer:

"All that glitters is not gold." Meaning: This proverb cautions against making judgments based solely on outward appearances. Something that appears attractive or valuable may not necessarily be so. It reminds us to be careful and not be deceived by superficial or flashy appearances, as they may not always reflect the true nature or value of a person or thing.

"A spark can start a fire that can burn the whole town." Meaning: This proverb emphasizes the power of small or seemingly insignificant actions or events to have far-reaching consequences. Just like a tiny spark can ignite a fire that spreads and causes significant damage, a small action or event can have a profound and widespread impact. It serves as a reminder to be mindful of the potential consequences of our actions, even if they seem inconsequential at the time.

"The old horse in the barn still yearns to run." Meaning: This proverb conveys the idea that age does not diminish one's desires, aspirations, or abilities. Even as a person grows older, they may still have the same ambitions, dreams, and passions as they did when they were younger. It underscores the fact that age is not necessarily a barrier to pursuing one's goals and that one can continue to strive for their desires regardless of their age.

"A well-dressed monkey is still a monkey." Meaning: This proverb implies that superficial changes or appearances do not alter the inherent nature or essence of something or someone. Regardless of how one may look or present themselves, their fundamental characteristics or nature remain unchanged. It serves as a reminder not to be deceived by external appearances and to look beyond superficialities to understand the true nature of a person or thing.

"Give a man a fish and you feed him for a day. Teach a man to fish and you feed him for a lifetime." Meaning: This proverb emphasizes the value of education and empowerment over providing temporary solutions. Merely providing someone with immediate assistance may only address their immediate needs, but teaching them a skill or providing them with knowledge and tools can enable them to become self-sufficient and self-reliant in the long run. It highlights the importance of empowering others through education and skills development for long-term sustainability
