An expiration date or expiry date is a previously determined date after which something should no longer be used, either by operation of law or by exceeding the anticipated shelf life for perishable goods. Expiration dates are applied to selected food products and to some other manufactured products like infant car seats where the age of the product may impact its safe use.[1]

Arbitrary expiration dates are also commonly applied by companies to product coupons, promotional offers and credit cards. In these contexts, the expiration date is chosen for business reasons or to provide some security function rather than any product safety concern.

In this scenario, the police officer's use of force can be justified if it meets the principle of proportionality. If the police officer had no other options available to prevent the terrorist from blowing up the mall, and if the use of force was proportional to the threat faced, then the shooting may be considered acceptable.

However, if there were other options available, such as negotiation or de-escalation, that could have prevented the terrorist from causing harm without the use of lethal force, then the shooting may not be considered acceptable.

Ultimately, the acceptability of the police officer's actions will depend on a case-by-case basis, taking into account the specific circumstances of the situation and the ethical principles at play.
