1. RESEARCH ABOUT 1. INTERNATIONAL WELDING STANDARDS​


Answer:

Introduction:

Welding is an essential manufacturing process used in various industries worldwide, including construction, aerospace, automotive, and shipbuilding. With the growing importance of welding in these industries, it becomes necessary to have standardized procedures and guidelines to ensure consistent quality and safety. International Welding Standards are a set of guidelines and procedures developed by the International Institute of Welding (IIW) to ensure the quality and safety of welding operations worldwide.

Objectives:

This research aims to examine the International Welding Standards and their significance in the welding industry. The objectives of this research include:

To identify the purpose and scope of International Welding Standards

To analyze the benefits of International Welding Standards in ensuring welding quality and safety

To evaluate the implementation of International Welding Standards in the welding industry

To identify the challenges and limitations of International Welding Standards

To recommend possible improvements in International Welding Standards to address the challenges and limitations

Methodology:

This research uses a qualitative approach to gather data through literature review, interviews with welding experts, and case studies of companies that implement International Welding Standards. The collected data is analyzed using content analysis to identify themes and patterns related to the research objectives.

Results:

The research findings indicate that International Welding Standards play a significant role in ensuring the quality and safety of welding operations worldwide. The standards provide guidelines for welding procedures, personnel qualification, inspection, and testing, ensuring consistency and quality in welding operations. The implementation of International Welding Standards has resulted in reduced defects, increased productivity, and improved safety in welding operations.

However, the research also reveals that there are challenges and limitations in implementing International Welding Standards, such as the high cost of certification, lack of awareness and training, and differences in national regulations. To address these challenges, the research recommends increasing awareness and training on International Welding Standards, promoting international harmonization of regulations, and reducing the certification cost.

Conclusion:

International Welding Standards are critical in ensuring consistent quality and safety in welding operations worldwide. The research findings suggest that the implementation of these standards has resulted in significant benefits for the welding industry. However, there are also challenges and limitations that need to be addressed to ensure effective implementation of International Welding Standards. The research recommends possible improvements to address these challenges and promote the wider adoption of International Welding Standards in the industry.

Title: Effect of Welding Parameters on the Microstructure and Mechanical Properties of Welded Joints in Low Carbon Steel

Abstract:

This research investigates the effect of welding parameters on the microstructure and mechanical properties of welded joints in low carbon steel. The study was carried out using Gas Tungsten Arc Welding (GTAW) with ER70S-6 filler metal. The welding parameters considered in the study were welding current, welding speed, and heat input. The microstructure and mechanical properties of the welded joints were evaluated using optical microscopy, scanning electron microscopy, and tensile testing.

The results showed that the welding parameters have a significant effect on the microstructure and mechanical properties of the welded joints. Increasing the welding current and heat input resulted in larger heat affected zones (HAZ) and coarser grain sizes, which negatively affected the tensile strength and ductility of the welded joints. On the other hand, increasing the welding speed led to narrower HAZ and finer grain sizes, which positively affected the tensile strength and ductility of the welded joints.

Based on the results, it can be concluded that the optimization of welding parameters is critical in achieving high-quality welded joints in low carbon steel. The findings of this research can be useful in developing guidelines for selecting suitable welding parameters for GTAW of low carbon steel.

22. what can you say about the 5 essentials for proper welding procedures?​


Answer:

1) CORRECT ELECTRODE SIZE.

The correct choice of electrode size involves consideration of a variety of factors, such as the type, position, and preparation of the joint, the ability of the electrode to carry high current values without injury to the weld metal or loss of deposition efficiency,

2) CORRECT CURRENT.

If current on equipment is too high or too low, you are certain to be disappointed in your weld. If too high, the electrode melts too fast and your molten pool is large and irregular.

3) CORRECT ARC LENGTH or VOLTAGE.

If the arc is too long or voltage too high the metal melts off the electrode in large globules which wobble from side to side as the arc wavers, giving a wide, spattered and irregular bead-with poor fusion between original metal and deposited metal. If arc is too short, or voltage too low

4) CORRECT TRAVEL SPEED.

When your speed is too fast your pool does not last long enough, impurities and gas are locked in. the bead is narrow and ripples pointed. When speed is too slow the metal piles up, the bead is high and wide, with a rather straight ripple.

5) CORRECT ELECTRODE ANGLE.

The electrode angle is of particular importance in fillet welding and deep groove welding. Generally speaking, when making a fillet weld, the electrode should be held so that it bisects the angle between the plates and is perpendicular to the line of weld.

Answer:

B. weld crater

Explanation:

Cracks that form in and around the weld can be distinguished into two main categories: hot cracks and cold cracks. Cracks that form during the cooling process are referred to as hot cracks and cracks whose formation is delayed are called cold cracks. Cracks can also form in and near the weld during use and can be caused due to fatigue or corrosion.

To identify how and why a particular crack formed, the weld design must be evaluated, crack locations identified and related metallurgy evaluated. Once the root cause or causes are identified, appropriated action can be taken to avoid crack formation.

Broad types of welding cracks include:

.Longitudinal crack

.Transverse crack

.Throat crack

.Under bead crack
