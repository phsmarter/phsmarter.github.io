Answer:

The bond formed between hydrogen (H) and bromine (Br) results from a sharing of electrons where H provides 1 electron and Cl provides 1 electrons to form H : Br or H-Br. This is a covalent bond because there is a sharing of electrons. You would know this because H has only 1 valence electron and Br has 7 valence electrons. Br would like 1 more electron to complete the octet, and so it shares the electron that H has. You would also know it is covalent because both H and Br are NONMETALS, and when 2 nonmetals bond together, they form a covalent bond.

It is a polar bond because of the difference in electronegativity between H and Br. Since Br is more electronegative, it will pull electrons toward itself, making the bond polar.

So, in summary, the H - Br bond is polar covalent.

7. leds of br? Bromine​


Answer:

Bromine is a chemical element with the symbol Br and atomic number 35. It is the third-lightest halogen, and is a fuming red-brown liquid at room temperature that evaporates readily to form a similarly coloured gas. Its properties are thus intermediate between those of chlorine and iodine. Isolated independently by two chemists, Carl Jacob Löwig (in 1825) and Antoine Jérôme Balard (in 1826), its name was derived from the Ancient Greek βρῶμος ("stench"), referencing its sharp and disagreeable smell.

14. what is origin of bromine​


Answer:

Bromine was discovered in 1826 by the French chemist Antoine-Jérôme Balard in the residues (bitterns) from the manufacture of sea salt at Montpellier. Because of the bad odour of the element, the French Academy of Sciences suggested the name bromine, from the Greek word bromos, meaning “bad smell” or “stench.”

28. What type of chemical bond is expected between bromine and bromine?


Between bromine and bromine covalent bonds will occur by using shared electron pairs

Further explanation

Elements based on electrical conductivity can be divided into two elements, namely metal elements (conductors) and non-metals (non-conductors / insulators)

In modern periodic systems, the metal element is generally located on the left while the non-metal is on the right.

The formation of electron configurations such as noble gases can be done by sharing electrons with covalent bonds or hand over electrons with ionic bonds

1. ionic bonding

Bonding that occurs due to electron handover. Can occur in metal and non-metal atoms. To get stability, atoms release or bind electrons to get stable electron regulation from noble gases

2. covalent bonding

Bonding that occurs due to shared use of electron pairs

The two bound atoms contribute their electrons to produce bonds

Generally occurs in non-metallic elements

Unstable atomic atoms will try to form stable electron configurations like those of noble gases. Where noble gases have the number of outer electrons (valence) 2 or 8

The small difference in electronegativity between two nonmetal elements results in a non-polar covalent bond. Whereas the greater electronegativity difference makes the compound ionic

If the electronegativity difference between the atoms is greater than 1.7, the compounds formed are ionic.

Generally, atoms with high ionisation energies (difficult to release electrons -> difficult to form positive ions) or atoms with low affinity (difficult to attract electrons -> difficult to form negative ions) will form electron pairs that are shared in the form of covalent bonds. The electron pair formed in a bond can originate from one atom or from 2 binding atoms

The Br atom is an atom located in group 7A and period 4, and belongs to a nonmetal atom.

The atomic number of Br is 35, so it takes 1 electron to be stable like the electron configuration of the Kr atom (noble gas) which has atomic number 36.

Br atoms have a large ionisation energy so that it will be difficult to release electrons, so that the bound Br atoms will use each other together with their electrons and form covalent bonds of Br₂

29. How do bromine atoms in halons react with the ozone layer?how do bromine atoms in holland's react with the ozone layer ​


Answer:

Halons are a class of chemicals that contain bromine atoms and are used as fire extinguishing agents. When halons are released into the atmosphere, the bromine atoms can react with ozone (O3) in the stratosphere, which is the layer of the Earth's atmosphere that contains most of the ozone.

The reaction between bromine atoms and ozone is similar to the reaction between chlorine atoms and ozone, which is the basis for the ozone hole. The reaction proceeds as follows:

Br + O3 → BrO + O2

In this reaction, a bromine atom (Br) reacts with ozone (O3) to form bromine monoxide (BrO) and oxygen (O2). The bromine monoxide can then react with another ozone molecule to form oxygen and bromine, which can then react with another ozone molecule to continue the cycle.

This reaction is significant because it reduces the amount of ozone in the stratosphere, which can lead to increased levels of harmful ultraviolet radiation reaching the Earth's surface. Halons are now largely banned because of their damaging effect on the ozone layer.
