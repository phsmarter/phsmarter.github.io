3. ExplainLet's discuss in detail the factors affecting potential energy and kinetic energy and how these factors affect kinetic energy and potential energy.​


Answer:

The usual situation you find yourself in is that you’re studying an object that is surrounded by a field. It could be a mass in a gravitational field or a charged particle in an electric field, etc. You then choose a coordinate system to use, and your object may have a velocity in that coordinate system. That will determine its kinetic energy. You have to choose a zero reference point for the field’s potential. Common choices include the Earth’s surface, for terrestrial problems, or infinity for orbital problems. Once you’ve chosen that reference point your object’s potential energy will be defined. Both potential and kinetic energy are proportional to the object’s mass.

That’s all “setup.” Once you’ve done that the object will generally experience a force that is calculated as the negative gradient of the potential field. It will accelerate in response to that force - changes in velocity will change the kinetic energy and changes in position will change the potential energy. And energy will be conserved.

13. What are the factors that affect kinetic energy?​


Answer:

mass and speed

Explanation:

The equation for kinetic energy is 1/2 mv^2 so the two factors that influence kinetic energy are the object’s mass and its speed (squared).

If your question is asking what factors can cause the kinetic energy to change, the answer is work. The work-energy theorem states that W=KEf−KEi .

Work W causes the initial kinetic energy KEi to change to a final value of kinetic energy KEf .

For a constant force, work is W=Fx , where x is the distance over which the force F acts. You're left with Fx=KEf−KEi so a force applied over a given distance can change the kinetic energy.
