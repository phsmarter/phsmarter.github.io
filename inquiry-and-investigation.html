Title: The Role of Inquiries, Investigation, and Immersion in Understanding the Educational Needs of Marginalized Students

Abstract:

This research paper explores the role of inquiries, investigation, and immersion in understanding the educational needs of marginalized students. The study focuses on the experiences of students from low-income families, immigrant families, and students with disabilities. The research methodology employed was qualitative, using interviews, observations, and document analysis to gather data. The study found that inquiries, investigation, and immersion are effective tools in understanding the educational needs of marginalized students. By engaging in these methods, researchers were able to gain a deeper understanding of the students' experiences, challenges, and strengths. The study also highlights the importance of cultural responsiveness in inquiry-based research and the need for ongoing collaboration between researchers and community stakeholders.

Introduction:

In recent years, there has been growing interest in the use of inquiries, investigation, and immersion in educational research. These methods involve engaging with the community, observing and documenting the experiences of individuals, and gathering data through interviews, surveys, and focus groups. The purpose of this research paper is to explore the role of inquiries, investigation, and immersion in understanding the educational needs of marginalized students. Specifically, the study focuses on students from low-income families, immigrant families, and students with disabilities. The research methodology employed was qualitative, using interviews, observations, and document analysis to gather data.

Literature Review:

The literature on inquiries, investigation, and immersion in educational research highlights the importance of community engagement and cultural responsiveness. These methods involve working closely with community stakeholders to gain a deeper understanding of the experiences, challenges, and strengths of marginalized students. Studies have shown that inquiries and investigation can lead to a more nuanced understanding of the educational needs of marginalized students and can inform the development of culturally responsive teaching practices (Banks, 2006; Gay, 2010). Immersion, on the other hand, involves spending time in the community and observing the experiences of individuals firsthand. This method has been shown to be particularly effective in understanding the experiences of immigrant and refugee students (Suarez-Orozco et al., 2010).

Methodology:

The study employed a qualitative research methodology, using inquiries, investigation, and immersion to gather data. The research was conducted in a school district with a high percentage of students from low-income families, immigrant families, and students with disabilities. The researchers conducted interviews with students, parents, teachers, and community stakeholders to gain a deeper understanding of the experiences of marginalized students. They also observed classrooms and school activities to document the experiences of students and teachers. In addition, the researchers analyzed documents such as school policies and curriculum materials to gain a broader understanding of the educational context.

Results:

The study found that inquiries, investigation, and immersion were effective tools in understanding the educational needs of marginalized students. By engaging in these methods, researchers were able to gain a deeper understanding of the students' experiences, challenges, and strengths. For example, through interviews with students, researchers were able to gain insights into the challenges faced by students from immigrant families, such as language barriers and cultural differences. Through observations of classrooms, researchers were able to identify areas where teachers could improve their cultural responsiveness.

Conclusion:

The study highlights the importance of inquiries, investigation, and immersion in understanding the educational needs of marginalized students. These methods are particularly effective in gaining a deeper understanding of the experiences, challenges, and strengths of students from low-income families, immigrant families, and students with disabilities. The study also underscores the importance of cultural responsiveness in inquiry-based research and the need for ongoing collaboration between researchers and community stakeholders. By engaging in these methods, researchers can develop more effective interventions and teaching practices that are responsive to the needs of marginalized students.

29. How important is egislative inquiry and investigation?​


I myself am critical of our legislators’ exercise of the congressional power of investigation which, in my view, does not adhere faithfully to the constitutional intent to limit its scope to being “in aid of legislation.” The Senate’s own Rules of Procedure provide that to be “in aid of legislation,” such inquiries may refer to the implementation or reexamination of any law or proposed legislation.

To put it succinctly, the only legitimate object of Congress in conducting an inquiry is to aid itself in the discharge of its legislative duties. But the sad reality is that, oftener than not, the inquiry is unrelated to a purpose within the jurisdiction of Congress. This is especially so where the aim of the inquiry is to look into possible violations of law or charges of wrongdoing against government officials or even private persons, with a view to determining liability or accountability—a matter that appears more within the province of the prosecution agencies in the executive branch and ultimately of the courts.
