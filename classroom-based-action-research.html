What is Action Research?

 

Definition

 

Action research is known by many other names, including participatory research, collaborative inquiry, emancipatory research, action learning, and contextural action research, but all are variations on a theme. Put simply, action research is “learning by doing” - a group of people identify a problem, do something to resolve it, see how successful their efforts were, and if not satisfied, try again.  While this is the essence of the approach, there are other key attributes of action research that differentiate it from common problem-solving activities that we all engage in every day.  A more succinct definition is,

 

"Action research...aims to contribute both to the practical concerns of people in an immediate problematic situation and to further the goals of social science simultaneously.  Thus, there is a dual commitment in action research to study a system and concurrently to collaborate with members of the system in changing it in what is together regarded as a desirable direction.  Accomplishing this twin goal requires the active collaboration of researcher and client, and thus it stresses the importance of co-learning as a primary aspect of the research process."

3. 1. What kind of problems and questions should you consider worth studying using action research?2. Can you think of a common problem that you observe happening in your classroom? Is this worth doing an action research for?​


Answer:

1. Action research is a research methodology that involves collaboration between researchers and participants to identify and solve practical problems. It is particularly useful for addressing problems or questions that affect a specific context, such as a community, organization, or classroom. Action research is suitable for studying problems that require a deep understanding of the context, such as organizational culture, interpersonal dynamics, or local norms and values. Some examples of problems and questions that are worth studying using action research include:

Improving student engagement and motivation in the classroomEnhancing the quality of teaching and learning practicesReducing student absenteeism and dropout ratesImproving communication and collaboration among team membersEnhancing the effectiveness of a specific program or interventionAddressing cultural or diversity issues within an organization or communityImproving the efficiency and productivity of a work process

2. A common problem that can be observed in a classroom is low student achievement or poor academic performance. This could be due to various factors such as ineffective teaching methods, low motivation, lack of resources, or insufficient support for struggling students. Addressing this problem using action research would involve identifying the root causes of the issue, developing and implementing interventions to address the problem, and evaluating the effectiveness of the interventions. This would require collaboration between the teacher, students, and other stakeholders to understand the context, develop appropriate interventions, and evaluate their impact. Therefore, it would be worth doing an action research project to improve student achievement in the classroom.

12. What does the term classroom research mean to you?​


Fundamentally, classroom research involves doing research in school settings about. teaching and learning. In this article I define classroom research as a process of investigating questions about teaching and learning that is undertaken in a systematic way by teachers who want to better understand their own work.

The goal of classroom action research is to improve teacher's own teaching in his own classroom, department, or school. While there is no requirement that the findings be generalized to other situations the results can add to knowledge base.
