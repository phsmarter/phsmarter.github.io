4. what is the composition of man according to aristotle?


Composition of Man

Aristotle posits the composition of man no longer consists of the two elements of body and soul, but rather three elements of:

Body Rational soulIrrational soul.

According to Aristotle a living creature is a ‘substance’.

Body = matter

Soul = form

Aristotle tries to explain his understanding of the distinction between the body and the soul using the analogy of an axe. If an axe were a living thing then its body would be made of wood and metal. However, its soul would be the thing which made it an axe i.e. its capacity to chop. If it lost its ability to chop it would cease to be an axe – it would simply be wood and metal.

For Aristotle, the body and soul are not two separate elements but are one thing. The body and the soul are not, as Plato would have it, two distinct entities, but are different parts or aspects of the same thing.

Aristotle does not allow for the possibility of the immortality of the soul. The soul is simply the Form of the body, and is not capable of existing without the body. The soul is that which makes a person a person rather than just a lump of meat! Without the body the soul cannot exist. The soul dies along with the body.

8. virtue according to Aristotle in passions virtue according to Aristotle in faculties ​


Answer:

According to Aristotle, there are two types of virtues: virtues of the passions and virtues of the faculties.

1. Virtues of the passions: These virtues are related to our emotional and appetitive aspects. Aristotle believed that virtues of the passion are developed through habituation and practice. Examples of virtues of the passions include courage, temperance, generosity, and humility. These virtues help us regulate our emotional responses to situations and develop self-control over our appetites.

2. Virtues of the faculties: These virtues are related to our intellectual and rational aspects. Aristotle believed that virtues of the faculties are developed through education and intellectual training. Examples of virtues of the faculties include wisdom, understanding, prudence, and justice. These virtues help us make good decisions and act morally in our daily lives.

In terms of passions, Aristotle believed that virtues of the passions involve finding the right balance between two extremes. For example, courage is the virtue that lies between the extremes of cowardice and recklessness. Temperance is the virtue that lies between the extremes of self-indulgence and self-denial.

In terms of faculties, Aristotle believed that virtues of the faculties involve developing practical wisdom and moral reasoning skills. Practical wisdom enables us to make good decisions in specific situations, while moral reasoning helps us understand what is right and wrong and act accordingly.

Overall, Aristotle believed that developing virtues is essential to living a fulfilling and flourishing life. By cultivating virtues of the passions and faculties, we can achieve a balance between our emotions and rationality, leading to a life of moral excellence and happiness.


9. what is terrestial motion according to aristotle​


Answer:

Aristotle believed that all motion on Earth could be classified as natural or violent. Natural motion occurred without external force and was inherent in objects, while violent motion was caused by an external force acting on an object. Terrestrial motion referred to the movement of objects on the Earth's surface or within the Earth's atmosphere. His understanding of motion was based on his observations of the natural world and his belief in natural causation.

Explanation:

Aristotle's ideas about terrestrial motion were based on his observation of the natural world and his belief in natural causation. He believed that objects moved in a certain way because of their inherent properties and that external forces were necessary to cause violent motion. His ideas were part of his broader philosophy, which aimed to understand the natural world through observation and reason. However, his ideas were later refined and improved upon by other philosophers and scientists who developed more precise and comprehensive theories of motion.

Aristotle’s Political Theory

First published Wed Jul 1, 1998; substantive revision Tue Nov 7, 2017

Aristotle (b. 384 – d. 322 BCE), was a Greek philosopher, logician, and scientist. Along with his teacher Plato, Aristotle is generally regarded as one of the most influential ancient thinkers in a number of philosophical fields, including political theory. Aristotle was born in Stagira in northern Greece, and his father was a court physician to the king of Macedon. As a young man he studied in Plato's Academy in Athens. After Plato's death he left Athens to conduct philosophical and biological research in Asia Minor and Lesbos, and he was then invited by King Philip II of Macedon to tutor his young son, Alexander the Great. Soon after Alexander succeeded his father, consolidated the conquest of the Greek city-states, and launched the invasion of the Persian Empire. Aristotle returned as a resident alien to Athens, and was a close friend of Antipater, the Macedonian viceroy. At this time (335–323 BCE) he wrote, or at least worked on, some of his major treatises, including the Politics. When Alexander died suddenly, Aristotle had to flee from Athens because of his Macedonian connections, and he died soon after. Aristotle's life seems to have influenced his political thought in various ways: his interest in biology seems to be expressed in the naturalism of his politics; his interest in comparative politics and his sympathies for democracy as well as monarchy may have been encouraged by his travels and experience of diverse political systems; he criticizes harshly, while borrowing extensively, from Plato's Republic, Statesman, and Laws; and his own Politics is intended to guide rulers and statesmen, reflecting the high political circles in which he moved.

19. according to Aristotle, what are four causes of conception? ​


Answer:

Aristotle's very ancient metaphysics often centered on the four causes of being. They are the material, formal, efficient, and final cause.

Explanation:

Aristotle's "four causes" Aristotle used the four causes to provide different answers to the question, "because of what?" The four answers to this question illuminate different aspects of how a thing comes into being or of how an event takes place.

The four causes or four explanations are, in Aristotelian thought, four fundamental types of answer to the question "why?", in analysis of change or movement in nature: the material, the formal, the efficient, and the final. Aristotle wrote that "we do not have knowledge of a thing until we have grasped its why, that is to say, its cause." While there are cases in which classifying a "cause" is difficult, or in which "causes" might merge, Aristotle held that his four "causes" provided an analytical scheme of general applicability.
