1. Who proposed the Philosophical position of positivism?


Positivism: Philosophical Theory

Auguste Comte (1798 - 1857)

He is the founder of positivism, a philosophical and political movement. Born in Montpellier on January 20, 1798. Positivism sank completely in oblivion during the twentieth century when it was eclipsed by neopositivism. The philosophical theory states that positive knowledge is based on natural phenomena, their relations and properties.

Positivism designates a philosophical tendency oriented around natural science and striving for united view of the world of phenomena through the applications of the methods and extensions of the result both physical and human.

18. The Christian philosopher who proposed that wisdom is the "father of all virtues."


Answer:

Saint Thomas Aquinas, a Catholic Priest and Christian Philosopher believed that wisdom is the father of all virtues. He is also known as the Doctor Angelicus and the Doctor Communis. According to Saint Thomas Aquinas, the gift of wisdom enables us to see that God is the “sovereign good, which is the last end…”  

Explanation:

Background of Saint Thomas Aquinas

Saint Thomas Aquinas believes that we can never achieve complete happiness in our earthly life. For him, final happiness is the union with God.

For Saint Thomas Aquinas, Theology is a science, the raw material data of which consists of written scripture and the tradition of the Catholic Church. These data were produced by the revelation of God to different individuals and groups of people throughout history.  

Faith and reason, used cohesively, are the two primary tools for processing various data of theology. Saint Thomas Aquinas, believed both were necessary—or, rather, that the confluence of both was necessary—for one to obtain true knowledge of God. Saint Thomas Aquinas, mixed Greek philosophy and Christian doctrine by suggesting that rational thinking and the study of nature, like revelation, were valid ways to understand truths pertaining to God.  

According to Saint Thomas Aquinas, God reveals himself through nature, so to study nature is to study God. The ultimate goals of theology are to use reason to grasp the truth about God and to experience salvation through that truth. The central thought is that grace does not destroy nature, but perfects it.

Views of Saint Thomas Aquinas On The Nature of Human Action

According to Saint Thomas Aquinas, human goodness depends on performing acts that are in accord with our human nature.

He explains that reason is comprised of two powers :

Cognitive- The cognitive power is the intellect, which enables us to know and understand. The intellect also enables us to apprehend the goodness a thing has. Appetitive - The appetitive power of reason is called the will. Aquinas describes the will as a native desire for the understood good.  

On Cardinal Virtues

Aquinas offers several definitions of virtue. According to one very general account, a virtue is a habit that “disposes an agent to perform its proper operation or movement” The Cardinal Virtues are:

Prudence Temperance Courage Justice

On Natural Law

According to Saint Thomas Aquinas, every law is ultimately derived from what the eternal law-- God’s providential ordering of all created things to their proper end.  

On Charity and Beatitude

In keeping with Christian teaching, he also acknowledges that we cannot achieve beatitude solely by means of our own virtuous efforts. Aquinas’ argument for this claim is as follows: the happiness to which we incline is of two sorts—incomplete happiness and complete happiness.

Incomplete happiness is a state we achieve by means of our natural human aptitudes. Through them, we can cultivate some measure of virtue and, in turn, be happier than we would be otherwise. Perfect or complete happiness, however, lies beyond on what we are able to achieve on our own.
