1. How to management kidnap ?


Answer:

1. Remain calm: Keeping yourself under control is the most important and the most difficult part of the situation. Panic or desperate decisions can put the abductee in more danger.

2. Follow Instructions: Listen carefully to the kidnapper and follow his/her instructions. Avoid arguments and don't try to negotiate.

3. Document the Incident: Try to remember as many details as possible about the incident, including specifics about the abductor, such as height, build, hair color, eye color, and clothing style.

4. Contact the Police Immediately: Notify the police as soon as you have the opportunity. Give an exact description of the kidnap situation and cooperate with the police on the matter.

5. Create a Kidnap Plan: Having a kidnap plan in place will help to reduce the risks and provide guidance in an emergency. Make sure that all family members are aware of the plan and have access to contact information.

Kidnapping is a pervasive presence across a range of insecure global settings: fragile states where crime, violence and impunity reign; diverse conflict-zones; unstable regions where global inequalities play out; and, increasingly, the precarious paths of migrant flows. Whilst this illicit phenomenon is often presented in sensationalist terms by the media, as a research topic kidnapping remains under-examined and under-theorised. Certainly, elite-kidnappings for ransom and abductions by militant groups are recognised as important concerns. However, the (often-uncritical) concentration upon these more orthodox manifestations of kidnapping, and state-led responses to it, masks other variants of this illicit practice, as well as innovations in counter-kidnapping from beyond the state.

About kidnapping

In criminal law, kidnapping is an illegal deviation and confinement of a person against his will. Thus, kidnapping is a joint crime. It can also be defined as false imprisonment by means of kidnapping, both of which are separate crimes which when committed simultaneously on the same person combine as the only crime of kidnapping. The element of kidnapping is usually but not necessarily done with force or fear. This means that the perpetrator can use the weapon to force the victim into the vehicle, but still kidnap if the victim is enticed into entering the vehicle voluntarily, for example, in the belief it is a taxi.

Kidnapping can be carried out to demand a ransom in exchange for releasing the victim, or for other illegal purposes. Kidnapping can be accompanied by bodily harm which increases the crime for aggravated kidnapping.
