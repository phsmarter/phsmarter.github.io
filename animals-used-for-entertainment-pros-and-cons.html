5. What are the pros and cons in using the Mohs scale of hardness?


Mohs’ Scale of Hardness:  Definition:  

     Mohs’ Scale of Hardness is a hardness testing scale that is named after its German developer Friedrich Mohs. It is used to describe how hard a mineral or gemstone is. It is based on whether a mineral can scratch another mineral, if it can it means it has a higher hardness.  

Pros:  Easy to do the test.  Can be done anytime and anywhere as long as the place is not dark or dim.  Mohs scale is highly relevant for field geologists to roughly identify minerals using scratch kits.  Can be done with few handy kits or no kits at all.  

Explanation:  

The test is too simple making it the easiest form of harness test. The individual conducting the test will only scratch the mineral with common objects including copper coin or wire, fingernail, glass, nail, or steel knife.

Aside from being the easiest it is also the most practical since it can be done anytime and anywhere as long as the place is not dark or dim to see the results of scratching easily.  

Mohs scale is highly relevant for field geologists since it provides the idea of  hardness which enables classification of minerals.  

The individual conducting the test will only scratch the mineral with common objects including copper coin or wire, fingernail, glass, nail, or steel knife and it will tell about the bonding structure, the harder the mineral the stronger the bonds leading to a mineral which will only fracture not cleave.  

Cons:  The scale is qualitative and not quantitative.  Cannot be used to test accurate hardness of industrial materials.  

Explanation:  

Since the scale is qualitative and not quantitative it is considered not the best test because the items which are necessary to determine the hardness may not always be available and the chance of carrying around a piece of microcline, topaz, and other minerals to determine the higher hardness is too small.  

The scale is neither accurate nor precise when measuring the hardness of certain industrial materials.

27. pros and cons of using WYSIWYG platforms.​


Answer:

Pros

They are easy to use, so even people who have no knowledge of HTML can use them to create their websites.

Gives you more creative control as you get to focus more on what the design looks like instead of what the HTML code looks like.

You will save time because a lot of things that take long time to hand code in HTML are quickly and easily done with a few clicks of a mouse.

Cons

Most WYSIWYG include HTML code that is hard to read that usually doesn’t comply with Web coding standards set forth by the World Wide Web Consortium, also known as the W3C.

Usually the codes are very specific to the product you are using to generate them, which may create issues with their viewing, or editing.

It will be harder to market yourself to employers who want you to know HTML, not a specific WYSIWIG editor. You won’t be able do well if you are used to one particular program and are forced to go to using organic HTML with notepad.

You will not be able to control the elements on the page as precisely as you can with straight HTML coding. While you will likely still be able to edit all of your alt tags, you will probably have to go through leaps and bounds to get to them.

You will have to use your mouse a lot, which could cause what is known as a Repetitive Stress Injury and cause pain in your wrists or shoulders.
