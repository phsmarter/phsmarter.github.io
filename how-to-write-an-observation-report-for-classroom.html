3. how to make an report for the classroom​


Answer:

. Be Precise

Carefully chosen and precise feedback can be really influential.

Statements like ‘she has done a good job’ are too vague. They’re not clear enough and don’t provide any concrete ways to improve.

Instead, be more specific, so parents and children know exactly what to do next.

Use Examples

Another way to improve school report writing is to give examples.

Why is this beneficial?

It helps the student understand what worked and what didn’t.

The student will be able to remember the example, so they can help explain it to their parents.

It means the report is individual to each learner.

For example, you could write: “Claire is beginning to understand that hard work leads to success. This can be evidenced by her essay on ‘Deforestation’ in which she has used the feedback from the last term to improve her grade this term.”

Share the Gradings and Provide a Model or Example

If you explain more about how children have been graded or scored, it’ll help learners and parents to better understand what they’ve achieved so far and what is needed to reach the next level.

A model or example can also help with this. It puts the grade into practice and can inspire children to improve their work.

Sandwich Model

One model to be aware of is the ‘sandwich model.’ The Open University has credited this for its high students satisfaction scores in assessment and feedback.

The basic model is to provide constructive criticism sandwiched in between praise, like a slice of cheese between bread!

First, praise the student for something they’ve done well. Next, give them an example of where they haven’t met the expected outcome. Finally, finish with more praise.

This gives you a good balance between praising students, which ensures they keep up their good work, and constructive feedback that helps them to understand any inaccuracies.

Refer to Guidelines

It’s worth checking school guidelines before you start writing your school reports.

For example, your school may want you to use the National Curricular statements for most of the report and devote only 10% for anecdotal evidence.

Feedforward

It’s important to remember that you need to include concrete steps students need to take.

This can be as simple as listing a step. For example, “learn a word a day to improve vocabulary.” Or you could point parents to a resource that might benefit the student.

Timely

School reports should be timely.

Feedback should be given as soon as possible and, in theory, you shouldn’t wait for a report to give feedback. Instead, the report should reinforce any feedback you’ve already given.

It’s also worth bearing in mind the pace of life for parents. I used to send two major reports per year, but found out that they’d only read it weeks after receiving it.

This often means there’s a delay in them carrying out the actions in the report.

Now, I send at least two other minor reports that alert the parents to what’s coming up and any actions they might need to take.

An example of a minor report could read something like this:

“Claire has been performing consistently in all her subjects. It would appear that she has acted on the feedback from my year-end report and made the necessary adjustments to her daily practices. I sincerely thank you for the support you have provided in helping Claire actualise her potential. By the first week of July, I will send a detailed year-end report in which I will articulate how well Claire has performed in her subjects in grade 8 in relation to national standards and with peers from her class. If you would like to discuss any aspects of Claire’s progress, please reach out to me.”

Personalise It

This isn’t easy when you’re writing dozens of reports. But a few tweaks can go a long way.

Here's a quick checklist:

Done

Get the child’s name and pronoun correct.

Include an anecdotal sentence about the child’s behaviour. For example, “Claire is always eager to help her peers and she demonstrated this yet again when she helped her peers who were struggling with their science project.”

If the child has mentioned they’re interested in something, mention that in the report.

Be honest. You don’t want to say that the child is good at reading when the parent knows for a fact that they aren’t. Not only is it inaccurate, it suggests you don’t know the child well.

Proofread

Make sure to proofread every report you write!

Attention to detail is very important. It shows parents that their child is receiving a quality education and reassures them that their children are in good hands.

15. Describe the classroom observation setting with the following categories: The children being ﻿ observed are unaware, the setting is natural and with participant observer​


Answer:

The classroom observation setting in this scenario is one where the children being observed are not aware of the observation taking place. This means that the observer is able to gain a natural and unobtrusive view of the classroom environment, as the children are behaving as they would normally without feeling the need to modify their behavior due to being watched. Additionally, the setting is natural, meaning that the classroom environment is not altered in any way for the purpose of observation. Finally, the observer in this scenario is acting as a participant observer, meaning they are actively involved in the classroom environment while also observing, further contributing to the natural setting.
