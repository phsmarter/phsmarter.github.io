9. characteristic of qualitative research


Characteristic of qualitative research

There are at least ten overlapping trends of qualitative observational analysis that researchers should be mindful of when gathering and interpreting evidence. Patton (1980) addresses these features of Qualitative Evaluation Methods to assist researchers in the design of studies.

Naturalistic Inquiry

Qualitative observational research is naturalistic in the sense that it studies a community in its natural environment. "Naturalistic investigation is thus compared to laboratory experiments where the author tries to fully monitor the state of the sample," Patton says. For example, if you were to study BEV speakers in college, you would not perform your research at a primarily Caucasian college or university.

Inductive analysis

Since it helps the researcher to become immersed in a community, this trait is common in qualitative study. The researcher begins with answers and gradually develops questions during the study process. Hypotheses and hypotheses will evolve in response to what the analyst needs to hear. For example, an outsider will notice that the intent of several BEV speech actions is to enhance the speaker's prestige. As a result, the observer's duty is to figure out why. This could lead to further study into BEV's rhetorical tactics and goals.

Holistic perspective

In other terms, nearly any activity or conversation must be seen as a component of the larger phenomena of a particular society or culture. However, this feature of qualitative observational analysis may be inconvenient since it can lead researchers to analyze any little action while composing a narrative. For example, a researcher may find that certain BEV speakers use a certain rhetorical technique in their writing. This phenomena, though, may have little to do with BEV and its rituals or tactics. It may be related to something else going on in their lives.

Summary

To summarize, the qualitative observational researcher must make every effort to preserve a non-judgmental bias during the analysis. The aim of the researcher is to observe and explain group dynamics, similarities, and differences as they emerge. Preconceived notions or stereotypes about an individual's or group's actions impede the researcher's ability to say the group's or culture's story fairly and accurately. Furthermore, preconceived notions prevent the researcher from noticing nuanced nuances in character and expression that could be essential in understanding group attitudes or experiences. Although total objectivity is unlikely, it is critical that participants approach the field or study community with an open mind, an understanding of their own biases, and a willingness to separate as far as possible from such biases while studying and reflecting the group.

11. qualitative research characteristics


Research methods can typically be categorized into two main branches: quantitative and qualitative. On the surface, these two categories have one main difference - quantitative research deals with *numerical data* while qualitative research deals with *non-numerical* data.

Let me give you an example of this distinction. Say you were studying students in your class. With quantitative research, you may collect data on how many students are in your class or what their ages are. But with qualitative research, you can collect data for questions like “what is your mood today and why?” or “how do you get to school every morning?”. Both these questions are legitimate research questions but they do not involve numbers, thus fall into qualitative research.

Other characteristics of qualitative research include:

- Multiple methods of collecting data (mixture of interviews, questionnaires, observation)

- Research tends to take place in the natural setting of the subject/s

- Research tends to answer “how” and “why” questions

- The researcher will often have a more personal relationship dynamic with the research subjects

- More often used by people in the natural sciences and journalism (but it can still be used in the natural sciences)

Characteristics of Qualitative Observational Research

Naturalistic Inquiry

Qualitative observational research is naturalistic because it studies a group in its natural setting. Patton explains, "Naturalistic inquiry is thus contrasted to experimental research where the investigator attempts to completely control the condition of the study

Inductive analysis

This characteristic is prevalent in qualitative research because it allows the observer to become immersed in a group. The researcher starts with answers, but forms questions throughout the research process.

Holistic perspective

It might be linked to something else in their lives.

Personal contact and insight

The researcher is responsible for becoming a part of a group to get a more in-depth study. However, the researcher also has to be aware of biases (both good and bad

Dynamic systems

Qualitative observational research is not concerned with having straightforward, right or wrong answers. In addition, change in a study is common because the researcher is not concerned with finding only one answer.

Unique case orientation

Researchers must remember that every study is special and deserves in-depth attention

Context sensitivity

Researchers must realize the different variables, such as values and beliefs, that influence cultural behaviors.

Empathic neutrality

Ideally, researchers should be non-judgmental when compiling findings. Because complete neutrality is impossible, this characteristic is a controversial aspect of qualitative research.

Design flexibility

Researchers can continue to do research on other topics or questions that emerge from the initial research.

Qualitative data

This is a detailed description of why a culture is the way it is. Triangulation, or the use of many data gathering methods, such as field notes, interviews, writing samples, and other data helps determine the cultural phenomenon of a group.

Summary

In sum, the qualitative observational researcher must attempt to maintain a non-judgmental bias throughout the study. The researcher's goal is to observe and describe group patterns, similarities, and differences as they occur. Preconceptions or expectations of an individual or group's behavior interferes with the researcher's ability to tell the group or culture's story in a fair and accurate manner. In addition, preconceived expectations preclude the researcher from observing subtle nuances of character and speech that may be important to understand group behaviors or interactions. While absolute objectivity is impossible, it is paramount that researchers enter the field or study group with an open a mind, an awareness of their own biases, and a commitment to detach from those biases as much as possible while observing and representing the group.
