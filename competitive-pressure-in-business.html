ADVANTAGES FOR BUSINESS COMPETITION

Although competition in business lessens your individual market share, it can also force you to become a better business. It can be easy to rest on your laurels when you’re the only option. But, they say, “competition breeds excellence.”

28. 1. Describe the context of the business competition model. 2. Enumerate and briefly describe each of the factors identified in Porter's business competition model. 3. Differentiate a competing product from a substitute product. 4. In what way can stakeholders' groups affect business competition? 5. Briefly discuss the term switching cost and describe how it affects business competition.​


The business competition model refers to the framework used to analyze and understand the competitive dynamics of an industry or market. It helps businesses identify the key factors that determine their position within a market and develop strategies to improve their competitiveness.

Porter's business competition model includes five factors that affect the competitiveness of a business:

Threat of new entrants: The degree to which new companies can easily enter a market and compete with existing businesses.

Bargaining power of suppliers: The ability of suppliers to dictate the terms of their relationship with a business, such as pricing, quality, and delivery.

Bargaining power of buyers: The degree to which customers can influence the terms of their relationship with a business, such as pricing, product features, and service quality.

Threat of substitutes: The extent to which alternative products or services can satisfy the same customer needs and compete with a business.

Intensity of competitive rivalry: The degree to which existing companies in a market compete with each other on price, product features, marketing, and other factors.

A competing product is a similar product offered by a direct competitor in the same market. On the other hand, a substitute product is a product that can replace the original product or service for the same customer needs but is offered by a different industry or market.

Stakeholder groups, such as customers, suppliers, employees, shareholders, and government regulators, can affect business competition in various ways. For example, customer preferences and demands can influence the product offerings and pricing strategies of businesses. Suppliers can affect business costs and the availability of key resources. Employees can affect productivity and innovation. Shareholders can influence the strategic direction and investment decisions of a business, and government regulators can affect the legal and regulatory environment of a market.

Switching cost refers to the cost that a customer incurs when switching from one product or service to another. It includes tangible costs such as financial expenses, time, and effort, as well as intangible costs such as loss of familiarity, trust, and loyalty. Switching cost affects business competition by creating a barrier to entry for new competitors and increasing customer loyalty for existing businesses. Businesses can use switching cost as a strategy to retain customers and deter competitors by offering incentives and loyalty programs.

Answer:

1. The business competition model is a framework used to analyze the competitive environment of a business. It involves assessing the factors that affect the level of competition in an industry or market, and how these factors impact the ability of companies to compete with one another.

2. Porter's business competition model identifies five key factors that affect business competition:

Threat of new entrants: the degree of difficulty for new companies to enter a market and compete with existing players

Bargaining power of suppliers: the extent to which suppliers can influence the pricing and quality of inputs

Bargaining power of buyers: the extent to which customers can influence the pricing and quality of outputs

Threat of substitutes: the extent to which alternative products or services can satisfy the same needs as the original product or service

Rivalry among existing competitors: the intensity of competition among existing players in the market.

3. A competing product is a product that is in direct competition with another product in the same market. A substitute product is a product that can be used as a replacement for another product to satisfy the same need or want.

The difference between the two is that competing products are similar products that are in direct competition, while substitute products are not necessarily similar but can be used in place of another product.

4. Stakeholders' groups can affect business competition by influencing the factors that impact competition. For example, labor unions can influence wages and working conditions, which can impact the cost structure of businesses and therefore their ability to compete. Government policies and regulations can also impact competition by limiting or enabling the entry of new players or setting standards for the industry.

5. Switching cost refers to the costs that customers incur when switching from one product or service to another. These costs can include financial costs (e.g. termination fees), time costs (e.g. relearning how to use a new product), and psychological costs (e.g. the fear of making a bad choice). Switching costs can affect business competition by creating a barrier to entry for new players, making it more difficult for them to attract customers away from existing players. It can also impact the bargaining power of buyers, as customers may be less likely to switch to a new product or service if they have already invested significant switching costs in their current provider.
