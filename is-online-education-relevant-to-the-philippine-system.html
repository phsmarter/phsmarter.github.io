Is Online Education Relevant to the Philippine System?

Philippines is not prepared for online learning. According to the Department of Science and Technology, the Philippines ranks 83rd out of 183 countries in terms of digital readiness. This indicates that the Philippines is not technologically sophisticated (DOST).

During the quarantine in most of the Philippines, education officials have advocated using online platforms for the 2020-2021 school year to continue the education of millions of students. Online education offers virtual classes that a student can take at home alone or with a group via phone call or video conference using software such as Messenger, Google Zoom, or Skype. Online education necessitates the use of suitable technology, such as a smartphone that supports video calls and a stable internet connection.

However, the Philippines cannot support online education for the majority of its pupils, therefore the attempt will fail for the majority of the country. Due to poverty in the Philippines, not every family has the means to acquire the necessary equipment for online learning. These families simply cannot adapt to the needs of online learning and will be left behind or forced to find alternative means of education for their children if they are not provided with an alternative. In addition, internet speed in the Philippines is still bad, as our country's more than 7,000 islands make it impossible to build fixed networks. In addition, the process of constructing cell towers is arduous and time-consuming.

However, Online Education allows both the teacher and the student to set their own learning rate, as well as a schedule that accommodates everyone's schedule. In addition, it can assist us in being highly organized, self-motivated, and time-management savvy. Consequently, employing an online educational platform allows for a better work-study balance.

In conclusion, the Philippines requires a clear set of regulations and norms based on an innovative educational system. This necessitates a thorough and serious evaluation of the nation's readiness to offer learning programs with rigorous criteria. This comprises instructor capability, the learner's position and context, and the effectiveness of the learning environment. These are in addition to the more obvious difficulties of internet speed, material cost, and delivery method. The greatest approach to advance is to take a step back and develop a strategy that involves instructors, students, parents, school officials, and technology-based corporations.
