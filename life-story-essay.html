24. Write an essay relating a real life situation to the story of romeo and juliet pointing out that the said story will not give the youth of today a better future. ​


Answer:

In the opening Prologue of Romeo and Juliet, the Chorus refers to the title characters as “star-crossed lovers,” an allusion to the belief that stars and planets have the power to control events on Earth. This line leads many readers to believe that Romeo and Juliet are inescapably destined to fall in love and equally destined to have that love destroyed. However, though Shakespeare’s play raises the possibility that some impersonal, supernatural force shapes Romeo and Juliet’s lives, by the end of the play it becomes clear that the characters bear more of the responsibility than Fortune does.

Though the Prologue offers the first and perhaps most famous example of celestial imagery in Romeo and Juliet, references to the stars, sun, moon, and heavens run throughout the play, and taken as a whole that imagery seems to express a different view of human responsibility. In Act 1, scene 4, Romeo says that he fears “some consequence yet hanging in the stars” when he and his gang approach the Capulet’s ball. In his next mention of stars, however, Romeo doesn’t refer to their astrological power. Rather, he uses the image of stars to describe Juliet’s otherworldly beauty. Most of the subsequent celestial images in the play follow in this vein, from Romeo’s love-struck comparison of Juliet to the sun to Juliet’s own wish to “cut [Romeo] out into little stars” when he dies. Throughout the play, these astral images are more often associated with the two lovers than with divine fate, emphasizing that, as the play’s action escalates, we cannot simply place the blame for the tragedy on some impersonal external force.

Answer:

The short story "The All-American Slurp" by Lensey Namioka portrays the life of a Chinese family who moves to America and experiences cultural differences while attending a dinner party with their American neighbors. The story highlights the clash between the traditional Chinese values and the American way of life. Through the various characters in the story, the author depicts the traditions of both the Chinese and American people.

The Chinese family, consisting of the parents and their two children, are portrayed as being very traditional in their beliefs and practices. They follow the customs and traditions of their Chinese heritage, such as wearing traditional clothing, using chopsticks to eat, and respecting their elders. The father, in particular, is shown to be very traditional, as he insists on wearing his Chinese tunic and trousers to the dinner party, despite his wife's protests. The mother also upholds traditional values, as she makes sure that her family is always respectful and polite, and she insists on bringing a gift to the host of the dinner party.

On the other hand, the American family is portrayed as being more casual and laid-back in their approach to life. They are shown to be more interested in having fun and enjoying themselves, rather than adhering to strict customs and traditions. The mother is depicted as being friendly and outgoing, and she is eager to welcome the Chinese family into her home. The father is portrayed as being more interested in American football than anything else, and he is somewhat oblivious to the cultural differences between his family and their Chinese guests.

The clash between these two cultures is shown through the various interactions between the characters. For example, when the Chinese family arrives at the dinner party, they are initially confused by the American-style buffet and the use of paper plates and plastic utensils. They are also uncomfortable with the loud music and the casual dress of the other guests. The Americans, on the other hand, are intrigued by the Chinese family's traditional clothing and customs, but they are somewhat taken aback by their formal manners and their use of chopsticks.

Despite these differences, however, the two families manage to find common ground and enjoy each other's company. They bond over their shared love of food, and they are able to laugh and joke together despite the language barrier. The Chinese family even manages to impress the Americans with their mastery of the "slurping" technique for eating soup, which they consider a sign of good manners.

In conclusion, "The All-American Slurp" depicts the clash between traditional Chinese values and American culture, as seen through the interactions between the two families in the story. The author uses various characters to highlight the different customs and traditions of the two cultures, and to show how they can sometimes clash but also find common ground. Ultimately, the story emphasizes the importance of respecting and learning from other cultures, even if they are very different from our own.
