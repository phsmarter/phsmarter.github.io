"The Story of an Hour" is a short story by Kate Chopin, first published in 1894. The story revolves around the character of Louise Mallard, who upon learning of her husband's supposed death, experiences a range of emotions, including grief, shock, and eventually a sense of liberation. The story has been analyzed from various literary perspectives, including formalist and feminist analyses.

Formalist analysis of "The Story of an Hour" focuses on the elements of the story itself, such as plot, character, and setting, without taking into consideration the author's intentions or historical context. Formalist critics may analyze the way the story is structured, the use of symbols and imagery, and the character development of Louise Mallard. For instance, they may discuss the way the story is structured with a clear beginning, middle, and end, and the way Chopin uses irony to create tension and suspense. They may also analyze the symbolism of the open window, which represents Louise's longing for freedom and the possibility of a new life.

Feminist analysis of "The Story of an Hour" focuses on the way the story deals with gender issues, such as the role of women in society, the effects of marriage on women's lives, and the restrictions placed on women's freedom. Feminist critics may examine the way Chopin portrays Louise Mallard as a woman who is trapped by the expectations of marriage and the social norms of her time. They may also analyze the way the story deals with female sexuality, such as the way Louise feels liberated by the thought of living without her husband and the implications of this desire for female independence.

When comparing the formalist and feminist analyses of "The Story of an Hour," it is clear that both approaches offer valuable insights into the story. While the formalist analysis focuses on the structure and elements of the story, the feminist analysis provides a broader context by examining the gender issues that the story raises. Both approaches offer different ways of understanding the story and help to deepen our appreciation of its themes and symbolism. For instance, the formalist analysis highlights the irony and symbolism in the story, while the feminist analysis provides a critique of the social norms that constrain women's freedom. Ultimately, both approaches complement each other and offer a more comprehensive understanding of the story

7. Analysis of "The Story of an Hour" Through the Lens of Feminist Analysis of "The Story of an Hour" Through the Lens of Historical Analysis of "The Story of an Hour" Through the Lens of Reader-Response​


Answer:

Through the Lens of Feminist Analysis:

Kate Chopin's "The Story of an Hour" can be analyzed through the lens of feminist theory, which seeks to understand the ways in which gender and power operate in society. The protagonist, Mrs. Mallard, is portrayed as a woman who is trapped in a marriage that limits her freedom and agency. When she learns of her husband's death, she experiences a sense of liberation and empowerment, which is ultimately taken away when her husband turns out to be alive.

Through a feminist lens, the story can be interpreted as a critique of the patriarchal society in which Mrs. Mallard lives. Her husband's death is not only a literal event but also a metaphorical one, symbolizing the death of the patriarchal system that oppresses women. Mrs. Mallard's brief moment of freedom represents a glimpse of what life could be like for women if they were not constrained by the societal expectations placed upon them.

Through the Lens of Historical Analysis:

"The Story of an Hour" can also be analyzed through a historical lens. The story was published in 1894, at a time when women's rights were a contested issue in American society. The suffrage movement was gaining momentum, and women were beginning to demand greater social and political equality.

The story reflects the tensions and contradictions of this historical moment. Mrs. Mallard's desire for freedom and agency is in tension with the social norms of the time, which dictated that women should be submissive and obedient to their husbands. At the same time, the story can be seen as a reflection of the growing feminist consciousness of the time, which challenged these norms and advocated for greater rights and freedoms for women.

Through the Lens of Reader-Response:

Finally, "The Story of an Hour" can be analyzed through a reader-response lens, which emphasizes the role of the reader in interpreting and making meaning from a text. Each reader brings their own experiences, beliefs, and values to the text, which shape their understanding and response to it.

For example, a reader who identifies as a feminist may focus on Mrs. Mallard's struggle for agency and empowerment, while a reader who is more conservative may focus on the story's depiction of the breakdown of traditional gender roles. Similarly, a reader who has experienced the loss of a loved one may identify with Mrs. Mallard's emotional turmoil, while a reader who has not may view her reactions as unrealistic or exaggerated.

Ultimately, the meaning of "The Story of an Hour" depends on the individual reader's interpretation and response. The story's ambiguity and complexity invite a variety of readings, each of which reflects the reader's own perspective and context.

17. Explain why the story Frozen 1 is an example of a Feminist Literature. Focus on the characteristics of Feminism in doing this analysis. yung maayos pong sagot​


Answer:

Frozen is now the highest grossing animated film ever and the fifth highest grossing film regardless of genre. In May 2014, the Disney store announced that it would hold drawings for people wanting to purchase Frozen costumes.  In addition to its mass popularity, Frozen has also garnered critical acclaim for its gender representation and is often hailed as the most progressive and feminist Disney movie yet.Most of the arguments about Frozen’s progressive gender representation revolve around a few key themes and occurrences in the movie. These are: 1) Disney rejects the “happily ever after” heterosexual romance trope in this movie, 2) Elsa is a powerful idol of women’s empowerment with a message of independence, 3) Anna is another strong woman role model who is independent, adventurous, and brave. Is Frozen really as progressive as these arguments claim?

According to the first argument Disney is mocking its earlier versions of princess stories by portraying the idea of falling in love at first sight as foolish especially since Hans turns out to be a scheming prince. But is the heterosexual romance trope missing? Certainly not. Most of the movie revolves around Anna and Kristoff’s relationship, and we do see it culminate in a kiss. Further, it seems that Anna and Kristoff haven’t known each other for more than two days! Thus, Anna and Kristoff’s relationship certainly falls within Disney’s previous versions of romance.

The next argument is framed around Elsa, who is seen as a powerful and independent woman who learns to love her power instead of concealing it. Yet, her storyline undermines that message. For instance, we see that once Elsa goes into exile, she unleashes her power, which is symbolized by the fantastic ice palace she builds for herself. However, we see shortly after, that her power and independence start to turn her evil. This is evident when she nearly murders two men—by almost impaling one, and trying to push another off the mountain, and when she sends a snow monster after Anna. It is only when she returns to her village and uses her powers for people’s entertainment (by building an ice rink), that she is in fact accepted by people. This is a version of femininity that is soft, safe and selfless; it is about pleasing and nurturing people, and not about building monuments that celebrate one’s power.

The final set of arguments for the progressiveness of Frozen center on Anna. Anna is adventurous and brave. However, Anna is never supposed to be taken seriously by us.  She seems adventurous because she doesn’t seem to know any better, not because she is a capable young woman. The comic relief most often comes from her being child-like, and not physically capable.

Explanation:

Feminism is said to be the movement to end women's oppression (hooks 2000, 26). One possible way to understand ‘woman’ in this claim is to take it as a sex term: ‘woman’ picks out human females and being a human female depends on various biological and anatomical features (like genitalia). Historically many feminists have understood ‘woman’ differently: not as a sex term, but as a gender term that depends on social and cultural factors (like social position). In so doing, they distinguished sex (being female or male) from gender (being a woman or a man), although most ordinary language users appear to treat the two interchangeably. More recently this distinction has come under sustained attack and many view it nowadays with (at least some) suspicion. This entry outlines and discusses distinctly feminist debates on sex and gender.
