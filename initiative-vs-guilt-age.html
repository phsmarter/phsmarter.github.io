19. During this stage, the infant is uncertain about the world in which they live, and looks towards theirprimary caregiver for stability and consistency of care.a. Trust vs. Mistrustc. Autonomy vs. Shame and Doubtb. Initiative vs. Guiltd. Industry vs. Inferiority​


Answer :

A. Trust vs. Mistrust

Explanation :

Trust vs. mistrust is the first stage in Erik Erikson’s theory of psychosocial development. This stage begins at birth continues to approximately 18 months of age. During this stage, the infant is uncertain about the world in which they live, and looks towards their primary caregiver for stability and consistency of care.

If the care the infant receives is consistent, predictable and reliable, they will develop a sense of trust which will carry with them to other relationships, and they will be able to feel secure even when threatened.

If these needs are not consistently met, mistrust, suspicion, and anxiety may develop.

If the care has been inconsistent, unpredictable and unreliable, then the infant may develop a sense of mistrust, suspicion, and anxiety. In this situation the infant will not have confidence in the world around them or in their abilities to influence events.

Success in this stage will lead to the virtue of hope. By developing a sense of trust, the infant can have hope that as new crises arise, there is a real possibility that other people will be there as a source of support. Failing to acquire the virtue of hope will lead to the development of fear.

This infant will carry the basic sense of mistrust with them to other relationships. It may result in anxiety, heightened insecurities, and an over feeling of mistrust in the world around them.

Consistent with Erikson’s views on the importance of trust, research by Bowlby and Ainsworth has outlined how the quality of the early experience of attachment can affect relationships with others in later life.

Answer:

Guilt is a feeling of remorse or responsibility that arises when one believes they have violated a moral or ethical code. Guilt can be a normal and healthy response when it is an appropriate response to a specific situation or behavior. However, excessive or inappropriate guilt can be detrimental to one's mental and emotional wellbeing.

Answer:

Guilt and grower practices are two different approaches to learning and personal development.

Guilt practices are based on the idea that we need to constantly criticize and judge ourselves in order to improve. People who use guilt practices tend to focus on their mistakes and failures, and they may engage in negative self-talk, self-blame, and self-punishment as a way to motivate themselves. For example, a student who uses guilt practices may beat themselves up for getting a low grade on a test, and use that guilt to motivate themselves to study harder next time.

Grower practices, on the other hand, are based on the idea that we can learn and grow through experience and reflection. People who use grower practices tend to focus on their strengths and opportunities for growth, and they may engage in positive self-talk, self-reflection, and self-compassion as a way to motivate themselves. For example, a student who uses grower practices may recognize that they struggled on a test because they didn't study effectively, and use that reflection to develop better study habits in the future.

Overall, guilt practices can be detrimental to our well-being and personal growth because they can lead to feelings of shame, low self-esteem, and burnout. In contrast, grower practices can help us develop resilience, self-awareness, and a growth mindset, which can lead to greater satisfaction and success in our personal and professional lives.
