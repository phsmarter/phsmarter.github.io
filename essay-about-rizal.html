12. Essay about Rizal and his works' significance to our time​ minimum 10 sentences


Answer: Jose Rizal is one of the most influential figures in Philippine history, and his works continue to have significant relevance to our time. He was a renowned nationalist, writer, and revolutionary who fought for the country's freedom against the Spanish colonial rulers. His literary works, including novels, essays, and poems, are regarded as masterpieces that inspired the Philippine Revolution.

One of Rizal's most significant contributions to Philippine literature is his novel, Noli Me Tangere, which exposed the corruption and injustices of the Spanish colonial government. The book, which was published in 1887, sparked a revolution and ignited a sense of nationalism among the Filipinos. Its themes of oppression, injustice, and resistance continue to resonate with Filipinos today, who are still fighting against corruption and social inequality.

Rizal's other novel, El Filibusterismo, which was published in 1891, also had a profound impact on Philippine literature and society. The book delved deeper into the issues of colonialism and class struggle, exploring the various forms of oppression and resistance present in the Philippines at the time. It remains a powerful critique of the Philippine society's flaws and continues to be a source of inspiration for contemporary writers and social activists.

Apart from his literary works, Rizal was also a staunch advocate for education and social reform. He believed that the country's progress would depend on the education of its people and that the youth must be empowered to effect social change. His ideas on education and social reform remain relevant today as Filipinos continue to fight for access to quality education and equal opportunities for all.

In addition to his literary and social contributions, Rizal was also a revolutionary leader who fought for the country's independence. He believed that the Philippines could only achieve freedom through peaceful means, and his nonviolent approach to revolution has inspired many modern-day social activists who advocate for peaceful protests and nonviolent resistance.

Overall, Rizal's significance to our time lies in his unwavering commitment to social justice, freedom, and equality. His literary works, ideas on education, and nonviolent approach to revolution continue to inspire generations of Filipinos to fight for a better future. His life and legacy serve as a reminder of the importance of courage, perseverance, and dedication to one's ideals.

In this essay, we will examine why Rizal wants the Philippines to be free from the Spaniards.

The Spanish government implemented policies that favored Spanish nationals, resulting in social inequality and economic exploitation of the Filipino people. Rizal witnessed firsthand the poverty and suffering of his fellow Filipinos, and he wanted to put an end to it. He believed that the Philippines deserved to be an independent nation, where Filipinos could enjoy equal rights and opportunities, without the burden of colonialism.

Secondly, Rizal believed that the Philippines should be free from the Spaniards because of the suppression of Filipino culture and heritage. The Spanish colonizers tried to erase Filipino identity by imposing their own culture and language on the Filipinos. They forbade the teaching of Filipino languages and suppressed the practice of Filipino traditions and customs. Rizal believed that the Filipino people should be proud of their heritage and culture, and he fought for their right to practice their traditions and speak their languages without fear of persecution.

Lastly, Rizal wanted the Philippines to be free from the Spaniards because he believed in the inherent dignity and worth of the Filipino people. He believed that Filipinos had the right to self-determination and self-governance, and that they were capable of managing their own affairs. He saw the Philippines as a nation with great potential, with the ability to thrive and prosper without the oppressive hand of the Spanish colonizers.

In conclusion, Rizal's desire for the Philippines to be free from the Spaniards was fueled by his passion for justice, equality, and freedom. He wanted Filipinos to have the right to govern themselves, practice their own culture, and enjoy equal rights and opportunities. In 1890, Rizal, 29, left Paris for Brussels as he was preparing for the publication of his annotations of Antonio de Morga's "Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas." There, he lived in the boarding house of the two Jacoby sisters, Catherina and Suzanna who had a niece also named Suzanna ("Thil") HistorianGregorio F. Zaide states that Rizal had "his romance with Suzanne Jacoby, 45, the petite niece of his landladies." Belgian Pros Slachmuylders, however, believed that Rizal had a romance with the niece, Suzanna Thil, in 1890. Belgian Jean Paul Verstraeten proved this. Jean Paul traced in the archives of Brussels that in the same house as Suzanne Jacoby, a little niece was living, Suzanne Till. Letters from Suzanne to Rizal were signed by "Petite Suzanne".

Rizal's Brussels stay was short-lived, as he moved to Madrid, leaving the young Suzanna a box of chocolates. Suzanna replied in French: "After your departure, I did not take the chocolate. The box is still intact as on the day of your parting. Don’t delay too long writing us because I wear out the soles of my for running to the mailbox to see if there is a letter from you. There will never be any home in which you are so loved as in that in Brussels, so, you little bad boy, hurry up and come back…" (Oct. 1, 1890 letter). The house where Rizal was staying, in Rue Philippe de Champagne 38 doesn't exist anymore following some writers. (the house numbers jump from 36 to 42). Jean Paul Verstraeten proved during his research that this house still exists, on August 10, 1900, the number of the house was changed into 42. Jean Paul traced the owner of this house and convinced her to put a historical marker. Slachmuylders’ group in 2007 unveiled this historical marker commemorating Rizal's stay in Brussels in 1890.
