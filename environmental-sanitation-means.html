Answer:

Poor environmental sanitation refers to the inadequate or improper management of waste, water, and other environmental resources that lead to the contamination of the environment and pose health risks to humans, animals, and plants.

There are various factors that can cause poor environmental sanitation, including a lack of proper waste disposal facilities, inadequate water supply and sanitation systems, and poor urban planning and management.

Examples of poor environmental sanitation include:

Open defecation: This is the practice of defecating outside without using a toilet, which can lead to the contamination of water sources and the spread of diseases such as cholera and typhoid fever.

Improper waste disposal: This refers to the dumping of waste in unauthorized areas, such as rivers, streets, and open spaces. This can cause environmental pollution, create breeding grounds for disease vectors, and cause respiratory problems.

Poor drainage systems: This refers to the inadequate or blocked drainage systems that can cause flooding and waterlogging during the rainy season, leading to the breeding of mosquitoes and other disease vectors.

Contaminated water sources: This refers to the use of contaminated water for drinking, cooking, and other domestic purposes. This can cause waterborne diseases such as diarrhea, cholera, and typhoid fever.

To prevent poor environmental sanitation, it is essential to adopt good environmental practices such as proper waste management, regular cleaning and maintenance of drainage systems, and the provision of safe water and sanitation facilities. This can be achieved through effective urban planning and management, community participation, and public education on the importance of environmental sanitation.
