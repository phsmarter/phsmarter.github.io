10. Explain the quote: it takes a great Great deal of history to produce a little literature. Literature is a luxury, fiction is necessity Please I need help


Answer:

The quote "it takes a great deal of history to produce a little literature" refers to the idea that literature is a product of its historical context and cannot be divorced from the events, culture, and ideas of the time in which it was written. This means that a writer's experiences and the events that have shaped their lives and their world often find their way into their works and influence the themes and content of the literature they produce.

The second part of the quote, "Literature is a luxury, fiction is necessity," suggests that while literature may be seen as a luxury or an indulgence, fiction is an essential part of our lives. Fiction serves a vital role in helping us to understand and process our experiences and emotions, as well as helping us to see the world from different perspectives. This part of the quote implies that while literature may be seen as a form of entertainment, fiction is a necessity for our personal and emotional growth.

18. explain Mozart's quote,"Too much for what I do,too little for what I could do".​


Answer:

At the time he supposedly said it, Mozart had been given a part-time job as “chamber composer” by Emperor Joseph II in December 1787, a month after the previous chamber composer, Christoph Willibald Gluck, died. Basically, this is Mozart complaining that he was unappreciated and getting ripped off - paid less than Gluck for the same work, and knowing that he could produce much more if he were given the chance.

The chamber composer’s job - part-time only - was to write music for the annual Emperor’s ball, and other infrequent, but formal occasions. He was paid only 800 florins per year. The Emperor was aware that Mozart was looking for opportunities outside of Vienna (he had just returned from Prague two months earlier, after the premiere of Don Giovanni), and this appointment was intended to convince Mozart to stay in Vienna. Mozart was popular in cities other than Vienna: Prague, Munich, and Mannheim especially, and he probably could have named his own price in any of those. But he wanted to make his fame in Vienna.

However, 800 florins was not nearly enough to live on and support a wife and children, and it was substantially less than what Gluck had been paid for the same job.

As a bribe to keep Mozart in Vienna, it worked, but it really wasn’t fair, and for Mozart, it led to disastrous results. Austria was at war with Turkey, the economy turned bad, and the aristocracy was not in a mood to spend money the way they had in the prior few years. After Don Giovanni flopped in Vienna, Mozart went nearly three years without a commission for a new opera. When Emperor Joseph II died during these years, his brother Leopold took the throne; Leopold was not a music-lover, and Mozart’s prospects sank still lower. In effect, even though Emperor Joseph II liked Mozart and Mozart’s music, he still screwed him over when it came to supporting him financially.

20. Instruction: Expound the quote below. Beware of little expenses. A small leak will sink a great ship. - Benjamin Franklin Activity: In at least five (5) sentences, expound the quote above.


Answer:

Benjamin Franklin's quote "Beware of little expenses. A small leak will sink a great ship" is a warning that small expenses, if left unchecked, can have a significant impact on one's financial stability. Just like a small leak in a ship can lead to its sinking, small and seemingly insignificant expenses can add up over time and lead to financial ruin.

The quote emphasizes the importance of being mindful of one's spending habits and keeping track of even the smallest expenses. It also highlights the need for individuals to prioritize their spending and focus on saving for important long-term goals.

To avoid falling into financial trouble, it is essential to keep track of all expenses, no matter how small they may seem. This requires creating a budget and sticking to it, as well as regularly reviewing one's spending habits to identify areas where cuts can be made.

In conclusion, Benjamin Franklin's quote serves as a reminder that financial stability requires vigilance and attention to detail when it comes to managing expenses, no matter how small they may seem.
