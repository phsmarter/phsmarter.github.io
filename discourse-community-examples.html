5. Define text with discourse and give 5 example​


Answer:

1.Definition of Discourse

Discourse is any written or spoken communication. Discourse can also be described as the expression of thought through language. While discourse can refer to the smallest act of communication, the analysis can be quite complex. Several scholars in many different disciplines have theorized about the different types and functions of discourse.

2.Types of Discourse

While every act of communication can count as an example of discourse, some scholars have broken discourse down into four primary types: argument, narration, description, and exposition. Many acts of communicate include more than one of these types in quick succession.

3.Common Examples of Discourse

Let us look at some examples of the different types of discourse from everyday life:

When you buy a box of Ritz crackers, on the back of the box, they have all these suggestions as to what to put on top of the Ritz. “Try it with turkey and cheese. Try it with peanut butter.” But I like crackers man, that’s why I bought it, ’cause I like crackers! I don’t see a suggestion to put a Ritz on top of a Ritz. I didn’t buy them because they’re little edible plates! You’ve got no faith in the product itself.

4.Significance of Discourse in Literature Discourse of any type is one of the most important elements of human behavior and formation. Countless studies have been done on the way the brain shapes thoughts into words and, indeed, the way that communication shapes the brain. Many studies have specifically targeted the way that speakers of different languages understand concepts differently. Thus, the creation and dispersion of discourse is of the utmost importance to the perpetuation of the human race. Literature is one of the primary ways of maintaining a record of discourse and creating new ways of understanding the world.

14. What is the example of discourse analysis research ?


Answer:

Discourse analysis research helps people understand how people use language in the world - whether that is in writing or through speaking. In general, people who conduct discourse analysis want to find out what a person's language says about their beliefs, their history, their social status, what they are trying to convey to their audience, and other things.

In practice, this involves studying a person's

VocabularyGrammarToneBody language and gestures (for spoken language)

to find out what a person is saying behind the words they're speaking or writing down on paper.

There are many ways to conduct a discourse analysis research, and here are two examples:

Analyzing the personal letters and correspondences of a historical figure can reveal a lot about the nature of the metaphors and allegories they used, their personal beliefs, as well as the possible change of tone in a personal letter instead of a professional letter.Studying casual conversations between two people of a distinct society helps researchers understand the terms that are commonly used in casual conversations, what kind of metaphors people in that society use, as well as the tone that is appropriate for such conversations.
