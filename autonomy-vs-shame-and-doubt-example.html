Answer:

Autonomy versus shame and doubt is the second stage of Erik Erikson’s stages of psychosocial development. This stage occurs between the ages of 18 months to around age 2 or 3 years. According to Erikson, children at this stage are focused on developing a greater sense of self-control.

Autonomy vs. Shame and Doubt Builds on the Previous Stage

Erikson's theory of psychosocial development describes a series of eight stages that take place throughout the course of life. The first stage of development, trust versus mistrust, is all about developing a sense of trust about the world. The next stage, autonomy versus shame and doubt, builds upon that earlier stage and lays the foundation for the future stages to come.

What Happens During Stage 2

If you are a parent or if you have ever interacted with a child between the ages of 18 months and 3 years, then you have probably witnessed many of the hallmarks of the autonomy versus shame and doubt stage. It is at this point in development that young children begin to express a greater need for independence and control over themselves and the world around them.

During the previous stage of development, trust versus mistrust, children are almost entirely dependent upon others for their care and safety. It is during this stage that children build the foundations of trust in the world. As they progress into the second stage, however, it is important for young children to begin developing a sense of personal independence and control.

5. During this stage, the infant is uncertain about the world in which they live, and looks towards theirprimary caregiver for stability and consistency of care.a. Trust vs. Mistrustc. Autonomy vs. Shame and Doubtb. Initiative vs. Guiltd. Industry vs. Inferiority​


Answer :

A. Trust vs. Mistrust

Explanation :

Trust vs. mistrust is the first stage in Erik Erikson’s theory of psychosocial development. This stage begins at birth continues to approximately 18 months of age. During this stage, the infant is uncertain about the world in which they live, and looks towards their primary caregiver for stability and consistency of care.

If the care the infant receives is consistent, predictable and reliable, they will develop a sense of trust which will carry with them to other relationships, and they will be able to feel secure even when threatened.

If these needs are not consistently met, mistrust, suspicion, and anxiety may develop.

If the care has been inconsistent, unpredictable and unreliable, then the infant may develop a sense of mistrust, suspicion, and anxiety. In this situation the infant will not have confidence in the world around them or in their abilities to influence events.

Success in this stage will lead to the virtue of hope. By developing a sense of trust, the infant can have hope that as new crises arise, there is a real possibility that other people will be there as a source of support. Failing to acquire the virtue of hope will lead to the development of fear.

This infant will carry the basic sense of mistrust with them to other relationships. It may result in anxiety, heightened insecurities, and an over feeling of mistrust in the world around them.

Consistent with Erikson’s views on the importance of trust, research by Bowlby and Ainsworth has outlined how the quality of the early experience of attachment can affect relationships with others in later life.

23. true to life example of excessive autonomy versus excessive interdependence​


Excessive autonomy and excessive interdependence are two extremes in the spectrum of independence and interdependence. Here's a true-to-life example that illustrates these concepts:

Let's consider a married couple, John and Sarah. John values his independence and often makes decisions without consulting Sarah, even if those decisions affect both of them. For example, John decided to buy a new car without discussing it with Sarah or considering her opinion. He also tends to prioritize his own needs and desires over Sarah's, and rarely takes her feelings into account.

On the other hand, Sarah values interdependence and tends to rely heavily on John for decision-making and emotional support. She often defers to John's opinions and desires, even when they conflict with her own. For example, Sarah wanted to attend a writing class but decided against it because John was not interested in it and she did not want to do anything that would upset him.

In this scenario, John exhibits excessive autonomy, while Sarah exhibits excessive interdependence. Both extremes can have negative consequences for their relationship, as John's unilateral decisions and disregard for Sarah's feelings can lead to resentment and conflict, while Sarah's lack of assertiveness and reliance on John for decision-making can lead to feelings of powerlessness and a lack of agency in her own life.

28. 5 examples of Dependence versus Autonomy and 5 examples of Order versus Disorder


Explanation:

5 examples of Dependence versus Autonomy:

A child depending on their parents for financial support versus a financially independent adult.A person relying on their partner for emotional support versus a person who is emotionally self-sufficient.An employee needing constant direction and guidance from their boss versus an employee who is given autonomy to complete tasks on their own.A student needing constant feedback and guidance from their teacher versus a student who can learn independently.A person who constantly seeks validation and approval from others versus a person who is self-assured and confident in their own abilities.

5 examples of Order versus Disorder:

A tidy and organized desk versus a messy and cluttered desk.A well-planned and structured schedule versus a chaotic and disorganized schedule.A city with well-maintained roads and infrastructure versus a city with dilapidated and poorly maintained infrastructure.A clean and tidy room versus a dirty and cluttered room.A system with clear rules and guidelines versus a system with ambiguous or non-existent rules.

Certainly, the following are 5 models for each:

Autonomy versus dependence:

1. In contrast to an adult who lives independently and makes their own decisions, a dependent child relies on their parents for basic needs like food and shelter (autonomy).

2. versus a worker who is trusted to make their own decisions and is given autonomy to complete tasks (autonomy), a worker who is micromanaged by their boss and needs approval for every decision

3. A patient in an emergency clinic requiring steady consideration and consideration from medical care experts (reliance) versus an individual who is sound and needs no clinical support (independence).

4. A nation that is self-sufficient and does not rely on outside assistance (autonomy) versus a nation that heavily depends on foreign aid to support its citizens (dependence).

5. A person who has an addiction to alcohol or drugs and needs help to get out of it (dependence) versus a person who has overcome their addiction and has control over their life (autonomy).

In contrast to disorder:

1. In contrast to a cluttered and disorganized workspace with things scattered about, a tidy and organized workspace has everything in its place (order).

2. versus a chaotic and disruptive classroom with no structure or consistency (disorder), a well-run and structured classroom with clear rules and routines (order).

3. An event that was well-planned and carried out, with everything going as planned (order), versus an event that was poorly planned and carried out, with everything going wrong and out of control (disorder).

4. In contrast to a lawless society lacking structure or order, order refers to a society where citizens adhere to clear laws and regulations.

5. A person who adheres to their day's plan and timeline (order) in contrast to a person who is disorganized and does not have a plan or structure for their day (disorder).
