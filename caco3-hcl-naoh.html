Further explanation  

Titration is a procedure for determining the concentration of a solution (analyte) by reacting with another solution whose known concentration (usually a standard solution) is called the titrant. Determination of the endpoint / equivalence point of the reaction can use indicators according to the appropriate pH range  

Titrations can be: acid-base titration, depositional titration, and redox titration. An acid-base titration is the principle of neutralization of acids and bases  

Acid-base titration formula  

[tex]\rm \large{\boxed{\bold{Va\times Na=Vb\times Nb}}}[/tex]

Na, Nb = acid base concentration  

Va, Vb = acid base volume

V HCl = 25 ml

V Ba(OH)₂ = 0.1 N

So

V HCl . N HCl = V Ba(OH)₂ . N Ba(OH)₂

[tex]\rm 25\times N_{HCl}=30.08\times 0.1\\\\N_{HCl}=\dfrac{30.08\times 0.1}{25}\\\\N_{HCl}=\boxed{\bold{0.12032}}[/tex]

Step 2

NaOH is titrated by HCl

V HCl = 25.10 ml

N HCl = 0.12032

V NaOH = 10 ml

So

V NaOH . N NaOH = V HCl . N HCl

[tex]\rm 10\times N_{NaOH}=25.10\times 0.12032\\\\N_{NaOH}=\dfrac{25.1\times 0.12032}{10}\\\\N_{NaOH}=\boxed{\bold{0.302}}[/tex]

8. How many millimeters of 0.55 M HCl are needed to react with 3.7 g CaCO3? 2HCl (aq) + CaCO3 (s) → CaCl2 (aq) + CO2 (g) + H2O (l)


Answer:

The balanced chemical equation for the reaction between HCl and CaCO3 is:

2HCl(aq) + CaCO3(s) → CaCl2(aq) + CO2(g) + H2O(l)

From the balanced equation, we can see that 2 moles of HCl react with 1 mole of CaCO3.

Calculate the moles of CaCO3:

moles of CaCO3 = 3.7 g / 100.09 g/mol = 0.037 mol

Use the mole ratio from the balanced equation to determine the moles of HCl needed:

moles of HCl = 2 x moles of CaCO3 = 2 x 0.037 mol = 0.074 mol

Calculate the volume of 0.55 M HCl required:

0.55 M HCl means 0.55 moles of HCl are present in 1 liter of solution.

moles of HCl = Molarity x Volume (in liters)

Volume (in liters) = moles of HCl / Molarity

Volume (in liters) = 0.074 mol / 0.55 mol/L = 0.1345 L

Convert the volume from liters to milliliters:

Volume (in milliliters) = Volume (in liters) x 1000

Volume (in milliliters) = 0.1345 L x 1000 = 134.5 mL

Therefore, 134.5 milliliters of 0.55 M HCl are needed to react with 3.7 g of CaCO3.

9. Determine the volume of 0.150 M NaOH solution required to neutralize 85 mL of a 0.715 M HCl solution. The neutralization reaction is NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) → H2O(l) + NaCl(aq)


Formula used -[tex]\large{\boxed{Molarity = \dfrac{Number\:of\:moles\:of\:solute}{Number\:of\:litres\:of\:solution} }}[/tex]Balanced chemical equation -

NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) → H2O(l) + NaCl(aq)

Thus -

=> 1 part of NaOH reacts with 1 part of HCl to give 1 part of water and 1 part of NaCl .

Step 1 : Finding the number of moles of HCl -

Given that -

85 mL of 0.715 M HCl solution is used

Thus -

Volume of solution in litres = 85/1000 L => 0.085 litres

Molarity = 0.715 M

Using the values in the formula for molarity -

[tex]\implies{0.715 = \dfrac{Number\:of\:moles\:of\:HCl\:present\:in\:solution}{0.085} }[/tex]

[tex]\implies{Number\:of\:moles\:of\:HCl\:present = 0.715 \times 0.085 = \boxed{0.060775\:\:moles}}[/tex]

Step 2 : Finding the number of moles of NaOH required to react with the HCl -

From the balanced chemical equation, it is clear that -

=> 1 part of HCl reacts with 1 part of NaOH

Now -

=> Thus, to react with 0.060775 moles of HCl, an equal number of moles of NaOH will be required.

=> So, the number of moles of NaOH required = 0.060775 moles

Step 3 : Finding the volume of 0.15 M NaOH solution required -

We now have -

Number of moles of NaOH = 0.060775 moles

Molarity of NaOH = 0.15 M (molar)

To find -

Volume of NaOH solution required in Litres = ?

Using the known values in the formula for molarity -

[tex]\implies{0.15 = \dfrac{0.060775}{Volume\:of\:NaOH\:solution\:in\:litres} }[/tex]

[tex]\implies{Number\:of\:litres\:of\:NaOH\:solution = \dfrac{0.060775}{0.15} = \boxed{0.40512\:\:litres}}[/tex]

⇒ Volume of required NaOH solution in mL = 0.40512 × 1000 => 405.12 mL

Answer -

=> About 405.12 millilitres of 0.150 M NaOH solution is required for complete neutralization.

21. CaCO3(s) + 2HCl -> CaCl2(aq) + H2O(l) (a) How much CO2(in L) will be produced by 250g of CaCO3 if it is completely reacted with HCl at 25oC and 1.0 atm?

V = 61 L

Explanation:

n CO2 = 250 g CaCO3 × (1 mol CaCO3 / 100.09 g CaCO3) × (1 mol CO2 / 1 mol CaCO3)

n CO2 = 2.5 mol

Convert 25 °C to K

T = 25 °C + 273.15 = 298.15 K

Use Ideal Gas Law to solve for the volume of CO2.

PV = nRT

V = nRT / P

V = (2.5 mol)(0.082057 L•atm/mol•K)(298.15 K) / 1.0 atm

V = 61 L

24. How many milliliters of 0.383 m hcl are needed to react with 16.2 g of caco3?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + CO₂ + H₂O

Step 2: Calculate the volume of HCl solution needed by using dimensional analysis.

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 1 mole of CaCO₃ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 2 moles of HCl.

The molar mass of CaCO₃ is 100.086 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{volume of HCl} & = \text{16.2 g} \: \text{CaCO}_3 \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{CaCO}_3}{\text{100.084 g} \: \text{CaCO}_3} \times \frac{\text{2 mol HCl}}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{CaCO}_3} \times \frac{\text{1 L soln}}{\text{0.383 mol HCl}} \times \frac{\text{1000 mL soln}}{\text{1 L soln}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{845 mL}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 845 mL of HCl is needed to react with 16.2 g of CaCO₃.

25. From the following data, find the percentage purity of a sample of KHSO4: Sample = 1.2118 g NaOH used = 26.28 ml HCl used = 1.53 ml 1 ml HCl≈ 1.206 ml NaOH 1 ml HCl ≈0.02198 g Na2CO3​


Answer:

First, we need to determine the number of moles of NaOH used:

1 ml of NaOH = 1.206 ml of HCl

26.28 ml of HCl used = 31.7 ml of NaOH used (26.28 mL × 1.206 mL NaOH/mL HCl)

The number of moles of NaOH used can be calculated as:

moles of NaOH = (concentration of NaOH) × (volume of NaOH used in L)

= 0.1 M × 0.0317 L

= 0.00317 moles

Since the reaction between NaOH and KHSO4 is a 1:1 ratio, the number of moles of KHSO4 present in the sample is also 0.00317 moles.

Next, we need to determine the mass of impure KHSO4 in the sample:

1 ml of HCl ≈ 0.02198 g of Na2CO3

1.53 ml of HCl used ≈ 0.0337 g of Na2CO3 (1.53 mL × 0.02198 g Na2CO3/mL HCl)

The number of moles of Na2CO3 present can be calculated as:

moles of Na2CO3 = mass of Na2CO3 / molar mass of Na2CO3

= 0.0337 g / 105.99 g/mol

= 0.000317 moles

Since the reaction between Na2CO3 and HCl is a 1:2 ratio, the number of moles of HCl that reacted with Na2CO3 is:

moles of HCl = 2 × moles of Na2CO3

= 2 × 0.000317 moles

= 0.000634 moles

Since the reaction between HCl and KHSO4 is a 1:1 ratio, the number of moles of KHSO4 present in the impure sample is also 0.000634 moles.

Now, we can calculate the percentage purity of the KHSO4 sample:

percentage purity = (mass of pure KHSO4 / total mass of impure sample) × 100

= (0.00317 moles × 136.17 g/mol / 1.2118 g) × 100

= 35.61%

Therefore, the percentage purity of the KHSO4 sample is 35.61%.

28. 5.80.3 mL of NaOH solution was added to 217 mL of a 0.20 M HCl solution to reach the endpoint.What is the concentration of the NaOH solution?


[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of HCl.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of HCl} & = \frac{\text{0.200 mol HCl}}{1000 \: \cancel{\text{mL soln}}} \times 217 \: \cancel{\text{mL soln}} \\ & = \text{0.0434 mol HCl} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the number of moles of H₂SO₄ using dimensional analysis.

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 1 mol NaOH is stoichiometrically equivalent to 1 mol HCl.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of NaOH} & = 0.0434 \: \cancel{\text{mol HCl}} \times \frac{\text{1 mol NaOH}}{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol HCl}}} \\ & = \text{0.0434 mol NaOH} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Final Step: Calculate the concentration of NaOH.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{molarity of NaOH} \: \text{soln} & = \frac{\text{0.0434 mol NaOH}}{80.3 \: \cancel{\text{mL soln}}} \times \frac{1000 \: \cancel{\text{mL soln}}}{\text{1 L soln}} \\ & = \text{0.540 mol NaOH/L soln} \\ & = \boxed{0.540 \: M} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the concentration of NaOH is 0.540 M.
