13. How does Fantine's lack of education contribute to her problems and ultimate death​


Fantine, a character in Victor Hugo's novel "Les Misérables," faces many challenges due to her lack of education. Her lack of education contributes significantly to her problems and ultimate death in several ways:

1. Difficulty finding employment: Without an education, Fantine is limited in the types of jobs she can secure. She ends up working in a factory, where she is subjected to harsh working conditions and meager pay. She is eventually fired from her job, which leaves her with no source of income.

2. Vulnerability to exploitation: Fantine's lack of education makes her vulnerable to exploitation by others. She is deceived by a man who promises to take care of her daughter but instead uses her as a means to extort money from Fantine.

3. Inability to defend herself: When Fantine is arrested, she is unable to defend herself because she is illiterate and cannot understand the charges against her. Her lack of education also makes it difficult for her to navigate the legal system and secure a fair trial.

4. Lack of access to healthcare: Fantine's poor health is exacerbated by her lack of education, which prevents her from understanding how to take care of herself. She cannot afford to see a doctor, and her untreated illness eventually leads to her death.

In summary, Fantine's lack of education limits her opportunities, leaves her vulnerable to exploitation, and prevents her from accessing essential resources like healthcare. These factors contribute to her tragic story and eventual death.

19. how does Mary obedience become an instrument in the fulfillment of God's promise to save us from sin? ​


Answer:

From the Annunciation to the Crucifixion of her Son, Mary can be seen as God's ultimate validation of free will. The Virgin Mary's obedience to the will of God as conveyed to her in the angel Gabriel's message was no less voluntary in its affirmation than the disobedience of the virgin Eve had been in its negation. In the 2nd century St. Irenaeus the Bishop of Lyon and a second generation disciple from the Apostle John wrote: "...so also Mary, betrothed to a man but nevertheless still a virgin, being obedient, was made the cause of salvation for herself and for the whole human race. [...]. Thus, the knot of Eve's disobedience was loosed by the obedience of Mary. What the virgin Eve had bound in unbelief, the Virgin Mary loosed through faith." Against Heresies, 3.22.4, Irenaeus, Bishop of Lyons.

Mary, as the first human to kiss the face of God and the first to believe in Jesus as her Savior, took her place in Salvation History as the first Christian. She is also the one disciple of Jesus who didn't flee or doubt when all the others fled and doubted, but who stayed and accepted to the very end the burden of being under the Cross. Down through the ages the weeping Mary of the Cross witnessing her son's torture and death stands in solidarity with all believers who also suffer and live under the shadow of the Cross.
