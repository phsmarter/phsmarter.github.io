3. essay about current issue in the Philippines ​


Answer:

The Philippines is a country in Southeast Asia that has been grappling with various issues for decades. From political instability to economic challenges, the country has had its fair share of struggles. In this essay, we will focus on one of the most pressing issues currently facing the Philippines: the war on drugs.

The war on drugs in the Philippines was launched by President Rodrigo Duterte in 2016, shortly after he took office. The campaign aimed to rid the country of illegal drugs and drug-related crimes. However, the approach taken by the government has been highly controversial and has drawn criticism from both local and international communities.

One of the main criticisms of the war on drugs is its heavy-handed approach. The Philippine National Police (PNP) has been accused of extrajudicial killings and human rights violations in their pursuit of drug suspects. According to Amnesty International, there have been over 27,000 deaths related to the war on drugs since it began in 2016. Many of these deaths were allegedly carried out by police officers or vigilante groups.

Another issue with the war on drugs is its impact on the poor. The majority of those killed or arrested in drug-related operations are from low-income communities. Many argue that this is because they are easier targets for law enforcement agencies and are less likely to have access to legal representation. Furthermore, the war on drugs has also led to overcrowding in prisons, with many suspects being held without trial or due process.

The war on drugs has also had economic implications for the Philippines. The country's tourism industry has suffered due to negative media coverage of the campaign. Additionally, some foreign investors have expressed concerns about doing business in a country where human rights violations are taking place.

12. Your goal is to write an argumentative essay on the issue of COVID 19 Pandemic in the Philippines.​


Answer:

In former US Secretary of State Henry Kissinger’s recent COVID-19 article, he mused that “nations cohere and flourish on the belief that their institutions can foresee calamity, arrest its impact and restore stability.” More than giving a rush of blood to the head of anyone in power, this brings into front and center the role of politics in a disease process as complicated and transcendental as this pandemic. Apart from being an epidemiologic phenomenon that has caused a global death toll that can parallel if not surpass that of previous plagues of our civilization, COVID-19 had unraveled the powers of nations and their leaders—their strengths and flaws—as they try their best, following a contagion dynamic, to maintain equilibrium in their part of the world by containing the disease lest it spirals out of control and spread to other areas.

COVID-19 was first reported in December last year as a mysterious respiratory illness afflicting seafood market workers in Wuhan, China. Next thing we knew, it had already spread rapidly to multiple countries and to date had ravaged the entire world with massive cases and deaths in its trail and immeasurable economic and social costs for our world to bear now and in generations to come. How did it start? Was it naturally occurring or intentionally released? Was there a delay in reporting? Was there a cover-up? What global cooperation strategies can be deployed? What nations will emerge victorious in the response? After this coronavirus pandemic, what superpower will change the world order? Which kind of politics will prevail? What will be the fate of global health governance? Which countries stand to gain? How will health systems be transformed? How will people benefit from changes in the global health security movement?

These are questions that can frame our understanding of politosomatics which explores the links between politics and pandemics. As Mika Aaltola theorized, a “dis-ease” at the individual somatic body may be viewed as part of a bigger movement in the global political hierarchy. From the deadly Spanish Flu (H1N1) pandemic in 1918 to the hyped-up (because it proved benign later) Swine Flu (also H1N1) pandemic in 2009 to the coronavirus disease of today, pandemics albeit invisible enemies are real, tangible and personal. Individuals and societies are often gripped by their resultant fear, distress and anxiety.

The old and new worlds of the East and West with their differing levels of economy and health system capacities, nuance of ideologies and culture, mix of fear, paranoia and indifference became good staging grounds for pandemics. This “coming plague” storyline started in the 1990s when Avian Flu was all the rage. Initially a disease of wild birds in China that threatened the poultry industry, human practices with livestock and mutation allowed the Avian Flu virus to jump from animals to humans and the thought of migratory birds flying to many destinations and spreading the disease propagated a medico-security paradigm that looks at external health threats as an important security issue of any country.

The “yellow danger” biohazard sign to mark unsafe sites couldn’t be more straightforward. This started the whole containment drama and military-inspired bio-preparedness tactics (think of Outbreak the movie) to mitigate the impacts of infectious diseases. Although disproportionately impacting the food security sector, Avian Flu was enough to stir the health security discourse and trigger the pandemic fantasies of the world then. Until 2003, with the US-led war on terror gaining traction after the 9/11 attacks, the perfect disease storm, Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome (SARS) came about. Again, it started in China but the disease surprised the world when it rapidly spread to major hubs around the world because of global interconnectedness through air travel and superspreaders—infected people who could unknowingly but exponentially infect many others—causing major economic losses and social disruptions in no time. In one fell swoop, SARS met the definition of a pandemic being able to shut down modern life support systems.

These interweaving pandemic and politics stories underscore the need for individual countries, regardless of their positions and power in international politics, to develop capacities for preparedness and resilience to pandemics--the quintessential health threat to the world order.

This is at the core of the International Health Regulations (IHR) and the global health security agenda.

The Philippines, if we are to benefit from, if not change, the new world order after coronavirus, should be strategic enough to use its political resources (and people) and work with global partners not only to respond to COVID-19 but also to do its role in shaping the future of global health security.

15. SURORTLINER Performance Test: Write an essay on the issue of social stratification in the Philippines: N "The effects of Rich and Poor social status in the Philippines and how should this be addressed​


Answer:

The effects of this rich-poor divide are far-reaching and deeply felt. Those at the bottom of the social ladder often lack access to quality education, healthcare, and other essential services, while those at the top enjoy privileges and opportunities that are out of reach for many. This leads to a perpetuation of poverty and inequality, as those who are born into disadvantaged circumstances are often unable to break out of them due to a lack of resources and opportunities.

One of the most pressing issues related to social stratification in the Philippines is the lack of upward mobility. Those who are born into wealthy families are much more likely to succeed and achieve financial stability, while those born into poverty are often trapped there for their entire lives. This lack of mobility has far-reaching consequences for both individuals and society as a whole, as it reinforces a sense of hopelessness and a lack of opportunity.

To address this issue, it is essential that the government and other institutions take steps to address the root causes of poverty and inequality. This includes investing in education and training programs, providing access to healthcare and other essential services, and implementing policies that promote economic growth and development. Additionally, it is important to address issues of corruption and ensure that the wealth of the country is distributed fairly and equitably.

Ultimately, addressing the issue of social stratification in the Philippines will require a concerted effort from all sectors of society. By working together and taking action to address the root causes of poverty and inequality, it is possible to create a more inclusive and just society for all.

18. speech about current issues in the philippines essay​


Good day everyone,

I am here to talk about some of the current issues that the Philippines is facing today. These issues are affecting our country and its people in many ways, and it is important that we are aware of them in order to find ways to address them.

One of the most pressing issues in the Philippines is poverty. Despite the country's economic growth in recent years, poverty remains a significant problem. According to recent statistics, around 16 million Filipinos are still living in poverty, and many of them lack access to basic necessities such as food, shelter, and healthcare. This is particularly evident in rural areas, where poverty rates are significantly higher than in urban areas.

Another issue that is affecting the Philippines is corruption. Corruption is widespread in the country, and it affects many aspects of daily life, from government services to business transactions. This not only undermines the government's ability to deliver services to its citizens but also hinders economic growth and development.

Environmental degradation is also a major concern in the Philippines. The country is home to some of the world's most diverse ecosystems, but these are being threatened by pollution, deforestation, and climate change. The degradation of natural resources is not only a threat to the environment but also to the livelihoods of many Filipinos who rely on these resources for their survival.

Lastly, the Philippines is also grappling with social issues such as inequality, discrimination, and human rights violations. These issues affect various sectors of society, including women, children, indigenous people, and the LGBT+ community.

In conclusion, these are just some of the issues that the Philippines is currently facing. While these problems are complex and difficult to solve, it is important that we acknowledge them and work towards finding solutions. As citizens of this country, we all have a role to play in creating positive change and ensuring a better future for generations to come. Thank you.

As one of the fastest growing countries in Asia, the Philippines faces environmental challenges, with disproportionate impacts on the poor and women. Ineffective management seriously degrades the country’s significant biodiversity resources; water and air pollution levels exceed generally accepted healthy standards; greenhouse gas emissions are increasing from the transport and power sectors; and the country is ranked as one of the world’s most vulnerable to the impacts of environmental disasters.

For the Philippines to become a more stable, prosperous and well-governed nation, the country must become more environmentally resilient and better able to cope with the impact of natural disasters and recover quickly. Natural resources play a critical role in the Philippine economy, as agriculture, fisheries, and forestry represent about 10 percent of gross domestic product and account for almost 30 percent of employment.
