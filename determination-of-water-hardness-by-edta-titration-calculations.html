2. calculate the CaCO3 content of a chalk sample weighing 0.2545 g, consuming 16.67 ml of 0.1150M Disodium EDTA in titration​


Answer:

The reaction equation of Disodium EDTA and Calcium Carbonate is:

CaCO3 + Na2EDTA → Ca(EDTA)2- + 2Na+ + CO2

The stoichiometry of the reaction is 1:1. It means that 1 mole of Disodium EDTA react with 1 mole of CaCO3.

Given data:

Weight of chalk sample = 0.2545 g

Volume of Disodium EDTA = 16.67 ml

Molarity of Disodium EDTA = 0.1150 M

To calculate the CaCO3 content, we need to find the number of moles of Disodium EDTA used in the reaction. We can calculate the number of moles by using the formula:

moles of Disodium EDTA = Molarity × Volume (in liters)

Volume in liters = 16.67 ml = 16.67/1000 = 0.01667 L

moles of Disodium EDTA = 0.1150 × 0.01667 = 0.00191755 moles

As the stoichiometry of the reaction is 1:1, the number of moles of CaCO3 consumed in the reaction is also 0.00191755 moles.

The molar mass of CaCO3 = 40.08 (for Ca) + 12.01 (for C) + 3 × 16 (for 3O) = 100.09 g/mol

The mass of CaCO3 in the sample = Number of moles of CaCO3 × molar mass of CaCO3 = 0.00191755 × 100.09 = 0.192171 g

The percentage of CaCO3 in the sample = (Mass of CaCO3 / Weight of sample) × 100%

= (0.192171 / 0.2545) × 100%

= 75.52%

Therefore, the CaCO3 content in the chalk sample is 75.52%.

Titration is a procedure for determining the concentration of a solution (analyte) by reacting with another solution whose known concentration (usually a standard solution) is called the titrant. Determination of the endpoint / equivalence point of the reaction can use indicators according to the appropriate pH range  

Titrations can be: acid-base titration, depositional titration, and redox titration. An acid-base titration is the principle of neutralization of acids and bases  

Acid-base titration formula  

[tex]\rm \large{\boxed{\bold{Va\times Na=Vb\times Nb}}}[/tex]

Na, Nb = acid base concentration  

Va, Vb = acid base volume

20. Colorimetric titration experiment - determine the amount of vitamin c in a medium peach


To determine the amount of vitamin C in a medium peach using a colorimetric titration experiment, you will need the following materials:

A medium peach

A knife or blender to prepare the peach sample

A 250 mL volumetric flask

A burette or micropipette

A beaker or Erlenmeyer flask

A pH meter or pH indicator solution

A standard vitamin C solution with a known concentration

A solvent, such as distilled water or acetic acid

Here is the general procedure for performing the colorimetric titration experiment:

Prepare the peach sample by blending or cutting the peach into small pieces and transferring it to the 250 mL volumetric flask. Add the solvent to the flask and fill it to the mark with solvent. This will create a solution of the peach sample in solvent.

Use the burette or micropipette to add a known volume of the standard vitamin C solution to the beaker or Erlenmeyer flask.

Add a few drops of pH indicator solution to the beaker or Erlenmeyer flask, or use a pH meter to measure the pH of the solution. The pH should be around 3.0.

Slowly add the peach sample solution from the volumetric flask to the beaker or Erlenmeyer flask, while continuously stirring the solution.

Observe the color change of the solution as the peach sample is added. The color will change from red to yellow as the vitamin C in the peach sample reacts with the indicator solution.

When the color change reaches a maximum, the titration is complete. The volume of peach sample solution added can be used to calculate the concentration of vitamin C in the peach sample using the formula:

[Vitamin C] = (Volume of standard solution added x Concentration of standard solution) / Volume of peach sample solution added

Remember to calibrate the burette or micropipette before performing the titration to ensure accurate measurement of the volume of standard vitamin C solution added. It is also important to use a clean, dry burette or micropipette and to handle the solutions carefully to avoid contamination.

24. Calculate the pH during the titration of 30.00 mL of 0.1000 M KOH with 0.1000 M HCl solution after 25.00 mL of the titrant was added.


[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

Step 1: Calculate the number of moles of HCl in 25.00 mL.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{25.00 mL} \times \frac{\text{0.1000 mol HCl}}{\text{1 L HCl}} \times \frac{\text{1 L}}{\text{1000 mL}} = 2.500 \times 10^{-3} \: \text{mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of KOH originally present in 30.00 mL.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{30.00 mL} \times \frac{\text{0.1000 mol KOH}}{\text{1 L KOH}} \times \frac{\text{1 L}}{\text{1000 mL}} = 3.000 \times 10^{-3} \: \text{mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the amount of KOH left after partial neutralization.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{amount of KOH left} & = 3.000 \times 10^{-3} \: \text{mol} - 2.500 \times 10^{-3} \: \text{mol} \\ & = 5.000 \times 10^{-4} \: \text{mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the OH⁻ concentration in 55.00 mL (30.00 mL + 25.00 mL) of solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} [\text{OH}^-] & = \frac{5.000 \times 10^{-4} \: \text{mol KOH}}{\text{55.00 mL}} \times \frac{\text{1000 mL}}{\text{1 L}} \\ & = 9.091 \times 10^{-3} \: \text{mol KOH/L} \\ & = 9.091 \times 10^{-3} \: M \: \text{KOH} \\ & = 9.091 \times 10^{-3} \: M \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 5: Calculate the pOH of the solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{pOH} & = -\text{log} \: [\text{OH}^-] \\ & = -\text{log} \: (9.091 \times 10^{-3}) \\ & = 2.041 \end{aligned}[/tex]

Final Step: Calculate the pH of the solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{pH} & = 14 - \text{pOH} \\ & = 14 - 2.041 \\ & = \boxed{11.959} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the pH of the solution is 11.959

    Nernst proposed the third law of thermodynamics, which is expressed by the Nernst equation, which compares the ability of an atom or ion to absorb one or more electrons (reduction potential) under any conditions to that under 298 K and one molar or atmospheric pressure. Cell potential may be calculated using the Nernst Equation under non-standard circumstances. It enables for the precise measurement of equilibrium constants by connecting the observed cell potential to the reaction's quotient (including constants of solubility).

Using Nernst Equation:

[tex]E_{cell} = E_{o} - \frac{RT}{nF} 1n Q[/tex]

where, T = 298.15K, F = 9648.5.309 Cal/mol, R = 8.3145 J/mol K, n = net ion change in the final electrode reaction.

Putting these values in the above equation and using lnQ=2.303×logQ, we get:
