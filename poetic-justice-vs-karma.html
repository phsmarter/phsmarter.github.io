Comparison of Hindus concept of Karma to Filipinos Karma:

Karma has been part of many culture, beliefs and religions such as Hinduism. It has great effect to Filipino cultures as well. It was originated in Sanskrit word (kamma) which means action. It means that when you do something the effect can be good or bad.  

In Hinduism, they believe in Hindu Gods. They worshiped them too. But the way they weigh things are way too different from us, Filipinos.Their God Brahma and other Gods in Hindu are known as loving, forgiving and won't serve justice to all who needs to be punish. He won't also reward people who are good. Instead, karma will work on them. If they do something good, then their action could be a result of something good. Also, bad things that are happening to them presently, are believed to be from the past. Karma in the Hinduism can be carry over from previous to next life. They also believe in reincarnation which means, a person can live again but with different body, life and identity. Therefore, they would strive to become better and good persons now to be able to harvest goodness in the future and next lives.

For Filipinos, karma could mean the same thing. If you are good, then you could expect better things to happen to you. But if you get a bad experience, then you can return it to someone or to the same person as he or she deserves to be treated bad. Karma works here as a payment. Karma also stays in one life since they do not believe (most of the Filipinos) in reincarnation.

11. Hinduism teachers on karma. what is Karma? explain.​


Answer:

(in Hinduism and Buddhism) the sum of a person's actions in this and previous states of existence, viewed as deciding their fate in future existences

Answer:

Karma, a Sanskrit word that roughly translates to "action," is a core concept in some Eastern religions, including Hinduism and Buddhism.

Though its specifics are different depending on the religion, karma generally denotes the cycle of cause and effect — each action a person takes will affect him or her at some time in the future. This rule also applies to a person's thoughts and words, and the actions other people take under that individual's instructions.

Today, people use the word karma in ways that are not wholly consistent with its traditional meaning. For example, karma is often misused to denote luck, destiny or fate. Karma is also misused as a way to explain sudden hardships.

With karma, like causes produce like effects; that is, a good deed will lead to a future beneficial effect, while a bad deed will lead to a future harmful effect.

Karma is concerned not only with the relationship between actions and consequences, but also the moral reasons or intentions behind actions, according to a 1988 article in the journal Philosophy East and West. So if someone commits a good deed for the wrong reasons — making a charitable donation to impress a potential love interest, for example — the action could still be immoral and produce bad karma.

Importantly, karma is wrapped up with the concept of reincarnation or rebirth, in which a person is born in a new human (or nonhuman) body after death. The effects of an action can therefore be visited upon a person in a future life, and the good or bad fortune someone experiences may be the result of actions performed in past lives.

What's more, a person's karmic sum will decide the form he or she takes in the next life.

There are a number of Western religious (and non-religious) phrases that are similar to karma, including "what goes around comes around" and "violence begets violence."

24. differences of justice for poor vs rich​


There are several differences in the way justice is often administered for the poor versus the rich:

Legal representation: The wealthy can often afford to hire the best lawyers, while the poor may have limited access to legal representation. This can result in unequal treatment before the law and a lack of resources to defend themselves.

Bail: The wealthy can often afford to pay bail, which allows them to avoid being detained while their case is being processed. On the other hand, the poor may not be able to pay bail and are more likely to be detained while awaiting trial.

Sentencing: The wealthy can often afford to hire experts to testify in their defense, and may receive more lenient sentences as a result. In contrast, the poor may be more likely to receive harsher sentences due to a lack of resources to mount a strong defense.

Plea bargaining: The wealthy may be more likely to have charges against them reduced or dropped through plea bargaining, which is a process that allows defendants to plead guilty to a lesser charge in exchange for a reduced sentence. The poor, on the other hand, may not have the bargaining power or resources to engage in plea bargaining.

Bias: There is evidence to suggest that judges and juries may be biased against the poor, and may be more likely to find them guilty even when the evidence is weak. This bias can lead to unjust outcomes in the criminal justice system.

Overall, these differences highlight the significant disparities in the administration of justice for the poor versus the rich. The poor are often at a disadvantage in the criminal justice system and may face greater challenges in defending themselves and receiving a fair trial.
