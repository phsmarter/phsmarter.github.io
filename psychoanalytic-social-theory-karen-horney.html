12. 1. Psychoanalytic Theory​


Answer:

Psychoanalytic theory postulates a multitude of different change mechanisms, and a host of new ways of conceptualizing the change process continue to emerge as psychoanalytic theories themselves evolve and proliferate. At the most basic level, there is an understanding that change generally involves making the unconscious conscious, as expressed by Freud’s oft cited axiom: “Where id has been there shall ego be.” Although Freud’s understanding of the nature of the change process evolved over the course of his lifetime, central to his mature thinking was the idea that change involves first becoming aware of our instinctual impulses and unconscious wishes, and then learning to deal with them in a mature, rational, and reflective fashion. For Freud, a central premise was thus that we are driven by unconscious wishes that we are unaware of and this lack of awareness results in driven or self-defeating behavior. Freud believed we delude ourselves about reasons for our behaviors and this self-deception limits our choice. By becoming aware of our unconscious wishes and our defenses against them we increase the choices available to us. Thus, as we decrease the extent to which we are driven by unconscious factors we assume a greater degree of agency.

17. Explain the two theories namely Marxism and Psychoanalytical Theory


Answer:

kindly tao the crown

Explanation:

Marxism and Psychoanalytical Theory are two prominent theories in the fields of social sciences and psychology, respectively. Here's an explanation of both theories:

Marxism:

Marxism is a social, political, and economic theory developed by Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels. The theory focuses on the relationship between the working class and the ruling class in society. According to Marx, the working class, or the proletariat, is exploited by the ruling class, or the bourgeoisie, through a system of capitalism.

Marx believed that capitalism creates social inequality and class conflict, which ultimately leads to the overthrow of the bourgeoisie and the establishment of a classless society. He argued that the only way to achieve this is through a revolution led by the working class.

Marxism also emphasizes the importance of material conditions in shaping society. According to Marx, the economic base of society determines the social, political, and cultural superstructure. In other words, the way society is organized economically shapes the way people think and behave.

Psychoanalytical Theory:

Psychoanalytical Theory is a psychological theory developed by Sigmund Freud. The theory focuses on the unconscious mind and how it influences behavior. According to Freud, the unconscious mind is the part of the mind that contains repressed memories, desires, and emotions that are too painful or unacceptable to consciously acknowledge.

Freud believed that behavior is determined by unconscious conflicts between the id (the primitive part of the psyche that seeks pleasure and gratification), the ego (the rational part of the psyche that mediates between the id and the external world), and the superego (the part of the psyche that represents societal and moral values).

Psychoanalytical Theory also emphasizes the importance of childhood experiences in shaping personality. Freud believed that early childhood experiences, particularly those related to sexuality, are crucial in shaping personality and behavior later in life.

In summary, Marxism and Psychoanalytical Theory are two different theories that focus on different aspects of society and psychology. Marxism focuses on social, political, and economic systems, while Psychoanalytical Theory focuses on the unconscious mind and personality development
