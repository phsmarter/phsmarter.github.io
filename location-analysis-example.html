The story is set in Yukon, a remote land where the winter is long and harsh. The story centers around an old man named Koskoosh, who is left to die in the snow by his tribe due to a tribal law. Throughout the story, London uses vivid imagery to show the harshness of the natural world and the inevitability of death.

Summary:

"The Law of Life" tells the story of an old man named Koskoosh, who is abandoned by his tribe in the snow to die. The tribe's law is that anyone who is no longer able to contribute to the tribe should be left to die. Koskoosh is left alone in the snow and must come to terms with his inevitable death. The story shows Koskoosh's final moments and his reflection on his long life.

Personal Reflection:

Reading "The Law of Life" gave me a new perspective on the inevitability of death. The story shows how the natural world can be harsh and unforgiving, and how death is a part of life. It also highlights the importance of community and the role of the tribe in determining the fate of an individual. As someone who lives in a society where the individual is valued above all else, it was interesting to see the role of the tribe in a different culture.

Critical Analysis:

Jack London's use of vivid imagery in "The Law of Life" helps to create a sense of the harshness of the natural world. The story also explores themes of life and death, and the importance of community. Additionally, it raises questions about the morality of abandoning the old and the weak. While the story is set in a different time and culture, its themes are universal and relevant to contemporary society.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, "The Law of Life" is a powerful short story that explores themes of life and death, community, and morality. London's vivid imagery and well-crafted characters help to create a memorable reading experience. The story prompts the reader to reflect on the inevitability of death and the role of community in determining an individual's fate. Overall, "The Law of Life" is a classic work of naturalism literature that continues to resonate with readers today.

Further explanation:Sedimentary rock layers reveal a relative age

The past of Earth is told through fossils. But the clues must be placed in the right order for the story to make sense is how old a particular event or object is in comparison to other events or objects. Many things in your life are presumably relative ages that you are aware of. For instance, even if you don't know a friend's brothers' actual ages, you can infer their relative ages from the fact that she has an older and younger brother.

Information about the relative ages of things that have happened on Earth is preserved in sedimentary rock layers. As you already read, sediments that settle at the bottom of lakes, rivers, and seas give rise to sedimentary rocks. Sedimentary rocks are created by the accumulation of sediment over time. Because it forms earliest, the lowest layer of rock is the oldest. The layer on top is the youngest of all, and each layer above that is younger.

However, over millions of years, rock layers can be disturbed by tectonic plate movement. Layers can be flipped entirely on their side. Like taco shells that start out as flat tortillas, rock layers can bend or even fold over. The youngest layer may not be visible at the top of a group of rock strata that has been disturbed. By contrasting the disturbed rock layers with an equivalent stack of undisturbed strata, scientists can ascertain the original order.
