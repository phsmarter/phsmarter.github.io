10 Social Norms That We Filipinos Applies During A Family Gathering/Reunion

"Ay, ang laki mo na!" Your tita, whom you only see once in a year, only ever says this to you, well, once a year. "Mag-mano ka sa tito/tita/lolo/lola mo." It's quite tiring, but you totally get that feeling of being respected, especially after your nephews and nieces do the same to you. "Kaninong anak ka nga?" Because every Baby is your tita and every Boy is your tito, relatives can get confused when you greet them and they give you aguinaldo."Nagustuhan mo ba 'yung mga pinadala ko?" How can you not? Your favorite aunt from abroad always sends you enough clothes, shoes, makeup, and accessories to get you through all the holiday parties. "Anak, sing for your tito/tita." The youngest kids in the reunion always fall prey to this, but they sure don't mind as long as they get a little extra aguinaldo. "Kailan ba ikakasal si <insert name of cousin here>?"Eager aunts and uncles will always tease young ladies in the family. Even a little show of domestication can earn you a "Pwede nang mag-asawa!" "Buhay pa ba si <insert name of old relative here>?" This is a little shocking to hear, but in a huge family with relatives really young and really old, it's a little difficult to keep track of everyone. "Honor student 'yang anak ko!" Your mom and dad will always be proud of you. They take family reunions as their chance to make sweeping declarations about your wits and talents. "I did it my way!" or "You've lost that loving feeling. Whoa, that loving feeling." A Filipino family reunion with no karaoke is unimaginable, unacceptable, and utterly preposterous. After a few drinks, expect a drunken uncle to sing his heart out.   "O, kodakan na!" A youngster in the family will always be confused as to what this means. Towards the end of the party, someone always announces the photo op when families in color-coordinated outfits line up for snapshots.

10 Values That We Followed Before A Wedding

Formal Introductions and Negotiations . Known in Tagalog as pamamanhikan, this acts as the first official step in a Filipino wedding. Wedding Announcements. The couple makes their rounds to the homes of invitees to personally make their wedding announcements and hand out official wedding invitations. Making Arrangements . The Bulungan, which translates to “whispering," takes place in the bride’s house and involves the bride and groom’s families (usually their elders) huddling together to quietly make plans, budget allocations, and task divisions for the upcoming wedding. The arrangements are all conducted with everyone whispering, so as to not attract bad spirits and misfortune.Wedding Preparations . The wedding preparations take place a day before the wedding and is centered around the Filipino concept of bayanihan, which involves performing small, heroic acts for the good of the community. Many in the bride and groom’s families and community chip in with preparing the reception area, cooking the wedding dishes, and clearing the route for the wedding retinue.Despedida De Soltera . Despedida de Soltera is a Spanish phrase that literally translates to 'farewell to singlehood'. This is also known as a bridal shower for the bride or bachelor / stag party for the groom.One Sibling At A Time . In the Philippines, having your wedding in the same year as your sibling is bad luck.No Peeking . Just like in many cultures, it is bad luck for the groom and the bride to lay eyes on each other hours before the wedding ceremony. No Last Minute Fittings. Brides are forbidden from wearing their wedding dress the day before the matrimony because it is said that it could bring bad luck to the wedding or may cause it to not push through at all.Traditional Filipino Wedding Attire . Grooms at Filipino weddings often wear an outfit known as Barong Tagalog. Wedding Reception . Filipino wedding receptions are known as Handaan. Historically, rice cakes were a major part of the menu at the reception, however, modern Filipino weddings often include all sorts of food.

10. paki answer nmn po if what are the Filipino Muslim wedding traditions​


Answer:

Filipino Muslim wedding traditions, also known as "Nikah" or "Islamic wedding," typically involve several ceremonies and rituals. Some of the key traditions include:

"Saman" or the engagement ceremony, where the bride and groom's families formally agree to the marriage and exchange gifts.

"Mahr" or the dowry, which is given by the groom to the bride as a symbol of his commitment to the marriage.

"Walima" or the reception, which is usually held on the third day after the wedding and is attended by family and friends.

"Ijab-Qabul" or the wedding ceremony, where the couple exchange vows and rings in the presence of an imam and witnesses.

"Seremony of Henna" is where the bride's hands and feet are decorated with henna designs.

"Pamamanhikan" is the traditional meeting of the bride and groom's families to discuss the wedding arrangements.

"Sab-e-Yaman" is the groom's family visit to the bride's house to formally ask for her hand in marriage.

"Mangni" is the engagement ceremony where the couple exchange rings and are formally considered engaged.

"Mehendi" is the henna application ceremony where the bride's hands and feet are decorated with henna.

"Baraat" is the groom's wedding procession from his house to the venue of the wedding ceremony.

Note that the names may vary in different regions, but the main traditions are almost the same across the Philippines.
