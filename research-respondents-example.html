1. PAHELP PODefine the given technical terms:1. Research 2. Abstract 3. Copyright4. Primary sources and examples 5. Secondary sources and examples 6. Research methodology 7. Respondents 8. Hypothesis 9. Variable 10. Research data​


HELLO FOLK1. Research: A systematic investigation of a subject matter or problem to generate new knowledge or establish facts.

2. Abstract: A brief summary of a research article, paper, or analysis.

3. Copyright: Legal protection granted to the creator of an original work to control its use and distribution.

4. Primary sources: Original data or information sources such as interviews, surveys, and experiments.

5. Secondary sources: Information sources that summarize or analyze primary sources such as books, articles, and reviews.

6. Research methodology: The systematic process of planning, conducting, and analyzing research.

7. Respondents: Individuals who participate in a research study and provide data or information.

8. Hypothesis: A proposed explanation or prediction for a phenomenon that is tested through research.

9. Variable: A measurable characteristic or factor that can change or vary in a research study.

10. Research data: Information or evidence collected during a research study that is used to answer research questions or test hypotheses.

Answer:

The choice of sampling method in selecting respondents depends on the research question, the population of interest, and the available resources. Common sampling methods include simple random sampling, stratified sampling, cluster sampling, and purposive sampling.

Yes, respondents are vital in a research study because they provide the data needed to answer the research questions. The selection of appropriate respondents can affect the validity and reliability of the study.

Yes, it is important to plan the procedures ahead of the actual gathering of data to ensure that the research is conducted efficiently and effectively. Planning can also help to identify potential problems and address them before they occur.

Researchers make their own research instrument after they have defined the research questions and objectives. They then select the appropriate method of data collection, design the instrument, and pilot test it to ensure that it is valid and reliable.

Before the research instrument is administered to the respondents, researchers should ensure that it is clear, easy to understand, and free of bias. They should also obtain ethical approval, select appropriate respondents, and decide on the method of administration (e.g. online, in-person, telephone).

TYPES OF COMMUNITY ENGAGEMENT

COMMUNITY ENGAGEMENT MODELS

The following community engagement models are supported by the Office of Community Engagement through community-engaged learning courses, student employment, or volunteer opportunities for groups or individuals.

Community Building

Projects that intentionally bring people together to simply get to know one another. Examples include the Morris Community Meal.

Community Education

Projects that provide instructional services or curricula, or serve to educate the public about a social issue (in a non-partisan way). Examples include the TREC program, Community ESL classes, and Gather in the Park.

Community Organizing

Projects that bring people together with the goal of solving a community issue. Please note that OCE cannot work on partisan issues, but can contribute to creating solutions for non-partisan, local issues. An example is assistance in establishing the Latino Parent Advisory Board for the local school.

Deliberative Dialogue

Projects that intentionally bring people together to build understanding across differences.

Direct Service

Projects that provide a service or product to an individual, group, or the community as a whole. Examples include filling a volunteer shift at a local organization, creating social media tools for an organization, or creating a community mural.

Economic Development

Projects that work on developing the regional economy in a sustainable way. Note that OCE does not partner with for-profit entities except when doing so would benefit the community as a whole and not the for-profit only organization. Examples include feasibility studies for new businesses and projects that attract people to small town business disricts.

Engaged Research

Research that directly benefits the community by clarifying the causes of a community challenge, mapping a community's assets, or contributing to solutions to current challenges and also fits a faculty member's research agenda. In the best case scenario, faculty with research expertise work alongside community members and students on such projects. An example would be the MIEI community needing assessment.

Institutional Engagement

University resources intentionally offered without undue barriers to the community. OCE can play a role in envisioning institutional engagement efforts. Examples include making Briggs library cards available for community members, making campus events accessible, and choosing to use local and sustainable businesses to supply services or goods.

Lack of standardization in the research process for distance education research. There are logistical issues surrounding the research process that need to be addressed. Oftentimes what seems like a streamlined process on paper does not necessarily pan out as such in actual implementation. DETA researchers expressed a top challenge for distance education research is translating the research plan into actual implementation at the designing stage, data collection stage, and analysis stage for distance education studies. Incentivizing participation. Many distance education researchers face the obstacle of finding enough faculty and student participants to warrant a sound, evidence-based research project. Thus, researchers face the difficulty of finding creative strategies for encouraging participation with limited resources. Reliance on self-reported data. 21. example situation of multistage sampling distribution like this:examples like these, please: For example, a researcher wants to understand pet feeding habits among people living in the USA. For this, he/she requires a sample size of 200 respondents. The researcher selects 10 states out of 50 at random. Further, he/she randomly picks out 5 districts per state. From these 50 randomly selected states, he/she then chooses 4 pet-owning households to conduct his research.​


The example you provided is a good illustration of a multistage sampling distribution. Here is another example:

A research team wants to study the impact of a new drug on a certain disease in a population of a large city. The city has multiple neighborhoods with different socio-economic statuses. The research team decides to use a multistage sampling technique to select their sample.

In the first stage, they randomly select 5 neighborhoods out of 20 neighborhoods in the city. In the second stage, they randomly select 50 households from each of the 5 neighborhoods. In the third stage, they randomly select 10 patients with the disease from each of the 50 households, resulting in a total sample size of 2500 patients.

This multistage sampling technique helps to ensure that the sample is representative of the population and allows for greater precision in estimating the prevalence of the disease in the population.
