25. 2. A certain brand of powdered milk is advertised as having a net weight of 250grms. If the net weight of a random sample of 10 cans are 253, 248, 252, 245, 247, 249,251,250, 247 and 248 grams, can it be concluded that the average net weight of the cans is less than the advertised amount?​


Answer:

Therefore, based on the given sample, we cannot conclude that the average net weight of the cans is less than the advertised amount.

Step-by-step explanation:

To determine if it can be concluded that the average net weight of the cans is less than the advertised amount, we need to perform a hypothesis test.

Let's assume that the population mean net weight is equal to the advertised amount of 250 grams. We can then set up the null and alternative hypotheses as follows:

- Null hypothesis (H0): The population mean net weight is equal to 250 grams.

- Alternative hypothesis (Ha): The population mean net weight is less than 250 grams.

We can then use a one-tailed t-test with a significance level of 0.05 to test the hypotheses. The degrees of freedom for the t-test is n-1 = 9.

Using a calculator or software, we can calculate the t-statistic and the corresponding p-value. The t-statistic is calculated as:

t = (sample mean - hypothesized mean) / (sample standard deviation / sqrt(n))

t = (249.3 - 250) / (2.472 / sqrt(10))

t = -1.22

The p-value for this test is 0.121, which is the probability of observing a t-statistic as extreme as -1.22 or more extreme, assuming the null hypothesis is true.

Since the p-value (0.121) is greater than the significance level (0.05), we fail to reject the null hypothesis. This means that we do not have enough evidence to conclude that the average net weight of the cans is less than the advertised amount. In other words, we cannot say for certain that the cans do not meet the advertised weight.

Therefore, based on the given sample, we cannot conclude that the average net weight of the cans is less than the advertised amount.
