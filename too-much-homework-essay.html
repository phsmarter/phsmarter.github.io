No Homework Policy

The claim that no-homework policies do not improve academic performance is the one made most frequently in favor of them. If young people find their academic pursuits tedious and challenging, how can it be true that they will lead the future generation? They are under pressure to perform to the best of their abilities due to the high expectations of society.

Even though I don't go to elementary or secondary school, I will support the "no homework policy" as a college student. I found it difficult to do all of my homework and paperwork during my senior year. Being the oldest in our family, I am also in charge of taking care of the home, making it challenging for me to complete my homework after school. Even on the weekends, we are unable to socialize with our friends, classmates, and family due to our schoolwork. We have no choice but to complete our schoolwork over the weekends, so we do it at home. Our mentality is one of survival, even if we don't even love it.

Not all of us are wealthy, and many of us have shaky financial situations. The money our parents worked really hard to earn is what we utilize every day. 27. "does homework help you learn?" argumentative essay​


Answer:

Research has shown that homework can improve the achievement of students in terms of exam results, grades, and the likelihood of attending college. Studies have also indicated that students who spend time each day on their homework scored better on exams compared to students who didn't do homework. One can develop a better memory and incorporate new skills with repetition. Homework is based on classwork and focuses on integrating the new skill through its practice. This helps in boosting memory and retaining the acquired knowledge for exams and future tests. Homework can provide an increased understanding of concepts taught at school. Giving students extra practice, allows those concepts to be committed to each student's long term memory. Good homework reinforces what is taught at school.

Homework has been a controversial topic for many years, and the question of whether it actually helps students learn is one that is still debated today. While some argue that homework is an essential part of the learning process, I believe that it is not an effective tool for helping students learn. In fact, homework can often be a waste of time for students who have responsibilities beyond their homes, and it can even lead to cheating and academic dishonesty.

One of the main arguments against homework is that it can be copied or found online. With the internet at our fingertips, students can easily search for answers to homework questions and copy them without actually learning anything. This can lead to a false sense of understanding and can even be detrimental to a student's learning in the long run. Additionally, students who copy homework may not actually understand the material and may struggle with future assignments or tests.

Furthermore, homework can be a waste of time for students who have responsibilities beyond their homes. Many students have part-time jobs, extracurricular activities, or family obligations that take up much of their time outside of school. For these students, spending hours on homework each night can be a burden and can even detract from their ability to focus on other important tasks. This can lead to stress and burnout, which can ultimately harm a student's academic performance and well-being.

In conclusion, I do not support the argument that homework helps students learn. While it may be a useful tool for some students, it can also lead to cheating and academic dishonesty, and it can be a waste of time for those with responsibilities beyond their homes. Instead of relying on homework as the sole means of learning, schools should focus on creating engaging and interactive classrooms that allow students to learn in a variety of ways. By doing so, we can help all students succeed and reach their full potential.
