The terms paradigm and theory are often used interchangeably in social science, although social scientists do not always agree whether these are identical or distinct concepts. Paradigms in Social Science

For our purposes, we’ll define paradigm as an analytic lens, a way of viewing the world and a framework from which to understand the human experience (Kuhn, 1962).See Kuhn’s seminal work for more on paradigms: Kuhn, T. (1962). The structure of scientific revolutions. Chicago, IL: University of Chicago Press. It can be difficult to fully grasp the idea of paradigmatic assumptions because we are very ingrained in our own, personal everyday way of thinking. For example, let’s look at people’s views on abortion. To some, abortion is a medical procedure that should be undertaken at the discretion of each individual woman who might experience an unwanted pregnancy. To others, abortion is murder and members of society should collectively have the right to decide when, if at all, abortion should be undertaken. Chances are, if you have an opinion about this topic you are pretty certain about the veracity of your perspective. Then again, the person who sits next to you in class may have a very different opinion and yet be equally confident about the truth of his or her perspective. Which of you is correct? You are each operating under a set of assumptions about the way the world does—or at least should—work. Perhaps your assumptions come from your particular political perspective, which helps shape your view on a variety of social issues, or perhaps your assumptions are based on what you learned from your parents or in church. In any case, there is a paradigm that shapes your stance on the issue.

13. Give your own review of the social phenomenon, "The Use of Social Media" based on the 3 Sociological perspectives (paradigms) of society: 1. Structural Functionalism Theory 2. Conflict Theory 3. Symbolic Interactionism Theory​


a brief overview of how each sociological perspective views the use of social media.

1. Structural Functionalism Theory: This theory would view social media as a tool that serves a particular function in society. It would suggest that social media can help people to connect with others, share information, and develop social networks. It would argue that social media can help to support social cohesion and reinforce shared norms and values. Social media can also serve to connect people on a global scale, allowing for communication and collaboration between individuals and groups who may be physically distant from one another.

2. Conflict Theory: This theory would view social media through the lens of power and inequality. It would suggest that social media can be used to reinforce existing power relationships, with those who hold more power in society being able to use social media to amplify their voices and control the narrative. Conflict theorists would argue that social media can be used as a tool for social control and manipulation, with those in power using it to shape public opinion and advance their own interests.

3. Symbolic Interactionism Theory: This theory would view social media as a platform for communication and interaction between individuals. It would suggest that social media allows people to create and maintain relationships, express themselves, and represent themselves to others. Symbolic interactionists would argue that social media has the potential to shape our identities, with individuals using social media to construct and present themselves in particular ways. They would also point out that social media can create new forms of social interactions and relationships that can have both positive and negative effects on individuals and communities.
