7. What is the significance of the motifs of journey and home in Homestretch by Velma Pollard.​


Explanation:

In "Homestretch" by Velma Pollard, the motifs of journey and home are significant in several ways. Firstly, the journey represents a physical and emotional process of self-discovery and transformation for the protagonist, Cynthia. Throughout her journey from Jamaica to England and back, Cynthia experiences a range of challenges, including homesickness, cultural differences, and racial discrimination. These experiences help her to develop a stronger sense of self-awareness and self-identity.

Secondly, the motif of home represents a sense of belonging and cultural identity. For Cynthia, home is not just a physical place, but also a cultural and emotional space where she feels connected to her roots and heritage. However, the idea of home is complicated by her experiences of racism and discrimination in England, which make her question where she truly belongs.

Overall, the motifs of journey and home in "Homestretch" highlight the complexities of identity, belonging, and cultural heritage in the context of migration and displacement. Through Cynthia's experiences, the novel explores the ways in which these themes intersect and interact with each other, and how they shape our sense of self and place in the world.


8. what did odysseus as cyclops​


The cyclops Polyphemus traps Odysseus and his men in a cave, behind an enormous rock. Only the cyclops is strong enough to move the rock, so Odysseus can’t escape. Instead, Odysseus hatches a plan. While the cyclops is out with his sheep, Odysseus sharpens a piece of wood into a stake and hardens it in the fire. Next, he gives the cyclops wine to get him drunk, and he tells the cyclops his name is “Nobody.” When the cyclops falls asleep, Odysseus blinds him with the hardened stake. Polyphemus’ screams summon the other cyclops, but when he shouts “Nobody’s killing me!” they go away again. In the morning, the cyclops must let his sheep out to graze. He feels the sheep as they leave, to make sure his prisoners aren’t escaping too, but Odysseus and his men are clinging to the sheep’s bellies. Odysseus’s escape from Polyphemus demonstrates his main character trait: a gift for tactics and trickery. It’s significant that Odysseus’s stratagem requires him to conceal his reputation and identify himself as “Nobody.” The Odyssey explores the nature of identity and the tension between a person’s reputation in the world and who he is in his inner life
