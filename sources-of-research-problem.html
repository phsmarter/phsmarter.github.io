22. 5 sources of research problems


Answer:

1. Objective or aim of the problem which is to be investigated.

2. The topic or theme which needs to be investigated.

3. The time dimension of a decision problem is always the future.

4. The area or location in which the study is to be conducted.

5. Population or universe from whom the data needs to be gathered.

Explanation:

1. Objective or aim of the problem which is to be investigated. This answers the question “Why?” Why is there a need for investigation, inquiry or study?

2. The topic or theme which needs to be investigated. This answers the question “What?” What is to be researched or studied?” For example: What would a rival company do if we decrease our prices by 25%? What would sales be if prices were Rs. 89 ? Rs. 99 ? How would a rival firms action influence our sales and profits? The right question needs to be addressed if research is to help decision makers. The decision maker can’t acquire all the information, but it is often feasible to identify the factors that are critical to the existing problem. These factors are then included in the problem definition.

3. The time dimension of a decision problem is always the future. The period or time of the study when the data are to be gathered. This answers the question “When?” When is the research to be performed?” Managers frequently run the risk of making the correct decision at incorrect time. It is essential that the decision maker as well as the researcher determine the right time reference for-the decision.

4. The area or location in which the study is to be conducted. This answers the question “Where?” Where we need to conduct the study? The space coordinates give you the geographic boundaries within which the action is to be taken. In the problem definition, these lines are hardly ever neat political divisions or subdivisions. The universe of interest should be defined either conceptually or by enumeration.

5. Population or universe from whom the data needs to be gathered. This answers the question “Who?” or “from whom?” Who are the respondents? From who are the data to be collected?” They may include  persons, groups of persons, business establishments.

29. What are the different sources of Research Problems?


Answer:

- Personal or professional experience

- Theory

- Literature and the media

Explanation:

Personal or professional experience

Everyday personal or professional experience could lead us to recognize a problem that we would like to solve. Alternatively, we may have a topic or question that we would like to try to resolve.

For example, on a personal level, you might prefer the taste of organically produced vegetables and so wonder whether people usually prefer the taste of organically produced vegetables to non-organic ones.

The research subject is an analysis of taste tastes and the question 'do people usually favor the taste of organically grown vegetables to non-organic ones? 'Alternatively, for example, as a knowledgeable nature reserve guard, you may want to promote the establishment and distribution of a specific plant species, since you know that it is a food source for rare butterflies. The study issue may be, 'How do I facilitate the spread of plant species of interest?

Theory

This is a study about the relationship between situations or events. The hypotheses may be universal, widely held views (like domestic cats are the cause of a reduction in the number of birds in the UK gardens) or more technical ideas (for example, that global warming is causing a time change of the seasonal responses of the flowering cherry tree in the UK).

Literature and the media

There are a variety of sources of literature, such as novels, journal articles and journals. It is possible to find gaps in understanding and expertise and issues about which there is actually no solution in the search and reading of literature. They can provide a strong base for analysis.We are often filled with knowledge from media sources, such as TV, that could contribute to your study.
