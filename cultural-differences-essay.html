5. essay about differences of biological and cultural evolution of human being ​


Answer:Sociobiology started as the study of the biological evolution of social behavior. Human behavior, however, is the result of the interaction of both biological and cultural factors. Therefore, a theory of human sociobiology should be focused on the interaction of biological and cultural evolution. The recent development of gene-culture coevolutionary theory is a first step into that direction though it is still far from being applicable to concrete situations. One of the main reasons for this is that so far a comprehensive theory of cultural evolution is lacking, thereby leaving unclear the precise nature of the influence of genetic change on cultural change, and vice versa. To be sure, attempts have been made to explain cultural evolution by simply transferring the mechanisms of biological evolution to a cultural setting. This approach, however, is not satisfactory, mainly because the principles that allow the conservation and transfer of information in cultural evolution are quite different from those in biological evolution. In fact, if both types of evolution are different expressions of the same principle, as the names suggest, it should be possible to formulate a more basic definition of evolution. This definition, then, could be used as the starting point for the construction of a theory of cultural evolution. Using concepts from general systems theory, this paper attempts to provide such a basis by formulating a generalized definition of evolution, which applies both to biological and cultural evolution.

9. Make an essay about What cultural differences and similarities between you and your classmate?


Cultural Differences and Similarities Between Me and My Classmate

My classmate and I have a distinctive set of cultural differences and similarities because we both grew up in different parts of the world. The experiences we had in school helped my Chinese classmate and I, who are both from the United States, to respect one another's cultural heritages.

Our communication styles are one of the biggest cultural differences between us. While I am more direct and assertive in my communication, my classmate tends to be more indirect and reserved. For instance, whereas I tend to be more outspoken and assertive when expressing my opinions, my classmate may be reluctant to speak up or offer their opinion during group discussions. Our group work has occasionally been complicated by this cultural difference, but we've learned to communicate and accommodate one another's learning styles.

Our tastes in food represent another cultural difference. I had never tried any of the traditional Chinese foods that my classmate introduced me to. I've also fed them some of my favorite hamburgers and hot dogs from the United States. Despite these differences, we share a love of trying new foods and an appreciation for the role that food plays in each of our cultures.

Nevertheless, despite these cultural differences, we also have a lot in common. For instance, we both place a high value on education and work hard to succeed in school. We both love music, and we enjoy listening to a wide range of styles, from classical to pop.

We also recognize the value of family and the reverence for elders in our respective cultures. Although we may express these values in different ways, the fundamental ideas remain the same.

In conclusion, my classmate and I have experienced unusual interactions and difficulties as a result of our cultural contrasts and similarities. But through effective communication and learning to respect one another's backgrounds, our friendship has grown stronger and richer.

Kinship is a concept that has always fascinated me, as it is something that is central to human relationships and yet varies so widely across different cultures. Recently, I have been reflecting on my own experiences with kinship and how they have been shaped by the various social cultures I have encountered throughout my life.

Growing up in a Filipino family, kinship was a fundamental aspect of my upbringing. I was taught to show respect to my elders and to always prioritize the needs of my family over my own. This included extended family members such as aunts, uncles, and cousins, who were often considered as close as immediate family members. I remember spending weekends and holidays with my relatives, attending family gatherings, and taking part in traditional celebrations such as Christmas and Easter.

However, as I grew older and started to interact with people from different cultures, I began to realize that kinship could take on very different forms. For example, in Western cultures, it is common for people to move away from their families as they become adults and to prioritize their own individual goals and desires over those of their family. This is a stark contrast to the collectivist culture that I grew up in, where the needs of the group always come first.

These different cultural values have had a profound impact on the way I view kinship and family. On one hand, I value the closeness and support that comes with a collectivist culture, as it has instilled in me a strong sense of community and connection with my relatives. On the other hand, I also appreciate the independence and self-reliance that comes with individualist cultures, as it has allowed me to pursue my own goals and aspirations.

In my opinion, the way that kinship is conceptualized and practiced reflects broader social and cultural values. For example, a culture that values individualism may prioritize personal achievement and independence, whereas a culture that values collectivism may prioritize the needs of the group and the maintenance of social harmony.

In conclusion, my reflections on kinship have shown me the importance of understanding and appreciating different social cultures. By recognizing the diverse ways in which people conceptualize and practice kinship, we can gain a deeper understanding of ourselves and the world around us. Ultimately, this can help us to build stronger and more inclusive communities that are grounded in mutual respect and understanding.
