20. differences of spantaneous Abortion and Induced Abortion​


Answer:

PIP: Recent epidemiologic studies have found that spontaneous abortion has no association with breast cancer, while induced abortion is associated with a moderately increased risk. Although this finding may, in part, reflect confounding and ascertainment bias, there are credible biologic reasons for a difference in risk between these two events. Experiments with rats have indicated that proliferated but not differentiated terminal duct and lobular cells increase the susceptibility of breast tissue to carcinogens. Since ductal and lobuloalveolar breast cells proliferate in the first two trimesters of pregnancy and differentiate in the third, it would follow that the later (up through the late second trimester) the pregnancy is terminated, the greater the risk. Although there is a trend toward induced abortion at earlier gestational ages, spontaneous abortion tends to be the earlier event. Even when induced and spontaneous abortions occur at the same gestational age, dead embryos or fetuses are probably retained in utero for several weeks before expulsion, meaning hormone levels drop appreciably earlier than is the case with induced abortion, where the hormone profile drops only after termination. Moreover, since the average time to another pregnancy is significantly shorter after spontaneous abortion (12 months), the protective effect of subsequent full-term pregnancies may dilute the risk effect of spontaneous abortion to a greater extent than is the case with induced abortion. Now that most induced abortions occur in the first trimester, the implications for breast cancer risk should become minimal for both types of interrupted pregnancies.

29. What is The Influence of the Catholic Church on the issue of Abortion and Reproductive Health Law? Please provide the sources of your research regarding the topic itself.​


Answer:

The Catholic Church has a long history of opposing abortion, as it believes that life begins at conception and that taking a human life is wrong. The Catholic Church's influence on the issue of abortion and reproductive health law is seen both in its vocal opposition to abortion and its active involvement in politics. In 2012, the Church openly urged Catholics to consider the Church’s position when electing political leaders, and in 2013, the Pope declared abortion “intrinsically evil” during a public mass.

In most Catholic countries, abortion is illegal except in cases of a medical emergency or risk to the health of the mother. Catholic Church leaders such as U.S. Knights of Columbus Supreme Knight Carl Anderson have cited Pope Francis’ statement in 2013 to argue against abortion access in the U.S. The Church also plays a key role in the push for laws limiting contraception access and has filed amicus briefs in related court cases.

In terms of the influence of the Church on reproductive health law, it is important to note that the Catholic Church has a long history of opposing contraception, sterilization, and in vitro fertilization, and its views on the issue are widely reflected in the laws of many Catholic countries. In a strongly Catholic society such as Mexico, abortion remains illegal except in very specific cases despite the fact that abortion has been decriminalized in other Latin American countries.
