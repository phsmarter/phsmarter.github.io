Indigenous peoples, also known as first peoples, aboriginal peoples or native peoples, are ethnic groups who are the original inhabitants of a given region, in contrast to groups that have settled, occupied or colonized the area more recently. Groups are usually described as indigenous when they maintain traditions or other aspects of an early culture that is associated with a given region. Not all indigenous peoples share this characteristic, usually having adopted substantial elements of a colonising culture, such as dress, religion or language. Indigenous peoples may be settled in a given region (sedentary) or exhibit a nomadic lifestyle across a large territory, but they are generally historically associated with a specific territory on which they depend. Indigenous societies are found in every inhabited climate zone and continent of the world. Body

Indigenous people often face discrimination and marginalization due to the fact that they are not members of the majority population in their respective countries. They may be seen as inferior or considered to be a burden on society. As a result, they may be denied basic rights and access to essential services.

This includes the right to self-determination, the right to their ancestral lands, and the right to cultural and linguistic expression. 16. Reflection of Indigenous science and technology in the Philippines


Indigenous science and technology in the Philippines refers to the traditional knowledge, practices, and technologies of the country's indigenous communities, which have been developed over generations through close observation of the natural environment and the application of local knowledge and resources. These practices and technologies are deeply rooted in the cultural and spiritual beliefs of the indigenous peoples and are often closely linked to their livelihoods, social organization, and community life.

In the Philippines, the indigenous peoples comprise a significant portion of the population and are recognized by the government as distinct cultural communities with their own rights and identities. Many of these communities have developed sophisticated systems of agriculture, medicine, architecture, and crafts that are adapted to their specific ecological and cultural contexts. For example, the Ifugao people of the Cordillera region are known for their terraced rice fields, which are not only aesthetically impressive but also highly efficient in conserving water and soil and maximizing crop yields. The T'boli people of Mindanao are skilled in weaving intricate patterns from abaca fiber, while the B'laan people are known for their knowledge of medicinal plants and their use in treating various ailments.

Despite the richness and diversity of indigenous science and technology in the Philippines, it has often been marginalized and undervalued in the face of the dominant Western scientific paradigm. Indigenous knowledge and practices have been dismissed as "primitive" or "superstitious" and have often been replaced by modern technologies and ways of life that are not always compatible with local ecosystems and cultures. This has led to the erosion of traditional knowledge and practices and has resulted in the loss of biodiversity and cultural diversity.

However, in recent years, there has been a growing recognition of the value of indigenous science and technology in promoting sustainable development and cultural diversity. Many indigenous communities are now actively involved in efforts to preserve and promote their traditional knowledge and practices, and there are also initiatives to incorporate indigenous knowledge into mainstream science and education. For example, the Department of Education in the Philippines has developed a curriculum that integrates indigenous knowledge and practices into science and social studies subjects, and there are also research programs that focus on documenting and studying indigenous science and technology.

24. reflection about Indigenous Peoples Rights Act​


ABSTRACT

The article examines the application of the UN Declaration on Indigenous Rights (UNDRIP) beyond the confines of a State entity. Specifically, it explores the potential integration of indigenous housing, land and property protections into an occupation setting – where sovereignty is suspended and the responsibility to protect the population’s rights is vested in an external governing power. Can and should indigenous protections be integrated into the law of occupation? What implications would that entail vis-à-vis the limits that the law of occupation imposes on the occupant to introduce new legal obligations into occupied territory? The need to address this theoretical inquiry arises from the dire conditions of indigenous Bedouin in the West Bank, a Palestinian territory occupied by Israel since 1967. Historically and culturally, the West Bank Bedouin have close ties to Bedouin within Israel, the latter acknowledged internationally as indigenous people. Therefore, arguably, the West Bank Bedouin are also entitled to indigenous protections – the relevant difference being that the latter live in occupied territory. The case study of the West Bank Bedouin offers thus a unique opportunity to examine the potential relationship between the law of occupation and the indigenous rights regime as embodied in UNDRIP.
