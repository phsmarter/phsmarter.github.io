1. write a one paragraph essay about Carbohydrates, Proteins, Lipids, Nucleic acids​


Carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids are organic molecules found in every living
organism. These macromolecules are large carbon based structures. The macromolecules are assembled by joining several smaller units, called monomers, together through a chemical reaction called dehydration synthesis. The resulting polymer can be disassembled through the complimentary process called hydrolysis.
Carbohydrates are used by living organisms as an important source of energy. Examples of carbohydrates include glucose, fructose, sucrose, galactose, ribose, deoxyribose, cellulose and chitin.
Lipids (Fats) Lipids are energy rich storage molecules. One gram of lipid stores twice as much energy as one gram of a carbohydrate.
Proteins are composed of amino acids. Proteins serve as the major building blocks of organisms. Proteins are large complex molecules that combine to form various components of living organisms such as muscle fibers, enzymes, and hemoglobin. Proteins are made of unique combinations of the twenty amino acid monomers. A string of amino acid monomers joined together by peptide bonds is called a polypeptide.
Nucleic Acids are RNA and DNA. DNA is the carrier of genetic information- specifically the code for making proteins. RNA aids in the process of synthesizing proteins.

17. 7 important information about proteins​


Answer:

9 Important Functions of Protein in Your Body

1. Growth and Maintenance

Your body needs protein for growth and maintenance of tissues.

Under normal circumstances, your body breaks down the same amount of protein that it uses to build and repair tissues. Other times, it breaks down more protein than it can create, thus increasing your body’s needs.

2. Causes Biochemical Reactions

Enzymes are proteins that aid the thousands of biochemical reactions that take place within and outside of your cells (7Trusted Source).

The structure of enzymes allows them to combine with other molecules inside the cell called substrates, which catalyze reactions that are essential to your metabolism (8Trusted Source).

3. Acts as a Messenger

Some proteins are hormones, which are chemical messengers that aid communication between your cells, tissues and organs.

They’re made and secreted by endocrine tissues or glands and then transported in your blood to their target tissues or organs where they bind to protein receptors on the cell surface.

4. Provides Structure

Some proteins are fibrous and provide cells and tissues with stiffness and rigidity.

These proteins include keratin, collagen and elastin, which help form the connective framework of certain structures in your body (13Trusted Source).

5. Maintains Proper pH

Protein plays a vital role in regulating the concentrations of acids and bases in your blood and other bodily fluids (16Trusted Source, , 7 neutral and 14 the most alkaline.

6. Balances Fluids

Proteins regulate body processes to maintain fluid balance.

Albumin and globulin are proteins in your blood that help maintain your body’s fluid balance by attracting and retaining water (21Trusted Source, 22Trusted Source).

7. Bolsters Immune Health

Proteins help form immunoglobulins, or antibodies, to fight infection (25Trusted Source, 26Trusted Source).

Antibodies are proteins in your blood that help protect your body from harmful invaders like bacteria and viruses.

8. Transports and Stores Nutrients

Transport proteins carry substances throughout your bloodstream — into cells, out of cells or within cells.

The substances transported by these proteins include nutrients like vitamins or minerals, blood sugar, cholesterol and oxygen (30Trusted Source, 31Trusted Source, 32Trusted Source).

9. Provides Energy

Proteins can supply your body with energy.

Protein contains four calories per gram, the same amount of energy that carbs provide. Fats supply the most energy, at nine calories per gram.

25. Learning Task 4. Essay Explain how protein is made using information from DNA.​


Answer:

Proteins are complex biomolecules that are essential for the proper functioning of living organisms. The process of protein synthesis involves the conversion of genetic information stored in DNA to functional proteins. This process is carried out in two main steps: transcription and translation.

Transcription is the first step of protein synthesis. During transcription, a section of DNA, called a gene, is copied into a single-stranded RNA molecule, which is called messenger RNA (mRNA). The enzyme RNA polymerase reads the DNA template and builds a complementary mRNA molecule by matching nucleotide bases. The mRNA molecule then separates from the DNA template and is transported out of the cell nucleus to the ribosomes, where it is used as a blueprint to build a protein.

Translation is the second step of protein synthesis. It occurs in the ribosomes, which are the cellular structures where proteins are made. During translation, the ribosome reads the mRNA molecule in groups of three nucleotides, called codons, and matches them with the corresponding amino acid. The ribosome then links the amino acids together to form a protein chain, which eventually folds into a functional protein.

The sequence of nucleotides in the DNA determines the sequence of amino acids in the protein. There are 20 different amino acids that can be combined in various ways to form different proteins. The order of the amino acids in a protein determines its shape and function. Therefore, the DNA sequence provides the blueprint for protein synthesis, and any changes in the DNA sequence can affect the protein's structure and function.

In summary, protein synthesis involves the conversion of genetic information stored in DNA to functional proteins. This process is carried out in two main steps: transcription and translation. During transcription, a section of DNA is copied into mRNA, which is then transported to the ribosomes. In translation, the ribosome reads the mRNA and links the amino acids together to form a protein chain. The sequence of nucleotides in the DNA determines the sequence of amino acids in the protein, and any changes in the DNA sequence can affect the protein's structure and function.

Proteins are large, complex molecules that play a critical role in a wide range of biological processes, including the structure and function of cells and tissues, metabolism, and the immune system. Protein synthesis, the process by which proteins are made, is a complex and highly regulated process that involves several steps and multiple molecules.

The first step in protein synthesis is transcription, in which the information in DNA is used to produce a messenger RNA (mRNA) molecule. This occurs in the nucleus of the cell, where the DNA is located. During transcription, an enzyme called RNA polymerase binds to the DNA molecule and reads the sequence of nucleotides (the building blocks of DNA) to create a complementary RNA molecule. The mRNA molecule contains the information needed to produce the protein.

Next, the mRNA molecule moves out of the nucleus and into the cytoplasm, where it encounters ribosomes, the structures responsible for assembling proteins. The mRNA molecule binds to the ribosome, and the process of translation begins. During translation, transfer RNA (tRNA) molecules bring amino acids to the ribosome, where they are assembled into a chain to form the protein.

The sequence of nucleotides in the mRNA molecule determines the sequence of amino acids in the protein. Each three-nucleotide sequence, known as a codon, corresponds to a specific amino acid. The tRNA molecules recognize the codons in the mRNA molecule and bring the appropriate amino acid to the ribosome. The amino acids are then linked together to form the protein molecule.

The process of protein synthesis is complex and tightly regulated. Multiple enzymes, proteins, and regulatory molecules are involved in each step of the process to ensure that the protein is produced correctly and in the right amount. Mutations or errors in any of these molecules can lead to defects in protein synthesis and can result in a range of diseases and disorders.

In conclusion, protein synthesis is a complex process that involves multiple steps and molecules. DNA provides the blueprint for protein synthesis, and the sequence of nucleotides in the DNA molecule determines the sequence of amino acids in the protein. Understanding how proteins are made is critical to understanding a wide range of biological processes and diseases, and ongoing research in this area is critical to developing new treatments and therapies.

Handwashing with soap is a fundamental practice for maintaining personal hygiene and preventing the spread of infectious diseases. Soap is composed of long chains of molecules called fatty acids and salts. These molecules have two distinct ends; a hydrophilic end that is attracted to water and a hydrophobic end that repels water but is attracted to oil and grease. This unique molecular structure of soap makes it an effective tool for removing dirt, grime, and other impurities from the skin.

The protein composition of the coronavirus is essential to its structure and function. The virus has a protein coat that protects its genetic material and allows it to attach to and enter host cells. When the virus comes into contact with the skin, the fatty acid molecules in soap can interact with the protein coat, causing it to break down. This process is known as denaturation and occurs when the structure of a protein is altered, leading to its loss of function.

The hydrophobic end of the soap molecule interacts with the fatty acids in the lipid bilayer of the virus's protein coat. This interaction weakens the structure of the coat and breaks it apart, exposing the genetic material inside. The hydrophilic end of the soap molecule then interacts with the exposed genetic material, disrupting the bonds that hold it together. This process ultimately leads to the destruction of the virus, rendering it unable to infect host cells and replicate.

Moreover, washing hands with soap for at least 20 seconds helps to physically remove any viral particles that may be present on the skin. The mechanical action of rubbing the hands together, combined with the detergent action of soap, helps to lift the virus off the skin's surface and wash it away.

In summary, the unique molecular structure of soap makes it an effective tool for breaking down the protein coat of the coronavirus. By disrupting the structure of the coat, soap ultimately destroys the virus, rendering it unable to infect host cells and replicate. Additionally, the mechanical action of washing hands with soap helps to physically remove viral particles from the skin, further reducing the risk of infection. Therefore, handwashing with soap remains one of the most effective ways to prevent the spread of infectious diseases, including the coronavirus.
