15. Comparative essay about Aristotelian Concept of “Telos” of self and human actions and Book of Job’s Perspective about self and life


Answer:

In Aristotelian philosophy, the concept of "telos" refers to the idea that every individual and every action has a specific purpose or end goal, known as its "final cause." According to Aristotle, the ultimate purpose or "telos" of an individual is to achieve eudaimonia, or human flourishing, through the development of their unique potential and the pursuit of virtue. This concept is in contrast to the Book of Job's perspective on self and life, which emphasizes the suffering and challenges that individuals may experience in their pursuit of meaning and fulfillment. In the Book of Job, the main character grapples with the question of why he is experiencing hardships and suffering despite his righteousness, and ultimately concludes that the ultimate purpose or meaning of life is beyond human understanding and is ultimately up to God. Overall, the Aristotelian concept of "telos" and the perspective presented in the Book of Job differ in their views on the purpose and meaning of human life and actions.

Depending on the context of the question, answering who you are can require as simple, or a complex, answer. In part, this multi-faceted question has led to a wide body of literature from multiple fields of study, all trying to figure out what exactly is an identity.

If you think about it, do you really know or understand your identity? Are you a single, coherent expression of yourself in every situation, or do you change, and act differently, with different people? If you wake up in a bad mood, can you really say you're the same person you are on a good day?

Even popular music gets involved with the debate with the lyrics of a Lardpony song titled You Vary as a Function of Time.

And now, let's take a look at some of the scholarly theories of identity, the field of study they originated from, and the theorist involved.

Erik H. Erikson (1902-1994)

Field: Psychoanalysis and Psychology

Theory: Psychosocial Development Theory

Summary of Theory

In Erikson's model of development, introduced during the 1950s, the formation of identity by an adolescent was key to the total development of the person into adulthood. It moves through a linear series of stages in which the person incorporates, or rejects attitudes, beliefs, and behaviors. These are learned from what Erikson called ''agents of socialization,'' also known as family, co-workers, school teachers, classmates, and more. The focus of this development is the eventual formation of a singular, coherent sense of self, accompanied by a confidence that one can maintain this inner oneness.


27. analysis about one of the theories of Psychological perspective of the self that the best describe psychological development. ​


Answer:

There are many theories within the psychological perspective of the self that describe psychological development. One such theory is Erik Erikson's theory of psychosocial development, which proposes that individuals go through a series of eight stages of development throughout their lifespan, with each stage characterized by a particular psychological conflict that must be resolved in order for healthy development to occur.

According to Erikson, the first stage, trust vs. mistrust, occurs during infancy and is characterized by the development of trust in others, particularly caregivers, as a result of consistent, reliable care. The second stage, autonomy vs. shame and doubt, occurs during early childhood and is characterized by the development of self-control and independence.

The third stage, initiative vs. guilt, occurs during play age and is characterized by the development of a sense of purpose and the ability to initiate and plan activities. The fourth stage, industry vs. inferiority, occurs during school age and is characterized by the development of competence and a sense of accomplishment.

The fifth stage, identity vs. role confusion, occurs during adolescence and is characterized by the development of a sense of self and the exploration of different roles and identities. The sixth stage, intimacy vs. isolation, occurs during young adulthood and is characterized by the development of close relationships with others.

The seventh stage, generativity vs. stagnation, occurs during middle adulthood and is characterized by the development of a sense of contribution to the world through work or other activities. The final stage, ego integrity vs. despair, occurs during late adulthood and is characterized by the development of a sense of acceptance of one's life and the ability to look back on it with a sense of satisfaction and accomplishment.

Erikson's theory emphasizes the importance of resolving these conflicts in a healthy way in order for individuals to develop a strong sense of self and to lead fulfilling lives.
