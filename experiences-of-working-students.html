9. lived experiences of working students in their academic standing/performancesa significant of the study & sino at anong benefits ang makukuha nila sa topic ​


Answer:

The lived experiences of working students in their academic standing/performance is a significant topic of study as it sheds light on the challenges and struggles faced by students who have to juggle work and school simultaneously. This study can help identify the factors that affect the academic performance of working students, such as time management, work-life balance, financial constraints, and mental and physical exhaustion. By understanding these factors, academic institutions and employers can work together to develop policies and programs that can help support working students in achieving their academic goals.

The benefits of this study can be varied, both for the students themselves and for the academic institutions and employers. For students, this study can provide insights into the experiences of their peers and can help them develop strategies for balancing work and school responsibilities. Additionally, the study can help academic institutions identify the areas where they can provide additional support to working students, such as offering flexible scheduling, financial aid, or academic counseling. Employers can also benefit from this study by understanding the impact that work can have on a student's academic performance and identifying ways to accommodate working students while still meeting business needs. Ultimately, this study can help create a more supportive and inclusive academic and work environment for all students, including those who work while studying.

Answer:

Explanation:Understand the Situation

Take some time to think about the situation you're facing. Try to describe your situation in a sentence or two. What's stressful about this situation for you right now? It can help to write down your thoughts.

For example: My family just moved, so I switched to a new school in the middle of the year. The stressful parts are not knowing anyone, missing my old friends, and dealing with all new schoolwork.

Notice and name the feelings you have about the situation. Accept your feelings — it's understandable to feel the way you feel, given your situation. It can help to write down your feelings, too.

For example: I feel lonely and sad because of missing old friends and my old school. I'm mad that we had to move, especially now. I'm worried about keeping up in math and social studies. I feel left out because I'm the new kid. I guess anyone would feel this way if they were in my situation.

Learn more. Learn all you can about the situation you're dealing with. This might include reading about it, talking to others, or finding out what others in your situation have done and what to expect. Learning helps you feel more confident and prepared — plus it reminds you that you're not the only one who has gone through this.
