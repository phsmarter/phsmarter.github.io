13. Can you give historical examples that show why hate speech is a problem?


Answer:

Yes, there are several historical examples that demonstrate the harm caused by hate speech. Here are a few:

Nazi propaganda: In Nazi Germany, the government used hate speech to dehumanize Jews and other minority groups, which helped lay the groundwork for the Holocaust. By portraying these groups as subhuman and dangerous, the Nazi regime was able to justify their persecution and eventual extermination.

Rwandan Genocide: In 1994, a radio station in Rwanda called Radio Télévision Libre des Mille Collines (RTLM) used hate speech to incite violence against Tutsis, a minority ethnic group. This hate speech played a significant role in the genocide that followed, which claimed the lives of an estimated 800,000 people.

Jim Crow laws: In the United States, laws that enforced segregation and discrimination against Black people were justified in part by racist propaganda and hate speech. This hate speech helped create a culture of fear and hatred towards Black people, which contributed to the violence and discrimination they faced.

Anti-Asian hate during COVID-19: During the COVID-19 pandemic, there has been an increase in hate crimes against Asian Americans. This increase has been linked in part to the use of hate speech by politicians and media outlets, who have used terms like "Chinese virus" and "Kung Flu" to describe the virus.

These historical examples demonstrate the dangerous and harmful effects of hate speech. Hate speech can create a culture of fear and hatred, contribute to violence and discrimination, and even lead to genocide.

20. what are the sexist hate speech


Answer:

Sexist hate speech is a form of violence against women and girls that perpetuates and exacerbates gender inequality. Both knowledge and policy action at all levels need to be developed to combat sexist hate speech.

Answer:

Sexist hate speech is a daily phenomenon for many women that is only beginning to be addressed. It requires both legal remedies and action to combat gender stereotypes.

Sexist hate speech relates to expressions which spread, incite, promote or justify hatred based on sex. Some groups of women are particularly targeted by sexist hate speech (notably young women, women in the media or women politicians), but every woman and girl is a potential target for online and offline sexist hate speech. The increasing availability and use of Internet and social platforms have contributed to growing occurrences of sexist hate speech. The Council of Europe has started to address this issue by looking at the potential use of existing standards and at policies on combating gender stereotypes.

Answer:

According to Britannica Hate speech, speech or expression that denigrates a person or persons on the basis of (alleged) membership in a social group identified by attributes such as race, ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation, religion, age, physical or mental disability, and others.

Typical hate speech involves epithets and slurs, statements that promote malicious stereotypes, and speech intended to incite hatred or violence against a group. Hate speech can also include nonverbal depictions and symbols. For example, the Nazi swastika, the Confederate Battle Flag (of the Confederate States of America), and pornography have all been considered hate speech by a variety of people and groups.
