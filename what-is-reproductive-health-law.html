Reproductive Health Law

The bill mandates the government to "promote, without biases, all effective natural and modern methods of family planning that are medically safe and legal." Although abortion is recognized as illegal and punishable by law, the bill states that “the government shall ensure that all women needing care for post-abortion complications shall be treated and counseled in a humane, non-judgmental and compassionate manner”.

The bill calls for a "multi-dimensional approach" integrates a component of family planning and responsible parenthood into all government anti-poverty programs. Age-appropriate reproductive health and sexuality education is required from grade five to fourth year high school using "life-skills and other approaches."

The bill also mandates the Department of Labor and Employment to guarantee the reproductive health rights of its female employees. Companies with fewer than 200 workers are required to enter into partnership with health care providers in their area for the delivery of reproductive health services. Employers with more than 200 employees shall provide reproductive health services to all employees in their own respective health facilities. Those with fewer than 200 workers shall enter into partnerships with health professionals for the delivery of reproductive health services. Employers shall inform employees of the availability of family planning. They are also obliged to monitor pregnant working employees among their workforce and ensure they are provided paid half-day prenatal medical leaves for each month of the pregnancy period that they are employed.

The national government and local governments will ensure the availability of reproductive health care services like family planning and prenatal care. Any person or public official who prohibits or restricts the delivery of legal and medically safe reproductive health care services will be meted penalty by imprisonment or a fine.

The Reproductive Health Law, also known as the Responsible Parenthood and Reproductive Health Act of 2012, is a law in the Philippines that aims to provide universal access to a wide range of family planning methods and information, as well as maternal and child health services. The law also requires that reproductive health education be included in the curriculum of elementary and high school students.

One of the main goals of the Reproductive Health Law is to reduce the incidence of unintended pregnancies and abortions, which can have negative health and social consequences, particularly for women and girls. The law aims to empower individuals, particularly women, to make informed decisions about their reproductive health and to have access to the services and information they need to do so.

Some of the provisions of the Reproductive Health Law that have been controversial include the inclusion of modern family planning methods, such as contraceptives, in the range of services covered by the law. Some people believe that the use of contraceptives is against their religious beliefs or goes against the natural order of things. Others argue that the use of contraceptives is a personal choice and that the government has a responsibility to provide information and access to a range of family planning options.

Another controversial provision of the Reproductive Health Law is the requirement for reproductive health education to be included in the curriculum of elementary and high school students. Some people argue that this is inappropriate or unnecessary, while others believe that it is important for young people to have accurate and comprehensive information about reproductive health so that they can make informed decisions about their own bodies and health.

In summary, the Reproductive Health Law aims to provide universal access to a range of family planning methods and information, as well as maternal and child health services, in order to improve the reproductive health and well-being of individuals in the Philippines. While the law has been controversial, many people believe that it is important for individuals to have access to accurate and comprehensive information about reproductive health and the ability to make informed decisions about their own reproductive health.

25. What is The Influence of the Catholic Church on the issue of Abortion and Reproductive Health Law? Please provide the sources of your research regarding the topic itself.​


Answer:

The Catholic Church has a long history of opposing abortion, as it believes that life begins at conception and that taking a human life is wrong. The Catholic Church's influence on the issue of abortion and reproductive health law is seen both in its vocal opposition to abortion and its active involvement in politics. In 2012, the Church openly urged Catholics to consider the Church’s position when electing political leaders, and in 2013, the Pope declared abortion “intrinsically evil” during a public mass.

In most Catholic countries, abortion is illegal except in cases of a medical emergency or risk to the health of the mother. Catholic Church leaders such as U.S. Knights of Columbus Supreme Knight Carl Anderson have cited Pope Francis’ statement in 2013 to argue against abortion access in the U.S. The Church also plays a key role in the push for laws limiting contraception access and has filed amicus briefs in related court cases.

In terms of the influence of the Church on reproductive health law, it is important to note that the Catholic Church has a long history of opposing contraception, sterilization, and in vitro fertilization, and its views on the issue are widely reflected in the laws of many Catholic countries. In a strongly Catholic society such as Mexico, abortion remains illegal except in very specific cases despite the fact that abortion has been decriminalized in other Latin American countries.
