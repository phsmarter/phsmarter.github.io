Help kids put it into words ‚Äî If your child is in the middle of an outburst, find out what's wrong. Remind your child to talk to you without whining, sulking, or yelling. Once your child calms down, ask what got him or her so upset. You might say, "Use your words to tell me what's wrong and what you're mad about." This helps your child put emotions into words and figure out what, if anything, needs to be done to solve the problem. However, don't push too hard for your child to talk right then. He or she may need some time to reflect before being ready to talk.

Listen and respond ‚Äî When your child puts the feelings into words, it's up to you to listen and say that you understand. If your child is struggling for words, offer some help: "so that made you angry," "you must have felt frustrated," or "that must have hurt your feelings." Make it clear that it‚Äôs OK to feel mad, but it‚Äôs not OK to react to that anger by yelling or hitting. "I know you're mad, but it's still not OK to hit." Creat clear ground rules and stick to them ‚Äî Talk about house rules regularly so your kids know what you expect of them. Be clear about what is and what is not acceptable without using threats, accusations, or putdowns.
