12. 2. Which do you think is the most challenging for a counselor?which do you think is the least? ​


The most difficult aspect of becoming a therapist is continually coming up against your shortcomings.

 

The frequently painful method of working through difficulties itself, the sluggish pace at which progress and recovery occur, the mental toll the job takes on a psychologist and counselors, and issues such as the abundance of paperwork and relatively low rates of pay are some of the most difficult aspects of working as a counselor.

 

When Things Take a Turn for the Worst Before They Turn for the Better

In most cases, wounds do not recover immediately. Medicines take time to work, treatments take time to strengthen muscle groups, and weakened tissues take time to regrow and heal. Treatment for mental health issues, as well as emotional distress incurred by traumas and traumatic life transitions, is similar. Setting clear goals for clients so they are aware of the ups and downs they can face through treatment may help people deal more with problems and make the experience less stressful for counselors.  

Progress at a Slow Pace

When clients actually make strides in therapy, it always happens in incremental steps and at a sluggish pace. Counselors can find this difficult, particularly if they are aware of the feelings, attitudes, and relationship dynamics that are holding their clients back, as well as what needs to be done to break these habits.  

Emotional Drainage

Compassion is another important trait of a successful counselor. A psychologist must be able to demonstrate sincere concern for the client in order to develop this bond and encourage the client to feel comfortable sharing even distressing emotions. However, knowing the weight of each client's dilemma may be draining mentally.

 

Counselors should cultivate a sense of humor, as well as an understanding of when and how to use it properly, for the good of both themselves and their customers.
