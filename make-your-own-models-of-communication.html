3. make your own "Model of Communication"


Answer:

Create your own? What would that even *mean*, considering that unless the speaker and listener share a model, communication becomes difficult? It’s one thing to select a model, and usually still workable if you make modifications to it (subverting tropes is a particular favorite of some advertising agencies). But the more impedance mismatch there is between your model and the listener’s model, the greater the danger of partial or complete failure of communication.

Explanation:

In other words, if your bank unilaterally decides to use mime and interpretive dance to explain its new fee structure, it’s almost certain to be the sort of train wreck that even Marcel Marceau wouldn’t be able to save. But if your bank asks whether you prefer interpretive dance, a smartphone app, or a webpage designed for laptops and desktops, that will probably increase the chances of them getting the information to you in the way you can best understand.

Answer:

Recognize that “your own communication model” isn’t very useful unless the person or people you’re communicating with is at least somewhat on board. Otherwise, there’s a very strong chance that the listeners start thinking “Is this a bad Powerpoint, or interpretive dance?” and not get what you’re doing.

Recognize that one communication model doesn’t work for all cases. The model I’d use for teaching in a classroom will be different than what I’d use while discusssing what to have for dinner.

13. If you are to make your own model of communication, what would it be? Please draw a draft of that model and explain the content specifically each element’s function.​


Answer:

Here's the elements of communication

1. Speaker- the source of information or message

2. Message- the information, ideas, or thoughts conveyed by the speaker in words

or in actions

3. Encoding-the process of converting the message into words, actions, or other

forms that the speaker understands

4. Channel- the medium or the means, such as personal or non-personal, verbal

or nonverbal, in which the encoded message is conveyed

5. Decoding-the process of interpreting the encoded message of the speaker by

the receiver

6. Receiver- the recipient of the message, or someone who decodes the message

7. Feedback-the reactions, responses, or information provided by the receiver

8. Context- the environment where communication takes place

9. Barrier- the factors that affect the flow of communication

My model of communication would consist of the following steps:

Encoding: The sender formulates a message and converts it into a form that can be transmitted, such as words, gestures, or symbols.

Transmission: The message is transmitted through a channel, such as speaking, writing, or broadcasting.

Decoding: The receiver interprets the message and tries to understand the intended meaning.
