1. What is the meaning of the minor and major characters? PASAGOT PO PLSSSSSS ​


Answer:

In literature, minor and major characters are terms used to describe the different roles that characters play in a story.

A major character, also known as a main character, is typically one of the central figures in the story. They are usually the focus of the plot, and the story revolves around their experiences, challenges, and growth. Major characters often have well-developed personalities, and their actions and decisions have a significant impact on the outcome of the story.

On the other hand, minor characters are those who play a supporting role in the story. They may appear in only a few scenes, and their actions may not have a significant impact on the main plot. Minor characters can serve a variety of functions, such as providing comic relief, serving as foils to the main characters, or helping to move the plot forward.

It's worth noting that the distinction between major and minor characters is not always clear-cut, and some characters may fall somewhere in between. Additionally, the importance of a character can also vary depending on the perspective of the reader or viewer.

4. other important minor character give name character traits important to story​


Answer:

Protagonist: The main character of the story is the protagonist. They should be carefully crafted with a logical backstory, personal motivation, and a character arc over the course of the story. Often the story will be told from their point of view. From a heroine like Katniss Everdeen in The Hunger Games to a miserable wretch like Doestoevsky’s Underground Man, the protagonist is the character the audience is most invested in.

Antagonist: The villain of the story is the antagonist. Think Lex Luthor, Lord Voldemort, or Dr. Charles Nichols in The Fugitive. Note that an antagonist is not the same as an anti-hero like the Joker or Walter White. Anti-heroes are villainous people who function in a protagonist’s role.

Love interest: The love interest is the protagonist’s object of desire. A good love interest will be compelling and three-dimensional, like Daisy Buchanan in The Great Gatsby or Rhett Butler in Gone With the Wind.

Confidant: This type of character is the best friend or sidekick of the protagonist, the Sancho Panza to their Don Quixote. Often the protagonist's goal flows through the confidant—although not every story needs one. A particularly famous confidant is Horatio in Shakespeare’s Hamlet, as is Hermione in Harry Potter.

Deuteragonists: These characters often overlap with confidants. A deuteragonist is close to the main character, but the story’s main plot does not directly correspond with their own character arc. Horatio doubles as a deuteragonist. In Tolkien’s Lord of the Rings trilogy, Samwise Gamgee is a deuteragonist. In Shakespeare’s Romeo and Juliet, Benvolio is a deuteragonist who doubles as a confidant, while Mercutio is a deuteragonist but not a confidant.

Tertiary characters: Tertiary characters populate the world of the story but do not necessarily link to the main storyline. These minor characters serve any number of functions and may have varying degrees of personal dynamism. Think of Padma and Parvati Patil in the Harry Potter series or Father Zosima in The Brothers Karamazov. In Spider-Man, various tertiary characters help fill out the world of Peter Parker’s New York.

Foil: A foil character primarily exists to bring the protagonist’s qualities into sharper relief. This is because the foil is effectively the opposite of the protagonist. In the Star Trek series, Captain Kirk and Mr. Spock serve as each other’s foils since their personalities are so notably different. Draco Malfoy is a foil to Harry Potter.

19. who is the minor characters in haji murad


Answer:

Shamil

Shamil was the tribal leader of the Caucasian Muslim warriors. He was very tall, powerful and charismatic. He holds Hadji Murad’s family and loved ones captives and threatens them so he can draw Hadji to where he is so that he can kill him.

Prince Mikhail Semenovich Vorontsov

This prince is one of the major commanders of the Russians. His wealth and connections enable him to be a great leader. He was vital to the plot because he is the only Russian commander that Hadji Murad listened to. Although Vorontsov does not fulfill Hadji Murad's every desire and command, he does let Hadji Murad move freely about Russia in search of his family.

Nicholas I of Russia

A very lethargic and bitter Tsar of Russia. He is very egotistical and mistreats women and those that are close to him, and is shown to be a poor decision maker and leader.

Petrukha Avdeyev

A young Russian soldier that died local military hospital after being shot outside the fortress He had no children and volunteered as a conscript in place of his brother who had a family of his own, thereby choosing the "harder life."

Khan Mohama

One of Hadji Murat's men, Khan Mohama is somewhat irreverent compared to the serious Murat. He is described as joyful, with a round face, black eyes, and a wide beaming smile. Khan Mohama lives recklessly, and would switch back and forth between the Russian and Caucasian sides if it served his sport.

Gomzalo

Gomzalo is a Chechen and another of Hadji Murat's men. He has a red beard and is blind in one eye, disfigured by a scar on his face. Gomzalo is a stricter Muslim than many other characters, and maintains a distrust of all Russians. He is a faithful believer in Shamil.
