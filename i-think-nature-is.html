Answer:

All life is dependent upon water. Water makes up 60 to 70 percent of all living matter and humans cannot live without drinking water for more than a week. The water cycle, or hydrologic cycle, distributes fresh water all over the earth's surface.

Process

The water cycle is made up of six stages. Evaporation is the process where water in its liquid state turns into a gas and rises into the atmosphere -- vapor. Condensation occurs when water vapor turns into tiny droplets of liquid -- clouds. Precipitation is the process where tiny condensed water droplets amalgamate and fall back to the earth in liquid form -- rain. Transpiration is the process where water is soaked up a plant's roots and evaporates out the leaves. Infiltration is the process where water soaks into the ground. Surface runoff occurs when gravity and solar heat transfer water around the earth's surface via rivers, streams, lakes, melting ice and the oceans.

1.Earth's water is always in movement, and the natural water cycle, also known as the hydrologic cycle, describes the continuous movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth. Water is always changing states between liquid, vapor, and ice, with these processes happening in the blink of an eye and over millions of years.

3.The water cycle is an extremely important process because it enables the availability of water for all living organisms and regulates weather patterns on our planet. If water didn’t naturally recycle itself, we would run out of clean water, which is essential to life.

Freshwater seems abundant, but when accounting for all the water on Earth, it's in limited supply. Just three percent of the water on our planet is freshwater. A majority of this water, about two percent of the world total, is contained in glaciers and ice sheets or stored below ground. The remaining one percent is found in lakes, rivers and wetland areas or transported through the atmosphere in the form of water vapor, clouds and precipitation. Rain and snowfall replenish freshwater sources, making it vital to know when, where and how much water is falling at any given time. Answer:

1.The pre-Socratic philosophers rejected myths and legends as bases of their beliefs because they believed that these stories were not based on reality. Instead, they believed that the natural world could be explained through reason and observation.

2.There are several key ways in which the philosophies of Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle differ with regard to the nature of man. Perhaps most notably, Socrates tended to emphasize the importance of ethics and morality, while Plato and Aristotle placed greater emphasis on the role of reason and knowledge. For Socrates, humans were primarily defined by their capacity for moral reasoning; for Plato, by their capacity for understanding eternal truths; and for Aristotle, by their capacity for rational thought more generally. Furthermore, Socrates tended to view the human soul as something immortal and separate from the body, while both Plato and Aristotle believed that the soul was mortal and intimately connected with the body. Finally, while Socrates believed that all people had the capacity for wisdom, Plato and Aristotle believed that wisdom was a rarer quality that only some people possessed.
