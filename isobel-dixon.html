Texting has become a popular and convenient form of communication among students in today's digital age. However, its effects on students' writing skills have been a topic of debate for educators, researchers, and the media. Some argue that texting has a negative impact on students' literacy, while others suggest that it may have positive effects depending on the context.

On the negative side, educators believe that texting encourages the use of textism, such as abbreviations and acronyms, which can harm students' writing skills. Verheijen (2015) reported that educators believe that texting negatively affects students' writing skills. This belief is supported by Powell and Dixon (2013) who found that some teachers have received work from students containing textism, which they consider to be evidence of the negative impact of texting on students' literacy.

On the other hand, some studies suggest that texting may have positive effects on literacy depending on the context. For instance, a study by Plester et al. (2008) found that the use of textism was positively related to spelling skills when used in a specific context, such as when texting with friends. Additionally, a study by Rosen et al. (2010) found that texting can improve reading skills when used in conjunction with other forms of communication.

Overall, the effects of texting on students' writing skills remain a topic of debate. While it is commonly assumed that texting has a negative impact on literacy, some studies suggest that it may also have positive effects depending on the context. As such, educators should consider the context in which texting is used and how it can be used to improve students' literacy skills, rather than simply dismissing it as a harmful practice.
