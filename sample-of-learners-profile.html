VICENTE LUGAGAY: He Received P300,000 In Prizes As 2018 Gawad Saka Winner In Fish Culture

Agri News

zbsarian January 14, 2019

VICENTE LUGAGAY HOLDING A TILAPIA BREEDER

A 53-year-old former ordinary farm worker in a fish farm quit his job and put up his own tilapia hatchery in Brgy. Rizal, Santiago City in Isabela. In 2018, he was the Gawad Saka winner in the Fish Culture Category and received P300,000 in prizes from the national and regional levels. Of course, that’s peanuts compared to his income from the sales of his tilapia fingerlings.

Vicente Lugagay produces 300,000 to 500,000 fingerlings a month which he sells not only in Isabela but also in other places in Nueva Vizcaya, Cagayan, Pampanga and the Cordillera Administrave Region.

Lugagay won his national and regional awards because of the practical and innovative technologies he has adopted in running his tilapia hatchery

CONTRIBUTIONS:

MORE STURDY FINGERLINGS – Lugagay also installed a special pump in his conditioning tanks instead of the conventional flow-through and aeration system. This allows for more sturdy fingerlings, thereby higher survival. Sex reversal is achieved in only four days instead of the usual 21 days.

Donnie bunting

Donnie Bunting started out in the dairy industry but decided to farm tilapia to stay ahead of the curve. Now, his family-owned business has expanded and produces tilapia, redclaw crayfish and Malayan prawns.

CONTRIBUTIONS:

The farm size is 150 hectares, with 40 hectares devoted to aquaculture. We grow tilapia along with redclaw crayfish and Malayan prawn in the same ponds. We also grow vegetables and raise sheep on the rest of the acreage.

Answer:

As an academic track student in the field of STEM/HUMSS, I have chosen to shop the issue of the digital divide in schools and communities. With the increasing reliance on technology in education, the digital divide has become a pressing issue that affects students, teachers, schools, and communities alike.

The digital divide refers to the gap between those who have access to digital technologies and those who do not. In today's digital age, access to technology and the internet has become a necessity for learning, as it provides opportunities for research, online collaboration, and access to educational resources. However, not all students, teachers, schools, and communities have equal access to technology, which can result in disparities in educational opportunities.

In schools, the digital divide can manifest in various ways. Some schools may lack the necessary infrastructure, such as reliable internet connectivity or updated computer equipment, to support digital learning. This can hinder students from accessing online resources or participating in virtual classrooms, putting them at a disadvantage compared to their peers in more technologically-equipped schools.

Teachers also face challenges related to the digital divide. Some teachers may not have the necessary digital literacy skills to effectively integrate technology into their teaching methods, which can impact their ability to deliver quality instruction and engage students in digital learning activities. Additionally, teachers may struggle with providing equitable opportunities for all students if some do not have access to the required technology outside of school hours.

Communities, especially those in rural or economically disadvantaged areas, may also face barriers to accessing technology. Limited internet connectivity, lack of affordable devices, and low digital literacy rates can hinder community members from accessing online educational resources, job opportunities, and other benefits of the digital world.

As an academic track student, I recognize the importance of addressing the digital divide to ensure equitable access to educational opportunities. To contribute to this issue, I plan to conduct research on the extent and impact of the digital divide in my local community and schools. I will also explore potential solutions, such as advocating for improved technology infrastructure in schools, promoting digital literacy programs for teachers and students, and advocating for policies that promote affordable access to technology in communities.

In conclusion, the digital divide is a critical issue that impacts students, teachers, schools, and communities in the field of STEM/HUMSS. Through research and advocacy, I aim to contribute to finding solutions to bridge the gap and ensure equitable access to technology and digital resources for all stakeholders in my respective field.
