2. Directions: Go back to the lectures and discussions of this module. Organize a list of your own DOs and DONTs in writing an essay​


Answer:

Informative Essay

●Do's and Don'Ts

•UNINTERESTING THESIS|Your thesis should be catchy and clearly states what new information the readers will get to know.

●UNSYSTEMATIC FACTS| Ideas should be presented in a clear and concise manner.

●TOO FAMILIAR TOPICS | Topics should be something the readers are not so formal.

● CONFUSING ,LONG TOPICS| Sentences must be short and accurate. Too much information and long discussion must be avoided.

●BIASED FACTS AND POINTLESS EXPLANATION | Informative essays are meant to inform not to convince .Personal views of the writer are not necessary.

PERSUASIVE ESSAY

Do's and Don'Ts

●NEGATIVE THESIS | Offensive statement about the writer's view is not interesting to the readers.

●NEGLECTED AUDIENCE | The reader must be included in your writings and make them think that you are all on the same.

●FALLACIES | Persuasive essays need facts - logical facts .Fallacies are illegitimate arguments or irrelevant points.They lack evidence that supports the claim.

● TOO FORMAL LANGUAGE | Words that affect directly the reader's emotions must be chosen sensibly.

●PREACHY TONE | Don't pull rank .Use softened words when persuading upwards .Be enthusiastic ,positive and likable.

Argumentative Essay

Do's and Don'ts

●ARGUMENT WITHOUT EVIDENCE | You need evidence .Feelings and opinions from life experiences may be used as evidence only from well-established in their field .

● LOST ON TRACK | Be sure to narrowly define what you are trying to say .Avoid vague ,broad and unrelated discussion.

● WEAK STATEMENTS | Use of " I believe" ,Or "I think" will weaken your claim.Add more convictions to your demand.

● UNAUTHORIZED OWNERSHIP | Don't own the words.Evidences look more credible if you use citations of the source.

● INFLATED PHRASES | Use your own voice and reduce wordiness

15. write about involvement and participation in lectures


Answer:

Class participation is an crucial aspect of student learning. This holds true that when students speak up in class, they learn to express their ideas in a way that others can understand. So far it is seen when they ask questions, they learn how to obtain information to enhance their own understanding of a topic.

Class participation also is a precious learning tool for teachers. Through students' questions, you learn what they don't understand, and can set your instruction accordingly.

Just as speaking in front of a group doesn't come easily to many adults, however, speaking up in class is a struggle for many students. That struggle might manifest itself in the classroom in a variety of ways -- not volunteering to reply questions, not asking for help, not speaking up in small-group activities, even not talking in class at all.

As a teacher, you will have greater success spurring a student to speak up if you can figure out why he is unwilling to participate. Whatever the reason for his reticence, your role is not to force him to speak; doing so will more likely make him clam up than open up. Your role is to create a supportive, encouraging climate that helps him feel more comfortable, more confident, and less fearful of speaking up.

X = 755

So the smallest number that could have attended both lectures is 755
