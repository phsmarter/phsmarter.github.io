In the Philippines, "bayanihan" refers to a tradition of community and cooperation where neighbors come together to help someone in need. This tradition is deeply embedded in Filipino culture and is often demonstrated during disasters, when people work together to rebuild communities and help those affected. Bayanihan is also reflected in everyday life, where people help each other with small things like shopping or sharing food. This Bayanihan spirit reflects the unified nature of Filipino society and is a source of pride for many Filipinos.

Another important aspect of Filipino culture is the celebration of festivals, or "fiestas." These festivals are usually held in honor of a patron saint and include parades, processions, street parties, and other forms of celebration. They are an opportunity for people to come together, celebrate their culture, and showcase their unique traditions and customs. Fiestas are an expression of the Filipinos' zest for life and joy and testify to their resilience and optimism, even in the face of adversity. The most famous of these festivals is the Ati-Atihan Festival in Aklan, known for its colorful costumes, lively music, and boisterous dancing.

When the United States ceased their Philippine colonization, they did not only leave the legacy of basketball, burgers, and, Hollywood movies. They left us a universal language, which is English.

Personally learning the language made it possible to read American classics, British tales, and, other kinds of foreign literature. Plus, it gave me confidence that I could easily communicate with other nationalities from online chatting to vacationing and studying abroad. In short, I feel English has provided me the opportunity to access a global buffet of ideas and potential foreign friendships.

On the other hand, it is highly valued to the point that it has become a medium of learning instruction and communication alongside Filipino. In addition to that, it created Taglish, the combination of Tagalog and English. I often hear this variation whenever I visit NCR, particularly, in the areas of Taguig and Makati. In these places, there are more well-off people, and, you can find international citizens in the locale.

Moreover, I noticed that there is a common connotation with speaking Taglish, which means a person is sosyal or associated with being rich and well-educated. This could equally mean that English fluency represents class and education. This is alarming yet no surprise for me. After all, the colonial mentality is strong in the Philippines.

However, despite the fact that English could threaten our Filipino pride, I believe that is key to achieving globalization because you cannot speak Tagalog in international diplomatic talks. You cannot woo investors with Cebuano or study abroad with only Hiligaynon in mind. You cannot order food in Ilocano when you're vacationing in France. In other words, we can vocally make our presence worldwide.

Nevertheless, as we speak in American slang, we must not forget to keep the Filipino pride burning through our dialects and traditions.
