Answer:

To create essays and theses about human experiences related to discrimination, gender, poverty, and economy in applied social science, it is essential to follow a structured approach. Here are some steps that can be helpful:

1. Identify the research topic: Choose a specific topic related to the subject matter. For example, the impact of gender discrimination on women's economic empowerment.

2. Conduct a literature review: Review academic sources and research studies related to the topic. This will provide a solid understanding of the current state of research, gaps, and areas that need further investigation.

3. Formulate research questions: Based on the literature review, formulate research questions that address the gaps in knowledge and contribute to existing research.

4. Develop a methodology: Select an appropriate research methodology and design the study. This will include selecting participants, data collection methods, and data analysis techniques.

5. Collect and analyze data: Collect data from participants using the chosen data collection method. Analyze the data using appropriate statistical techniques.

6. Interpret the findings: Interpret the results of the study in light of the research questions and literature review. Explain the significance of the findings and how they contribute to the existing body of knowledge.

7. Conclusion and recommendations: Conclude the essay or thesis by summarizing the main findings, discussing the implications of the study, and making recommendations for further research or policy changes.

When writing about human experiences related to discrimination, gender, poverty, and economy, it is important to approach the topic with sensitivity and empathy. It is also essential to maintain academic rigor and use credible sources to support arguments and findings.

Answer:

1. Primary socialization in sociology is the period early in a person's life during which they initially learn and build themselves through experiences and interactions around them. ... Several agents of primary socialization involve institutions such as the family, childhood friends, the educational system, and social media.

2. Secondary socialization refers to the process of learning what is the appropriate behavior as a member of a smaller group within the larger society. ... Such examples of secondary socialization are entering a new profession or relocating to a new environment or society.

3. Adult socialization is the process in adulthood of learning the practices and expectations associated with a social role or social circumstances; it contrasts with childhood socialization. Adult socialization explains how adults adjust to new circumstances and new roles, learning to meet related expectations.

4. Anticipatory socialization is the process, facilitated by social interactions, in which non-group-members learn to take on the values and standards of groups that they aspire to join, so as to ease their entry into the group and help them interact competently once they have been accepted by it.

5. Resocialization or resocialisation (British English) is the process by which one's sense of social values, beliefs, and norms are re-engineered. The process is deliberately carried out in -parent households and military boot camps through an intense social process that may take place in a total institution.

Answer:

physical self-

Activities you do that improve the well-being of your physical health.

Examples: Taking a walk during lunch breaks, sleeping eight hours a day, staying hydrated, etc.

mental self-

Any activity the stimulates your mind or your intellect.

Examples: Reading a book, solving a puzzle, playing chess, going to a museum, etc.

emotional self-

Activities that help you connect, process, and reflect on a full range of emotions.

Examples: Seeing a therapist, writing in a journal, creating art, playing music etc.

social self-

Activities that nurtures and deepens the relationships with people in your life.

Examples: Brunch with friends, going on a date, making time to call your mom regularly, etc.

Ethical egoism -is the normative ethical position that moral agents ought to act in their own self-interest. It differs from psychological egoism, which claims that people can only act in their self-interest.
