Recently, I have been reflecting on my own experiences with kinship and how they have been shaped by the various social cultures I have encountered throughout my life.

Growing up in a Filipino family, kinship was a fundamental aspect of my upbringing. I was taught to show respect to my elders and to always prioritize the needs of my family over my own. This included extended family members such as aunts, uncles, and cousins, who were often considered as close as immediate family members. This is a stark contrast to the collectivist culture that I grew up in, where the needs of the group always come first.

These different cultural values have had a profound impact on the way I view kinship and family. On one hand, I value the closeness and support that comes with a collectivist culture, as it has instilled in me a strong sense of community and connection with my relatives. On the other hand, I also appreciate the independence and self-reliance that comes with individualist cultures, as it has allowed me to pursue my own goals and aspirations.

In my opinion, the way that kinship is conceptualized and practiced reflects broader social and cultural values. For example, a culture that values individualism may prioritize personal achievement and independence, whereas a culture that values collectivism may prioritize the needs of the group and the maintenance of social harmony.

In conclusion, my reflections on kinship have shown me the importance of understanding and appreciating different social cultures. By recognizing the diverse ways in which people conceptualize and practice kinship, we can gain a deeper understanding of ourselves and the world around us. Ultimately, this can help us to build stronger and more inclusive communities that are grounded in mutual respect and understanding.

In recent years, the rise of social media has led to a significant increase in the depth and width of this freedom. Social media platforms have given individuals the ability to express themselves in new and innovative ways, and to reach a global audience. However, with this freedom comes the responsibility to use it in a proper way.

The depth of freedom of expression on social media refers to the variety of ways in which individuals can express themselves. Social media platforms allow users to share text, images, videos, and audio, which gives them a vast array of options to express themselves. Users can also create and join groups and communities, which allows them to connect with others who share their interests and perspectives. This depth of freedom allows individuals to express themselves in a unique and personal way, and to connect with others who share their views.

The width of freedom of expression on social media refers to the global reach of social media platforms. Social media platforms connect people from all over the world, and allow individuals to express themselves to a global audience. This has led to the creation of global movements and campaigns, which would have been difficult to organize and mobilize without social media. The width of freedom of expression also allows for the spread of information and ideas, which can lead to greater understanding and acceptance of different cultures and perspectives.

However, with the increase in freedom of expression on social media, there is also a responsibility to use it in a proper way. The anonymity and accessibility of social media can lead to the spread of hate speech and misinformation. Social media platforms have a responsibility to ensure that their users are using the platform in a proper way, and that they are not spreading hate speech or misinformation. Additionally, individuals have a responsibility to use social media in a responsible and respectful manner, and to be aware of the potential impact of their words and actions on others.

In conclusion, the depth and width of freedom of expression on social media is a double-edged sword. Socialization affects us in so many ways far beyond what we see,  including self-image. Our socialization patterns shape our state of mind. Your self-image is a conceptual picture of yourself, both as a physical body and an individual. A healthy body image means that you know who you are, feel confident in your skin, and understand your full potential.

Elements and Dimensions of Self-Image

These are the proposed elements and dimensions of self-image (Oltmann, 2014):

The three elements of a person’s self-image are:

The way a person perceives oneself.

The way a person interprets another person’s perceptions of him or her.

The perception of the ideal self.

The six dimensions of a person’s self-image are:

Physical dimension

Psychological dimension

Intellectual dimension

Skills dimension

Moral dimension

Sexual dimension  

Examples of Positive and Negative Self-Image

A positive self-image is having a good judgment of yourself; for example:

Seeing yourself as a good-looking and desirable person.

Having an image of yourself as a smart and intellectual person.

Seeing a joyful, fit, and healthy person when you look in the mirror.

Believing that you are at best to your ideal self.

Thinking that others recognize you as a person as well as yourself.

On the other hand, negative self-image is the reverse of the above; it looks like:

Seeing yourself as unappealing and undesirable.

Having an image of yourself having below-average intelligence.

Seeing an unhappy and pale-looking when you look in the mirror.

Believing that you are not your ideal version of yourself.

Thinking that others do not recognize you as a person as well as yourself.
