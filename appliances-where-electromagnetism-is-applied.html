Electromagnetic waves have many practical applications in appliances, particularly in the fields of communication and energy transfer

Examples:

Radio and Television: Radio and television waves use electromagnetic radiation to transmit audio and video signals through the air. Radio waves are used for audio transmission, while television waves use a higher frequency to transmit both audio and video.

Microwave ovens: Microwaves use a high-frequency electromagnetic field to heat up food. The microwaves are absorbed by the water, fat, and sugar molecules in the food, causing them to vibrate and generate heat.

Wireless charging: Many appliances, such as smartphones and electric toothbrushes, can now be charged wirelessly using electromagnetic induction. The charging pad generates an electromagnetic field, which is picked up by a coil in the appliance and converted into electrical energy.

Wi-Fi: Wi-Fi networks use electromagnetic waves to transmit data wirelessly between devices. The router generates a radio wave signal, which is picked up by the devices and converted into data.

Bluetooth: Bluetooth technology uses short-range radio waves to connect devices wirelessly. The devices emit electromagnetic waves, which are received by the other device and used to transfer data.

Explanation on how electromagnetism is applied in a refrigerator:
The refrigerator uses the principles of electromagnetism in its cooling process. A refrigerator contains a compressor, which is powered by an electric motor that uses electromagnetism to create a magnetic field. This magnetic field causes the compressor to compress the refrigerant gas, which then becomes a high-pressure, high-temperature gas.

The high-pressure gas then flows through a set of coils called the condenser, where it releases its heat to the surrounding environment. The refrigerant then turns into a liquid, which flows through an expansion valve, causing it to expand and cool down. The cool refrigerant then flows through another set of coils called the evaporator, where it absorbs heat from the inside of the refrigerator and cools down the air inside.

This process of compressing, cooling, and expanding the refrigerant gas is repeated in a continuous cycle to maintain a cool temperature inside the refrigerator. Thus, electromagnetism is applied in the electric motor that powers the compressor, making it an essential part of the refrigeration process.
