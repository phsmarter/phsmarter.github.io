6. Type of mutation: Point or Frameshift (deletion or insertion)How did the mutation affect amino acid sequence (protein)?​


Answer:

Point Mutation:

A point mutation is a change in a single nucleotide base pair in DNA. The effect of point mutation on the amino acid sequence depends on the location of the mutation within the gene and the specific codon affected.

For example, a point mutation that changes a single base in a codon may result in a different amino acid being incorporated into the protein chain. This type of mutation is known as a missense mutation. Alternatively, the point mutation may not result in a change in the amino acid sequence if the new codon still codes for the same amino acid. This is known as a silent mutation.

Frameshift Mutation:

A frameshift mutation occurs when one or more nucleotides are either inserted or deleted from the DNA sequence, causing a shift in the reading frame. This shift changes the way the mRNA is read during translation, leading to a completely different amino acid sequence in the resulting protein.

For example, if a single nucleotide is deleted from a coding sequence, it will shift the reading frame of the gene, altering the entire sequence of amino acids that follow the site of the deletion. This type of mutation is called a frameshift deletion. Similarly, if a single nucleotide is added to the coding sequence, it will also shift the reading frame and result in a different amino acid sequence. This type of mutation is called a frameshift insertion.

Overall, the effect of a mutation on the amino acid sequence of a protein depends on the type and location of the mutation within the gene. A point mutation can result in a different amino acid being incorporated into the protein chain, whereas a frameshift mutation alters the entire sequence of amino acids following the site of the mutation.

How are these chromosomal changes affect the structure & functions of proteins? ​

Chromosomal changes can have various effects on the structure and function of proteins, depending on the nature and extent of the change. Here are a few examples:

Deletions: When a portion of a chromosome is deleted, it can result in the loss of one or more genes that code for proteins. This can lead to a reduced or absent production of the corresponding proteins, which can affect the normal functioning of cells and tissues. For example, a deletion in the gene that codes for the protein dystrophin can cause Duchenne muscular dystrophy.

Duplications: When a segment of a chromosome is duplicated, it can result in the presence of extra copies of one or more genes. This can lead to an overproduction of the corresponding proteins, which can also affect normal cellular functions. For example, duplications in the gene that codes for the protein alpha-synuclein have been implicated in the development of Parkinson's disease.

Translocations: When parts of two chromosomes exchange places, it can lead to the fusion of two genes that are not normally together. This can result in the formation of a fusion protein that has altered structure and function, which can contribute to the development of cancer. For example, the BCR-ABL fusion protein, which is produced by a translocation between chromosomes 9 and 22, is found in the majority of cases of chronic myelogenous leukemia.

Point mutations: These are changes in a single nucleotide of a gene, which can result in the substitution of one amino acid in the corresponding protein. Depending on the location of the mutation, this can affect the structure and function of the protein in various ways. For example, a point mutation in the gene that codes for the protein hemoglobin can result in the formation of an abnormal hemoglobin molecule, leading to sickle cell anemia.

Overall, chromosomal changes can have significant impacts on the structure and function of proteins, which can affect various cellular processes and contribute to the development of genetic disorders and diseases.
