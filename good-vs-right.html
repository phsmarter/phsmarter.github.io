Answer:

Sure, here are the meanings of the 5 elements of plot and the 6 types of story conflict:

5 Elements of Plot:

1. Exposition - This is the introduction of the story where the characters, setting, and initial conflict are introduced. It sets the stage for the story and provides important background information.

2. Rising Action - This is the part of the story where the conflict starts to develop and the tension begins to rise. It includes a series of events that lead up to the climax.

3. Climax - This is the turning point of the story and the most intense part. It is the moment of greatest tension, where the main character faces the biggest challenge.

4. Falling Action - This is the part of the story where the tension starts to decrease, and the events begin to wind down. It leads to the resolution of the conflict.

5. Conclusion - This is the final part of the story where the loose ends are tied up, and the story comes to a close. It provides closure to the story and gives the reader a sense of satisfaction.

6 Types of Story Conflict:

1. Character vs. Supernatural - This is a conflict where the character is pitted against supernatural forces like ghosts, monsters, or other paranormal entities.

2. Character vs. Technology - This is a conflict where the character faces challenges with technology or machines. It could be a malfunctioning computer system, a deadly robot, or some other technological issue.

3. Character vs. Society - This is a conflict where the character is pitted against the norms, beliefs, or laws of society. It could be an individual struggling to fit into society or challenging its rules and expectations.

4. Character vs. Nature - This is a conflict where the character faces challenges from the natural world. It could be a natural disaster, a wild animal, or extreme weather conditions.

5. Character vs. Self - This is a conflict where the character struggles with their own beliefs, emotions, or desires. It could be a battle with addiction, a crisis of conscience, or an internal struggle to overcome fears or insecurities.

6. Character vs. Character - This is a conflict where the character is pitted against another character. It could be a physical fight or a battle of wits between two characters with opposing goals or values.

11. What does the distance vs time graph on the right represents


Answer:

The slope of a distance vs time graph represents the average velocity of an object.

Explanation:

If an object moves along a straight line, the distance traveled can be represented by a distance-time graph. In a distance-time graph, the gradient of the line is equal to the speed of the object. The greater the gradient (and the steeper the line) the faster the object is moving.

22. RP1 ACT2B COMPARING AND CONTRASTING EXPRESSIONS. Without going back to the lessons in this module, compare and contrast each given pair of expression. Words in Pairs Comparison Contrast 1. Research vs. Report Writing 2. Author-date system Humanities VS. system 3. MLA vs. APA 4. One-line quotation vs. Five- line quotation 5. Conclusion vs. Recommendation Left-Right 6. margin Top-Bottom margin 7. Abstract vs. Introduction 8. language Literary Language VS. Academic VS. 9. Passive voice vs. Active voice 10. Research report VS. Literature report​


Answer:

1.) Research vs. Report Writing

Comparison: Both research and report writing involve gathering and presenting information.

Contrast: Research is the process of investigating a topic or problem, while report writing is the act of presenting the findings and conclusions of the research.

2.) Author-date system vs. Humanities VS. system

Comparison: Both systems are used to cite sources in academic writing.

Contrast: The Author-date system is commonly used in the social sciences and sciences, while the Humanities VS. system is commonly used in the humanities.

3.) MLA vs. APA

Comparison: Both are citation styles used in academic writing.

Contrast: MLA is primarily used in the humanities, while APA is primarily used in the social sciences.

4.) One-line quotation vs. Five-line quotation

Comparison: Both types of quotations are used to incorporate the ideas of others into one's own writing.

Contrast: One-line quotations are shorter and are generally used to support smaller points, while five-line quotations are longer and are generally used to support larger points.

5.) Conclusion vs. Recommendation

Comparison: Both conclusion and recommendation provide an end to the discussion and offer a course of action.

Contrast: Conclusions are drawn from the results of a study, while recommendations are suggestions for future actions.

6.) Margin Top-Bottom margin

Comparison: Both margins serve a similar purpose, to provide space between the text and the edge of the page.

Contrast: The top and bottom margins determine the distance between the text and the page edges at the top and bottom of the page, respectively.

7.) Abstract vs. Introduction

Comparison: Both abstracts and introductions provide an overview of the paper.

Contrast: Abstracts are brief summaries of the paper's main points, while introductions provide background information, context, and thesis statements.

8.) Literary Language VS. Academic Language

Comparison: Both types of language are used in writing.

Contrast: Literary language is used in literature and creative writing, while academic language is used in scholarly research and writing.

9.) Passive voice vs. Active voice

Comparison: Both passive and active voice are grammatical constructions used to convey meaning in writing.

Contrast: Passive voice emphasizes the object or recipient of the action, while active voice emphasizes the subject or doer of the action.

10.) Research report VS. Literature report

Comparison: Both types of reports present information on a topic.

Contrast: Research reports present findings from research studies, while literature reports analyze existing literature on a topic.
