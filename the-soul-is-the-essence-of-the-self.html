9. Self is the soul and immortal agree or disagree


Answer:

agree

Explanation:

As a supposed student of Socrates, Plato agreed that the soul is immortal and separate from the body. However, he upped the ante a bit. He believed the soul was eternal. According to Plato, the soul doesn't come into existence with the body; it exists prior to being joined to the body.

Sounding a whole bunch like reincarnation, Plato believed the soul exists within a body until that body dies. It then sets up house in another body. For this reason, Plato called the body the prison of the soul.

Augustine also believed the soul to be immortal. However, he believed the soul AND the body make up a human. The body isn't just a prison for a soul that jumps from body to body. Instead, one body and one soul make up one person. Yes, he agreed that the soul is immortal; he just didn't buy into the idea that it hops from body to body.

30. 2. Discuss the essence of Self according to Medieval Philosophe​


Answer:

The concept of self in medieval philosophy was highly influenced by Christian theology and the works of Aristotle. According to medieval philosophers, the essence of self was closely tied to the concept of the soul.

Medieval philosophers believed that the soul was the essence of self, and that it was immortal and unchanging. They also believed that the soul was responsible for the individual's consciousness, personality, and moral character.

The concept of the soul was closely tied to religious beliefs, and medieval philosophers believed that it was created directly by God. They saw the soul as the link between the individual and God, and believed that it was the source of all spiritual and moral values.

Medieval philosophers also believed that the self was distinct from the body, and that the body was simply a vessel for the soul. They believed that the body was temporary and mortal, while the soul was eternal and divine.

Overall, the essence of self in medieval philosophy was closely tied to the concept of the soul, and was seen as a divine and immortal entity that was responsible for the individual's consciousness, personality, and moral character.
