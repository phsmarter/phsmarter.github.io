14. 5 important functions of mangrove ecosystem


Protection from natural disasters: Mangrove forests act as natural barriers to protect coastal areas from strong winds, tidal surges, and storms. The dense root systems of mangroves help to stabilize coastal sediment and prevent erosion.

Biodiversity: Mangrove forests provide habitat for a wide range of animal and plant species, including many migratory bird species. They serve as nurseries for many fish and other aquatic species, helping to maintain marine biodiversity.

Carbon storage: Mangrove forests are highly effective in storing carbon, with estimates suggesting that they can store up to 10 times more carbon per hectare than other tropical forests. This makes them important in mitigating climate change.

Water filtration: The dense root systems of mangroves trap sediment and pollutants, improving water quality and clarity. They also help to reduce nutrient levels in the water, which can cause algal blooms and other water quality issues.

Economic value: Mangrove forests provide a range of economic benefits to local communities, including timber, fish, and other natural resources. They also support tourism and recreation, and have cultural and spiritual significance for many communities.

Why Mangroves Matter

Mangrove forests were once generally dismissed as swampy wastelands. Planners, scientists, and coastal dwellers have now come to value them as the remarkably diverse and important ecosystems they are. Mangroves, seagrass beds, and coral reefs work as a single system that keeps coastal zones healthy. Mangroves provide essential habitat for thousands of species. They also stabilize shorelines, preventing erosion and protecting the land — and the people who live there — from waves and storms.
