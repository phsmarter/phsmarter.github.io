23. . explain the justification for the knowledge of bioethics in sts at the educational and societal levels in the philippines.


"Bioethics"

The multidisciplinary study and solution to these moral and ethical issues is known as bioethics. Many times, overlapping issues from other academic disciplines, such as the biological sciences, biotechnology, public health, medicine, public policy, law, philosophy, and theology, are involved in bioethical challenges.

Bioethics in Educational Level

The greatest way to educate decision-making based on ethics and human values, which is essential for the development of a nation and even the world, is through the study of bioethics, which is also regarded as a learning framework. Transformative learning is the core goal of higher education.

To encourage more possibilities for contemplation and discussion, to encourage critical thinking, and to strengthen medical students' decision-making skills, bioethics education is crucial. To comprehend this topic better, more study may be helpful.

Bioethics in Societal Level

To put it another way, while law and morality evaluate novel events, bioethics investigates them to determine the ethical issues they raise, to weigh the benefits and risks associated with them, and to suggest solutions, which may include changing the norms, to maximize society's chance of surviving.

The predominately Catholic mindset of the Filipino populace has greatly influenced the primary issues of bioethics discourse. This has been demonstrated through public discussions on abortion, euthanasia, the use of contraceptives, and other reproductive health issues.

26. although reading education in the philippines aims to develop functional/practical literacy in learners,what level of literacy is being developed when classroom practices focus more on memorization rather than on understanding and application?​


As principal of the San Pascual Elementary School in Ubay, Philippines, Maria Jeana Polinar is committed to creating an environment where every child learns to read. Classrooms at her school have cozy reading corners stocked with books. Students learn to read and write in Visayan, their mother tongue, as well as Filipino and English, and there are remedial reading and writing classes for students who need extra help.

Polinar has even instituted a “zero non-reader” policy in her school to help teachers stay laser-focused on literacy instruction. “Reading is the foundation of learning,” she says. “There is no learning at all if a child can’t read and comprehend.”

Polinar’s approach is a remarkable departure from how literacy used to be taught in the Philippines. For decades, teachers taught exclusively in Filipino and English—even though most children spoke one of the Philippines’ 19 major local languages at home. But this changed in 2012 when the Philippines government passed a wide-ranging education reform bill that, among many changes, adopted mother tongue instruction for kindergarten through grade 3.

Implementing this new policy has been a huge undertaking for the Philippines Department of Education (DepEd)—but its partnership with EDC through the USAID-funded Basa Pilipinas program has delivered noteworthy results. Since 2013, Basa Pilipinas has improved literacy instruction for 1.8 million students in over 3,000 schools. DepEd is now preparing to roll out Basa’s approach and materials to every primary school in the country.
