The Garden of Death (Finnish: Kuoleman puutarha; 1896) is a painting by Finnish symbolist painter "Hugo Simberg". Like many of Simberg's paintings, it depicts a gloomy, otherworldly scene. The central figures are reminiscent of the classic black-clad Grim Reaper, but paradoxically are tending to gardens; traditionally symbols of birth or renewal. According to his description, the garden is situated "far away beyond the pine woods," and has "soft brown earth" and long grass. The Garden of Death is silent, except for the sound of the nightingale which sings its song all through the night. Simberg believed the garden of death to be a place where souls go before entering heaven. He depicts human souls as plants, almost as if man is as undeveloped compared to his paradisical self as a child is compared to an adult.

10. EXPLAIN THE PHENOMENA OF FESTIVAL USING THE STRUCTURAL FUNCTIONALISM, INTERPRETIVE AND CRITICAL PERSPECTIVE OF SOCIOLOGY. ​.


Answer:

Structural Functionalism: Festivals are an important part of social cohesion, as they bring people together to celebrate a common culture and values. They provide a sense of shared identity, a sense of belonging, and contribute to social stability. The various activities that take place during a festival encourage friendship and mutual understanding, strengthen trust and create a sense of unity.

Interpretive Perspective: Festivals are a way for people to express themselves and their values. They can be used as a means of shedding traditional status hierarchies and embracing creativity, new ideas, and cultural exchange. The performance, display, and discussion of various art forms can provide a way to challenge existing norms and form new collective understandings.

Critical Perspective: Festivals can act as a form of societal control by reinforcing dominant values and suppressing minority perspectives. They can be used to celebrate a certain power structure or a particular version of history as well as to discredit cultural practices seen as inappropriate or politically unpopular. These events can be manipulated and used as a tool by the powerful to maintain their own interests.

18. Think of something that best symbolizes life. Through an essay, explain the symbolism in a philosophical perspective.​


Answer:

Depending on whom one asks, the question, “What is the meaning of life?” is either the most profound question of human existence or else nothing more than a nonsensical request built on conceptual confusion, much like, “What does the color red taste like?” or “What is heavier than the heaviest object?” Ask a non-philosopher, “What do philosophers discuss?” and a likely answer will be, “The meaning of life.” Ask the same question of a philosopher within the analytic tradition, and you will rarely get this answer. The sources of suspicion about the question within analytic philosophy, especially in earlier periods, are varied. First, the question of life’s meaning is conceptually challenging because of terms like “the” “meaning” and “life,” and especially given the grammatical form in which they are arranged. Second, it is often asked with transcendent, spiritual, or religious assumptions at the fore about what the world “should” be like in order for there to be a meaning of life. In so far as the question is entangled with such ideas, the worry is that even if the concept of a meaning of life is coherent, there likely is not one.

20. is history a interpretative; it invites students to debate multiple perspectives, offer their opinions and educated interpretations, and challenge existing beliefs?​


Answer:

Yes, history is interpretive in nature. History is not simply a collection of facts or events, but rather an interpretation of those events through the lens of the historian's perspective and the available evidence.

Explanation:

Historians examine primary sources, such as documents, letters, and artifacts, to construct a narrative of the past. However, even with access to the same primary sources, historians may interpret events differently based on their own biases, values, and cultural perspectives.

As a result, history invites students to engage in debate and offer their opinions and interpretations based on the available evidence. History is not a static discipline, but rather a dynamic process of inquiry that is constantly being challenged and revised based on new evidence and perspectives.

Challenging existing beliefs and interpretations is an essential part of historical inquiry, as it allows for a more complete understanding of the past and its relevance to the present.


21. Short essay: In 5 sentences, provide a brief analysis of the Philippine education system using the three major perspectives in education. 1. Functionalist Perspective2.cnflict perspective3.symbolic interactionist perspective​


Answer:

The functionalist perspective sees the Philippine education system as a means to prepare individuals for the workforce and contribute to the economy. The conflict perspective views the unequal access to quality education among different socioeconomic classes as a perpetuation of social inequality. The symbolic interactionist perspective highlights the importance of teacher-student relationships and how it shapes the students' experiences and outcomes in education. Overall, the Philippine education system faces challenges such as underfunding, teacher shortages, and outdated curricula, which affect the quality of education and hinder its effectiveness in producing well-rounded individuals who can contribute to society.

EXPLANATION:

The theoretical framework is a description or plan which contains an explanation of all the things that are used as research material based on the results of the research carried out. The theoretical framework usually contains the relationship between a variable and another variable, usually there is a cause and effect of two or more than two variables. From this understanding, it can be concluded that a good theoretical framework is made or compiled before carrying out the scientific research process. Can be made in the form of schematics or diagrams, because the goal is to make it easier for the author to understand all the variables that form the basis for the emergence of a scientific work.

The theoretical framework can also be interpreted as a form of raw conclusions from problems whose topics have been determined in advance. Then it becomes a guideline or benchmark for writers in compiling scientific papers so that when compiling scientific works, the authors do not carry out useless discussions or get out of the main discussion topic. In addition, the theoretical framework is also incarnated as a guide for readers in understanding the contents of a scientific work. The goal is simple, namely so that the reader does not make mistakes when reading and understanding the contents of this scientific work. Not only makes it easier for writers when making this scientific work but also helps readers to be able to understand it.

Functionalists view society as a system in which all parts work—or function—together to create society as a whole. In this way, societies need culture to exist. Cultural norms function to support the fluid operation and continued stability of society, and cultural values guide people in making choices.
