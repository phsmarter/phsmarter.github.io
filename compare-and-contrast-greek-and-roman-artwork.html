29. 1. Compare and contrast Greek and Roman artworks. 2. Discuss how Mannerism art became an offshoot of works from the Renaissance. 3. Explain why Neoclassicism served as a response or reaction to the Baroque Period. 4. Give examples of artworks for each movement: Art Nouveau, Fauvism, Cubism, and Futurism. Describe them. #answerpls!!​


Answer:

1. Greek and Roman artworks share some similarities but also have distinct differences. Both styles show an interest in realism and the human form. However, Greek art tends to be more idealized and focused on the beauty of the human form, while Roman art is more realistic and practical. Greek sculptures often depicted gods, goddesses, and athletes, while Roman sculptures focused on historical figures and military leaders. In terms of architecture, Greek buildings were characterized by their symmetry and simplicity, while Roman buildings were grander in scale and often included arches and vaults.

2. Mannerism was an offshoot of the Renaissance that emerged in the late 16th century. It was characterized by a departure from the naturalism and harmony of the Renaissance and a focus on exaggerated poses, elongated forms, and complex compositions. Mannerist artists were interested in expressing emotion and creating dramatic effects, often using unnatural colors and lighting. This style was a reaction to the classical ideals of the Renaissance and was influenced by the religious and political unrest of the time.

3. Neoclassicism emerged in the 18th century as a reaction to the excesses of the Baroque period. Neoclassical artists were interested in reviving the classical styles of ancient Greece and Rome, which they saw as embodying reason, order, and rationality. This movement was characterized by a return to classical forms and motifs, such as columns, pediments, and friezes. Neoclassical art was often used to express political and social ideals, and it was favored by the Enlightenment thinkers of the time.

4. Art Nouveau was a decorative art movement that emerged in the late 19th century and was characterized by flowing, organic forms inspired by nature. Examples of Art Nouveau artworks include the glass designs of Emile Galle and the posters of Alphonse Mucha. Fauvism was a movement characterized by bold colors and loose, expressive brushstrokes. Examples of Fauvist artworks include Henri Matisse's "Woman with a Hat" and Andre Derain's "The Dance". Cubism was an art movement that emerged in the early 20th century and was characterized by the fragmentation and reassembling of forms. Examples of Cubist artworks include Pablo Picasso's "Les Demoiselles d'Avignon" and Georges Braque's "Violin and Candlestick". Futurism was an art movement that emerged in Italy in the early 20th century and was characterized by a celebration of technology and the modern world. Examples of Futurist artworks include Umberto Boccioni's "Unique Forms of Continuity in Space" and Giacomo Balla's "Dynamism of a Dog on a Leash".

1. Greek and Roman artworks both have a significant influence on Western art, but they also have distinct differences. Greek art emphasizes idealized human forms, balance, and harmony in their sculptures and pottery, with a focus on portraying the beauty of the human body. On the other hand, Roman art is characterized by its realism, practicality, and grandeur in architecture, sculpture, and painting. They were also known for using more diverse materials like bronze and glass, as well as incorporating realistic portraits and historical events into their artwork.

2. Mannerism art was an offshoot of works from the Renaissance because it was a reaction to the perfection and naturalism of Renaissance art. Mannerist painters and sculptors emphasized technical skill and virtuosity over realism, often deliberately distorting or exaggerating proportions or perspective for expressive effect. The style was marked by its complex compositions, unusual color schemes, and exaggerated poses. Mannerism developed as artists sought new ways to express themselves and break away from the strict rules of the Renaissance.

3. Neoclassicism served as a response to the Baroque Period because it was a return to the classical ideals of ancient Greece and Rome, which were seen as pure and rational compared to the excesses of the Baroque. Neoclassical artists focused on simplicity, clarity, and order, with an emphasis on geometric shapes, clean lines, and balanced compositions. They drew inspiration from classical literature, history, and mythology, often creating heroic or noble themes in their paintings and sculptures.
