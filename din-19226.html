16. black iris iii (1926) principles and elements ​


Answer:

A major influence to my own style of art, Georgia O’ Keeffe was a prolific painter since her start in 1916 at New York. Recognized as the “Mother of American Modernism”, her work with no doubt embodies many of the visual elements and principles laid out in Nemett’s text.

Black Iris II (1926) is a part of a series of O’Keeffe’s famous close up of flowers. Overall there is a asymmetrical arrangement to the structure. Though it might seem symmetrical at first because of our assumption with flowers and nature, O’Keeffe uses slightly dissimilar petals in order to create a more dynamic painting. Compared to its opposite side, the upper left white petals illustrate more gesture and it allows the viewer’s eyes to follow that gesture to its focal point. O’Keeffe uses the flower’s black core as the focal point of the painting in which the rest of the painting is composed around in a circular fashion, as if the rest of petals wrap around the core.

Georgia O'Keeffe (1926)

Black Iris II – Georgia O’Keeffe (1926)

Regarding the space that O’Keeffe uses in the Black Iris II, I would argue that at times she uses both allover space and deep space. O’Keeffe illustrates allover space because there is little differentiation between what is foreground, middle ground, or background. This is because many parts of the petal in the upper white petals and the lower gray petals are similar to each other in gesture which creates a feeling of all of the petals belonging to the same level. However, the black core of the flower can be considered an element of deep space. The use of the color black is so strong compared to the rest of the flower that it creates a feeling of distance in which the core is further back than the rest of the petals.

What makes O’Keeffe’s flower paintings unique is the use of scale that she uses to depict flowers up-close. Compared to the actual size of flowers, O’Keeffe zooms in and paints the inside of flowers in a larger scale. By using this difference in scale, O’Keeffe’s paintings of flowers leaves a more powerful impression. O’Keeffe’s image of flowers are more foreboding and intense, unlike typical illustrations of flowers which are dainty and delicate. The way she uses elements and principles of unity, space, and scale combined allow O’Keefe’s art to remain timeless and classic long after her death.

17. Find the surface area of each cylinder1. 3in and 5in 2. 5in and 6in3. 2in and 7inPlease help me if you can. Thank you s0 muxh..! ​


Step-by-step explanation:

To calculate the surface area of a cylinder, you need to add the area of the top and bottom circles (which is the same) to the area of the curved surface. The formula for the surface area of a cylinder is:

Surface Area = 2πr² + 2πrh

where r is the radius of the base of the cylinder and h is the height of the cylinder.

Using this formula, we can find the surface area of each cylinder:

1. Cylinder with a radius of 3 inches and a height of 5 inches:

Surface Area = 2π(3)² + 2π(3)(5)

Surface Area = 2π(9) + 2π(15)

Surface Area = 18π + 30π

Surface Area = 48π

Surface Area ≈ 150.8 square inches

2. Cylinder with a radius of 5 inches and a height of 6 inches:

Surface Area = 2π(5)² + 2π(5)(6)

Surface Area = 2π(25) + 2π(30)

Surface Area = 50π + 60π

Surface Area = 110π

Surface Area ≈ 345.6 square inches

3. Cylinder with a radius of 2 inches and a height of 7 inches:

Surface Area = 2π(2)² + 2π(2)(7)

Surface Area = 2π(4) + 2π(14)

Surface Area = 8π + 28π

Surface Area = 36π

Surface Area ≈ 113.1 square inches

Therefore, the surface area of each cylinder is approximately 150.8 square inches, 345.6 square inches, and 113.1 square inches, respectively.
