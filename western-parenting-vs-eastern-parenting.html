15. the self western and eastern thought​


Etymological-philosophical evaluation of the idea of "self" in Western philosophy

Later, in the 20th century, the Austrian neuroanatomist and neurologist Sigmund Freud introduced his famous structural model of the psychic apparatus, ego and superego, in which the ego or self occupies the middle place, and so becomes a symbol of personal identity. The Latin term ego relates to the English pronoun "I. Following Freud's model's introduction, at least in postsocialist Bulgaria, the concept of "self" is capitalized.

Arthur Deikman refers to a supernatural aspect, the so-called "observing self," as opposed to the "objective self," which can be recognized but neither observed nor localized. According to Deikman, consciousness or the "observing self" is fundamental, although its contents or intent are secondary. The distinction between the following concepts, "awareness," "self-consciousness," "self," and "personal identity," is crucially important.

On the one hand, it is possible to identify "consciousness," "soul," and "self" as synonymous. In contrast, self-consciousness arises when the self's attention is directed toward itself. Additionally, the consciousness of the self toward experiential things should be associated with the self as such — the self is the watching subject, which has its past. Finally, the concept of "personal identity," symbolizes the sum of experiences and their derivatives (thoughts, values, etc.) that are associated with the self and positioned within an interpretative system continuously enriched by new experiences.

Eastern philosophical idea of the self

The most well-known concepts of the self in Eastern philosophy include Confucianism, Taoism, Buddhism, and Hinduism.

Confucianism. According to Confucianism, the self-consciousness concept relates to the social aspect of human development. The self is theorized as an "interactional self" – "one who is particularly aware of the physical attractiveness of other individuals. The individual self is therefore reliant on all other-selves. Thus, the self is submissive, prioritizing social requirements over its own needs and aspirations. According to this theory, the ideal self can be attained by harmonizing one's daily interactions with other members of society.

Taoism. Taoism emphasizes the falsity of words long before the philosophy of linguistic analysis and Jacques Derrida's deconstruction. Human language is incapable of describing the so-called "Tao," the core of existence and the world, or the Way. For this reason, paradoxes, contradictions, anecdotes, metaphors, and aphorisms are employed. Tao is inexpressible. According to Taoist belief, "the self is but one of the innumerable manifestations of the Tao.

Buddhism. Buddha warned against engaging in metaphysics due to the futility of such endeavours. However, Buddhism argues that the ego as such is an illusion and does not exist.

Hinduism. Through a monistic theory, Hinduism describes the self (metaphysics). Hinduism, like Buddhism, considers the core of human life as suffering and claims that this is caused by an erroneous idea of the self: "The genuine self is permanent and unchanging; the false self is transient and constantly changing.

Conclusion

Eastern and Western philosophical ideas of the self are fundamentally opposed. Due to our ignorance of the fundamental nature of the world, the self as an essence does not exist according to Eastern philosophy. According to Western philosophy, the self does exist.

However, there are multiple perspectives on the subject. There is currently no agreement regarding the existence of the self and what it represents. These barriers and language ambiguity impair neurosciences research, which seeks the material substrate of the self in the brain's specific structures and networks. Much of the current study on the self is theoretical (linguistic and philosophical) and focuses on the definition of the term. However, the functioning of separate brain regions and networks provides empirical data that can aid the theoretical study of the self and vice versa.

22. compare eastern with Western hemisphere​


Some similarities between the Eastern and Western Hemisphere include:

1. Both hemispheres are home to diverse cultures, languages, religions, and ethnicities.

2. Both have a mixture of urban and rural areas.

3. Both have regions with different climates and weather patterns.

4. Both have experienced colonization and imperialism.

Some differences between the Eastern and Western Hemisphere include:

1. The Eastern Hemisphere is home to many of the world’s largest countries, including Russia, China, and India, while the Western Hemisphere has mostly smaller countries and island nations.

2. The Eastern Hemisphere is the birthplace of many of the world’s major religions, including Islam, Hinduism, and Buddhism, while the Western Hemisphere is predominantly Christian.

3. The Eastern Hemisphere has a longer history of human civilization, with ancient civilizations like China, India, and Egypt, while the Western Hemisphere was largely uninhabited until relatively recently.

4. The Eastern Hemisphere has a higher population density, with many of the world’s largest cities, while the Western Hemisphere has a lower overall population density.
