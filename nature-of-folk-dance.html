3. What is the nature of dance of Sakuting Folk Dance​


Answer:

The sakuting dance, originally performed solely by boys, portrays a mock fight using sticks. A sakuting stick is striped or bamboo and is about 1½ feet long and tapered at the end, like a candle. ... Its dance form is the comedia (a theatrical dance, also called moro-moro) and features a battalla (choreographed skirmish).Sakuting is a Philippine folk dance that interprets a fight between Ilocano Christians and non-Christians. This mock fight between rival folks is traditionally performed during Christmas at the town plaza or performed house-to-house as a form of traditional carolling show.

9. describes the nature and background of the traditional folk dance​


Answer:

A folk dance is a dance developed by people that reflect the life of the people of a certain country or region.[1] Not all ethnic dances are folk dances. For example, ritual dances or dances of ritual origin are not considered to be folk dances. Ritual dances are usually called "Religious dances" because of their purpose. The terms "ethnic" and "traditional" are used when it is required to emphasize the cultural roots of the dance. In this sense, nearly all folk dances are ethnic ones. If some dances, such as polka, cross ethnic boundaries and even cross the boundary between "folk" and "ballroom dance", ethnic differences are often considerable enough to mention.

11. explain the nature and background of the maglalatik folk dance​


Answer:

The Maglalatik (also known as Manlalatik or Magbabao) is a folk dance from the Philippines performed by male dancers.Coconut shell halves are secured onto the dancers' hands and on vests upon which are hung four or six more coconut shell halves. The dancers perform the dance by hitting one coconut shell with the other; sometimes the ones on the hands, the ones on the body, or the shells worn by another performer, all in time to a fast drumbeat.

24. research the nature and backgrou and "tinikling folk dance"​


Answer:

Tinikling is a traditional folk dance of the Philippines that originated from the central island of Leyte. The dance is named after the Tikling bird, which is known for its graceful movements as it steps in between bamboo traps set by rice farmers.

Tinikling involves two people beating, tapping, and sliding bamboo poles on the ground and against each other in coordination with the music. Dancers step in and out of the poles, imitating the movements of the Tikling bird. The dance requires speed, agility, and timing, as the bamboo poles are moved quickly and the dancers must avoid getting their feet caught between them.

The origins of Tinikling can be traced back to pre-colonial times, when the dance was performed as a form of entertainment and a way to celebrate the harvest season. It was also used as a form of exercise and training for agility and coordination. Over time, Tinikling has become a popular folk dance in the Philippines, often performed during festivals and other cultural events.

Tinikling gained international recognition in the 1950s, when the Bayanihan Philippine Dance Company began performing the dance around the world. The group helped to popularize Tinikling and other traditional Filipino dances, showcasing the rich cultural heritage of the Philippines to audiences around the globe.

Today, Tinikling remains a beloved and important part of Filipino culture, taught in schools and performed at events both in the Philippines and abroad. Its popularity has led to the development of different variations of the dance, with some incorporating modern music and dance styles.
