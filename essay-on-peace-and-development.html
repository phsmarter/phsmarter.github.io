7. ESSAYdifferentiate the positive and negative peace education ​


Answer:

The lack of violence is referred to as negative calm. Positive peace has positive elements such as the healing of relationships, the development of social structures that meet the needs of the entire community, and the peaceful settlement of conflict. Peace does not imply that there is no strife.

Positive Peace is a sincere, permanent, and sustainable peace for all peoples based on fairness. “Negative” Peace: “Peace is not just the absence of some negative force–war, tension, confusion–but it is also the presence of some positive force–justice, goodwill, the kingdom of God's power.” Martin Luther King, Jr. said it best.

Positive peace entails removing the core causes of conflict, violence, and injustice, as well as making a conscious effort to develop a society that represents these principles. Positive peace presupposes that all life is related. Negative peace is defined as a peace without justice.

No doubt war is an evil, the greatest catastrophe that befalls human beings. It brings death and destruction, disease and starvation, poverty, and ruin in its wake.

One has only to look back to the havoc that was wrought in various countries not many years ago, in order to estimate the destructive effects of war. A particularly disturbing side of modern wars is that they tend to become global so that they may engulf the entire world.

But there are people who consider war as something grand and heroic and regard it as something that brings out the best in men, but this does not alter the fact that war is a terrible, dreadful calamity.

This is especially so now that a war will now be fought with atom bombs. Some people say war is necessary. A glance at the past history will tell that war has been a recurrent phenomenon in the history of nation.

No period in world history has been the devastating effects of war. We have had wars of all types long and short. In view of this it seems futile to talk of permanent and everlasting peace or to make plans for the establishment of eternal peace.

We have had advocates of non violence and the theory of the brotherhood of man. We have had the Buddha, Christ and Mahatma Gandhi. But in spite of that, weapons have always been used, military force has always been employed, clashes of arms have always occurred; war has always been waged.

War has indeed been such a marked feature of every age and period that it has come to be regarded As part of the normal life of nations. Machiavelli, the author of the known book, The Prince, defined peace as an interval between two wars Molise, the famous German field marshal declared war to be part of God’s world order.

Poets and prophets have dreamt of a millennium, a utopia in which war will not exist and eternal peace will reign on earth. But these dreams have not been fulfilled. After the Great War of 1914-18, it was thought that there would be no war for a long time to come and an institution called the League of Nations was founded as a safeguard against the outbreak of war.

The occurrence of another war (1939-45), however, conclusively proved that to think of an unbroken peace is to be unrealistic And that no institution or assembly can ever ensure the permanence of peace.

22. Make a drama script about the Philippine Peace Processs; culture of peace and sustainable development.​


Answer:

Title: The Road to Peace

Act 1

Scene 1:

The play opens in a remote village in the Philippines, where farmers and indigenous people live together in peace. They are visited by a group of rebels who threaten to take their land and livelihood. The villagers are forced to flee and seek refuge in a nearby town.

Scene 2:

In the town, the villagers meet with government officials who promise to help them. However, they are met with skepticism as they have lost trust in the government due to past broken promises. The officials explain that the government is committed to the Philippine Peace Process and has been working towards a culture of peace and sustainable development.

Scene 3:

The officials introduce the villagers to a group of peace advocates who have been working tirelessly to facilitate peace talks between the government and the rebels. The advocates explain that they have been trying to negotiate a peace agreement that would ensure the rights of all Filipinos, including those in the marginalized sectors.

Act 2

Scene 1:

The peace advocates convince the villagers to join a peace rally in the city to show their support for the Philippine Peace Process. At the rally, they hear speeches from government officials and other peace advocates who urge everyone to work together to achieve peace.

Scene 2:

After the rally, the villagers participate in a series of workshops and dialogues organized by the peace advocates. They learn about the root causes of conflict, the importance of dialogue, and the role of sustainable development in maintaining peace.

Scene 3:

The peace advocates organize a meeting between representatives from the government, the rebels, and the villagers. They discuss the issues and come up with a plan to address the root causes of the conflict. They agree to work together towards a peaceful resolution.

Act 3

Scene 1:

The peace agreement is signed, and the rebels agree to lay down their arms and join mainstream society. The government and the peace advocates provide support to the rebels to help them reintegrate into society.

Scene 2:

The villagers return to their homeland, and efforts are made to restore the land and the livelihoods of the farmers and indigenous people. Sustainable development projects are initiated, and the community is given a greater voice in decision-making processes.

Scene 3:

The curtain falls with the villagers living in peace and prosperity, and the country moving forward towards a brighter future of sustainable development and a culture of peace. The audience is challenged to reflect on their own roles in contributing towards peace building in their own communities and the larger society.


23. write an essay regarding about peace


Peace is a time without any fights or wars. In a larger sense, peace (or peacefulness) can mean a state of harmony, quiet or calm that is not disturbed by anything at all, like a still pond with no dogs

Peace within and among states is a goal of many people and organizations. One organization that was set up to bring peace among the nations and try to make war a thing of the past was the League of Nations after World War I. When it failed to stop World War II, it was replaced by the United Nations which tries to make the world peaceful. This means that if any member is attacked or invaded by another country without attacking that country first, the other members will come to help the country that was attacked first. This idea was used by the United Nations to defend both South Korea and Kuwait when they were attacked.

Martin Luther King Jr. wrote in a letter he sent from the Birmingham jail that, "True peace is not merely the absence of tension: it is the presence of justice." In other words, Real peace is more than just problems being gone: there must be fairness to have peace.

Alfred Nobel created an annual award, the Nobel Peace Prize, for the person who had done the most to bring peace to the world.
