Properties of Minerals

One of the properties of minerals is luster, namely the ability of minerals to reflect light. Each mineral has a different ability to reflect light, so luster can be used to identify a mineral type.

Luster

The grade and degree of light reflection that a mineral exhibits is known as its luster. A mineral's luster can be described as vitreous (or glassy) if it reflects light like glass or metallic if it does so like chrome. There are other additional shine options, such as pearly, waxy, and resinous. Minerals with an adamantine brilliance are as highly reflective as diamond. With a little effort, shine may be just as easily distinguished as color and can be fairly unique, especially for minerals like quartz that can be found in a variety of tints.

Further explanation

Color

Unfortunately, a mineral's color, which is the most glaring feature, is also the least diagnostic. Many minerals have the same hue, much as how a headache can be a sign of a wide range of issues, from the flu to a head injury. For epidote, instance,  actinolite, and olivine are just a few minerals that have a green hue.

Crystal form

This feature can be very diagnostic since the interior atomic structure of a mineral crystal greatly influences its exterior form. In particular, the angular interactions between crystal faces determine the shape of a crystal. Halite and pyrite are two examples of minerals that have a cubic shape, while tourmaline is a prismatic mineral. Some minerals, such as the copper ores azurite and malachite, don't form regular crystals and are amorphous instead.

Hardness

There are several methods for determining a mineral's hardness. The most popular method for comparing the hardness of a mineral to another item is the scratch test; if, for instance, a nail can scratch a crystal, the nail is harder than the mineral. Friedrich Mohs, an Austrian mineralogist, created a relative hardness scale based on the scratch test in the early 1800s. Each mineral was given an integer number between 1 and 10, with 10 being the hardest.

Density

Minerals range in density from 1.01 g/cm3 to 17.5 g/cm3, a broad range. Water has a density of 1 g/cm3, pure iron is 7.6 g/cm2, and pure gold is 17.65 g/cm3. Therefore, minerals exist in the density range between water and pure gold. Most geologists have evolved a better instinctive sense for what constitutes "normal" density, what is exceptionally heavy for its size, and what is unusually light. Measuring the density of a given mineral involves laborious procedures.

Cleavage and fracture

The atomic structures of most minerals include intrinsic flaws along a plane where the bond strength is weaker than the ones around it. A mineral will often fracture along that plane of inherent weakness when hammered or otherwise shattered. The strength of the bonds affects how well this sort of breakdown, known as cleavage, occurs. The strength of the bonds affects how well this sort of breakdown, known as cleavage, occurs. Some minerals' atomic structures have no weak points at all. These minerals fracture rather than cleave, as opposed to other minerals.

All waves behave in certain characteristic ways. They can undergo refraction, reflection, interference and diffraction. These basic properties define the behaviour of a wave – anything that reflects, refracts, diffracts and interferes is labelled a wave.

Reflection of water waves at a coast is usually not an important part of their behaviour, unless the coast has a steep cliff or a seawall. However, reflected waves tend to interfere with the oncoming waves, and these patterns can be studied.
