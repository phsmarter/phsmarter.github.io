2. write an essay on how you can determine if the evidence is valid or not​ short essay​


Answer:

Determining the validity of evidence is crucial in any field that involves the use of data, whether it's scientific research, journalism, or legal proceedings. Valid evidence is evidence that is accurate, reliable, and relevant to the argument or claim being made. To determine if evidence is valid, one must consider several factors, such as the source of the evidence, the methodology used to collect it, and the potential for bias or confounding variables.

One of the first things to consider when evaluating evidence is the source of the evidence. Is the source reputable and reliable? Are they an expert in the field, and do they have a track record of producing accurate and unbiased data? Additionally, it's essential to consider the methodology used to collect the evidence. Was the study conducted in a rigorous and scientific manner, or was it subject to bias or confounding variables?

Another key factor in determining the validity of evidence is the relevance of the evidence to the argument or claim being made. Is the evidence directly related to the claim being made, or is it tangential or unrelated? Does the evidence support the argument or claim, or is it weak or contradictory? Additionally, it's essential to consider the context in which the evidence was presented. Was the evidence presented in a way that was clear and transparent, or was it presented in a way that was confusing or misleading?

Finally, it's essential to consider the potential for bias or confounding variables. Bias can occur when the researcher or source of the evidence has a vested interest in the outcome of the study or data. Confounding variables can occur when there are other factors that may be influencing the results, such as socioeconomic status or pre-existing medical conditions.

In conclusion, determining the validity of evidence is a critical skill that requires careful evaluation of multiple factors, including the source of the evidence, the methodology used to collect it, and the potential for bias or confounding variables. By considering these factors and applying critical thinking skills, one can determine whether evidence is valid or not and make informed decisions based on that evidence.

7. Essay:Is it heredity or environment that determines how an organism will develop?​


Answer:

Heredity explains why offspring resemble, but are not identical to, their parents and is a unifying biological principle. Heredity refers to specific mechanisms by which characteristics or traits are passed from one generation to the next via genes. Genes encode the information for making specific proteins, which are responsible for the specific traits of an individual. Each gene can have several variants, called alleles, which code for different variants of the trait in question.

Genes reside in a cell’s chromosomes, each of which contains many genes. Every cell of any individual organism contains the identical set of chromosomes. When organisms reproduce, genetic information is transferred to their offspring. In species that reproduce sexually, each cell contains two variants of each chromosome, one inherited from each parent. Thus sexual reproduction gives rise to a new combination of chromosome pairs with variations between parent and offspring. Very rarely, mutations also cause variations, which may be harmful, neutral, or occasionally advantageous for an individual. Environmental as well as genetic variation and the relative dominance of each of the genes in a pair play an important role in how traits develop within an individual. Complex relationships between genes and interactions of genes with the environment determine how an organism will develop and function.

17. determine ways to organize ideas and opinions in an argumentative essay​


Answer:

Tips for Organizing an Argumentative Essay

1.)  Pre-Write/Outline

-There are not many aspects of the writing process that are as universally dreaded as the outline. Sometimes it can feel like pointless extra work, but it actually is your best safeguard against losing focus in a paper, preserves organization throughout, and just generally makes it easier to sit down and write an essay.

2.) Make sure you begin each paragraph with a topic sentence.

-A topic sentence should state the point/claim you make in your paragraph.

-A topic sentence should not be description, summary, or general background information.

3.) Make sure every topic sentence (and therefore, every paragraph) relates directly back to your thesis statement.

-Each topic sentence should say why/how the content of the paragraph matters to your overall argument.

-If your paragraph doesn’t relate to your thesis, consider incorporating it into another one, or cutting it out entirely.

4) Use effective transitions between paragraphs.

-Transitions should be used to mark a connection/opposition between a previous point and your current one

Connection:

Paragraph A: talks about Alice’s encounter with the caterpillar

Transition to Paragraph B: Similarly, Alice encounters another strange creature in the form of the Cheshire Cat, with whom, like the caterpillar, she holds a conversation with larger implications for the world of the novel.

Opposition:

Paragraph A: details how the madness of Wonderland critiques the fact-based Victorian England.

5) Re-read your paper!

Many issues that might hamper flow are small/general enough that you can catch them just by proofreading–i.e., sentence fragments, clunky/lengthy sentences, etc.

Reading your paper out loud is especially helpful for catching awkward phrases/sentences.

26. write an essay on how you can determine if the evidence is valid or not​


Answer:

As we live in an age of information overload, it is of utmost importance to determine whether the evidence presented to us is valid or not. This holds true whether we are assessing data for personal decision-making or evaluating research work in academic or professional contexts. So, the question that arises here is, how can one determine if the evidence is valid or not? In this essay, we will be discussing some of the methods we can explore to validate the credibility of the evidence presented to us.

The first and foremost step in determining the validity of evidence is to assess the source of the information. Check if the source is reliable or trustworthy. Consider if the organization or the author has a reputation for being a credible source of information in their respective field. For instance, if you are evaluating scientific or academic research work, check for the reputation of the institution or the journal where the research paper is published. If you find that the claim is made by a reputable source, then it is highly likely that the information is credible.

The next step in evaluating the validity of evidence is to look for accuracy. You can do this by verifying the evidence with secondary sources. Make sure that the data or information presented is up to date and is based on facts rather than subjective opinions. For example, if you come across data presented in a research paper, cross-check those figures with data available on government or reputed institution websites.

Another essential aspect of evaluating evidence is analyzing the logical coherence of the argument or claim made. Evaluate whether the argument presented provides adequate support for the evidence or is there are some inconsistencies or gaps that need to be addressed. If you find logical coherence in the argument, then the evidence is likely to be reliable.

The fourth step is to analyze the methodology used by the researcher, if research work is being evaluated; or the approach used to gather data. For example, if there are surveys, you can evaluate whether the sample size is adequate, or whether there was any bias involved in choosing the sample. Similarly, you can also look into the data collection methods and whether they align with industry standards.

The last and perhaps most crucial step in determining whether the evidence is valid or not is to look for peer reviews or expert validation. Expert validation involves seeking the opinion of reputable individuals in the field to assess the accuracy and validity of the evidence presented. Peer reviews are done by other experts working in the same field. Peer review will help critically assess the evidence, find mistakes, and examine the quality of the scholarship. This process ensures that the evidence is thoroughly evaluated and helps promote a culture of continuous improvement.

In conclusion, determining the credibility of evidence requires rigour and an analytical skill set. Individuals must evaluate the source, verify the accuracy, analyze the coherence of the argument, examine the research design and methodology, and seek expert validation or peer review. The idea is to ensure that evidence is thoroughly reviewed and subjected to scrutiny so that individuals can trust and rely on the information presented. By analyzing and evaluating evidence rigorously, we can make better decisions, engage in crucial debates, and promote the growth of knowledge.
