1. Three Generations of Human Rights

Human rights are the basic rights and freedoms that every individual is entitled to, simply because they are human beings. These rights have been categorized into three generations based on the time period and the focus of the rights.

First Generation: Civil and Political Rights

The first generation of human rights includes civil and political rights, which were first recognized in the 18th and 19th centuries. These rights include the right to life, liberty, and security of person, freedom of expression, religion, assembly, and association, as well as the right to a fair trial and freedom from torture, slavery, and discrimination.

Second Generation: Economic, Social and Cultural Rights

The second generation of human rights emerged in the mid-20th century and focused on economic, social, and cultural rights. These rights include the right to education, work, social security, healthcare, and a standard of living adequate for the health and well-being of the individual and their family.

Third Generation: Collective Rights

The third generation of human rights arose in the latter half of the 20th century and focuses on collective rights. These include the right to peace, a clean and healthy environment, and the right to self-determination of peoples and their cultures.

2. Sources of Human Rights Law in the Philippines

The Philippine Constitution and laws are the primary sources of human rights law in the Philippines. The 1987 Constitution specifically enumerates the civil and political rights, as well as economic, social, and cultural rights of all Filipino citizens. The Bill of Rights, in particular, outlines the fundamental rights of individuals and imposes limitations on the power of the state to ensure the protection of these rights.

International law is also a source of human rights law in the Philippines. The Philippines has ratified several international human rights treaties, such as the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights and the International Covenant on Economic, Social, and Cultural Rights. These treaties have become part of Philippine law and are recognized as sources of human rights law.

The Duterte administration's war on drugs has been a contentious issue both within the Philippines and internationally. While the government has argued that the campaign is necessary to combat drug-related crime and protect the safety of citizens, critics have accused the administration of widespread human rights violations.

One of the primary concerns raised by human rights advocates is the use of extrajudicial killings and the lack of due process in the handling of suspected drug offenders. Human rights groups have documented numerous cases of individuals being killed without a trial or evidence of wrongdoing, raising concerns about the government's commitment to upholding basic human rights.

The Philippine Constitution guarantees the right to due process and the protection of human rights, including the right to life and liberty. The United Nations Universal Declaration of Human Rights also affirms these principles, which are recognized internationally as fundamental to human dignity and justice.

The International Criminal Court (ICC) is a global institution established to investigate and prosecute war crimes, genocide, and crimes against humanity. The ICC has the authority to investigate and prosecute crimes committed by individuals, including government officials, regardless of their position or nationality. However, for the ICC to exercise its jurisdiction over cases, a country must either be a member of the ICC or have accepted the court's jurisdiction on a case-by-case basis.

The Philippines is a member of the ICC, having ratified the Rome Statute in 2011. As such, the ICC has jurisdiction over crimes committed in the Philippines, including human rights violations. However, in 2018, the Philippine government announced its withdrawal from the ICC, claiming that the court's investigation into the war on drugs was politically motivated and an infringement of national sovereignty.

In conclusion, the Duterte administration's war on drugs has raised serious concerns about human rights violations, particularly regarding due process and extrajudicial killings. The Philippine Constitution and the international community recognize the importance of upholding human rights and due process, and it is the government's responsibility to ensure that these rights are protected. While the ICC has jurisdiction over crimes committed in the Philippines, the government's decision to withdraw from the court has complicated efforts to investigate and hold accountable those responsible for human rights violations.
