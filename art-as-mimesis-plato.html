9. "Plato philosophy of art"


"Plato Philosophy of Art"

Plato had a love-hate relationship with the arts. He must have had some love for the arts, because he talks about them often, and his remarks show that he paid close attention to what he saw and heard. He was also a fine literary stylist and a great story-teller; in fact he is said to have been a poet before he encountered Socrates and became a philosopher. Some of his dialogues are real literary masterpieces. On the other hand, he found the arts threatening. He proposed sending the poets and playwrights out of his ideal Republic, or at least censoring what they wrote; and he wanted music and painting severely censored. The arts, he thought, are powerful shapers of character. Thus, to train and protect ideal citizens for an ideal society, the arts must be strictly controlled.

Plato's influence on western culture generally is a very strong one, and this includes a strong influence on the arts, and on theories of art. In the case of the arts and aesthetic theory that influence is mostly indirect, and is best understood if one knows a little bit about his philosophy.

13. Explain how Plato and Aristotle's views enhanced the understanding of art and literature in general.​


❏ ART & LITERATURE

Plato and Aristotle were two ancient Greek philosophers who made significant contributions to the field of aesthetics and the study of art and literature.

Plato, who lived in the 4th century BCE, believed that art was a imitation of reality and that it had the power to influence people's beliefs and actions. He argued that the role of art was to educate and improve society, and that it should therefore be censored if it contained immoral or harmful ideas.

Aristotle, who lived in the 4th century BCE, also believed that art had the power to educate and influence people, but he took a more positive view of its role. He argued that art had the ability to provide pleasure and enrich the human experience, and that it could be a source of moral guidance.

Both Plato and Aristotle's views on art and literature have had a lasting influence on the way that these subjects are studied and understood. Their ideas about the role of art in society and the importance of its ability to educate and influence have shaped the way that art and literature are evaluated and appreciated.

22. Why did plato prefer censorship of art in a society?​


Plato makes a systematic case for censoring all arts in his work "The Republic". He believed that to have a good society, children must be exposed to good material and shielded from bad material. It is the obligation of the State to educate its citizens and therefore the State should allow only good material and suppress bad material. This censorship applies also to art.

29. What is mimesis according to Plato? Explain his conclusion that art is twice removed from reality. 2. What is the importance of poetry and tragic literature (or in our modern-day drama) in the process of CATHARSIS? 3. Explain the theory of Leo Tolstoy on Art as a Communication of Emotion.​


1. According to Plato, mimesis is the act of imitation or representation of reality in art. He believed that art is twice removed from reality because it is a mere copy or imitation of the physical world, which is already a copy or shadow of the "true" world of Forms or Ideas. Plato argued that artists create their works from a flawed perception of reality and are therefore unable to capture its essential truth. As a result, their creations are mere shadows of the physical world and cannot lead to genuine knowledge or understanding.

2. Poetry and tragic literature play a crucial role in the process of catharsis because they allow the audience to experience emotions in a controlled and safe environment. In this context, catharsis refers to the emotional cleansing or release that occurs when the audience identifies with the characters and their experiences on stage. Through the portrayal of tragic events and emotions, the audience is able to confront their own fears, desires, and anxieties, and ultimately achieve a sense of emotional clarity and reconciliation. This process of catharsis is seen as essential for personal and social transformation, as it allows individuals to confront and overcome their emotional limitations and biases.

3. Leo Tolstoy believed that the purpose of art was to communicate emotions, particularly moral and religious truths, to the audience. He argued that art was a form of indirect communication, through which the artist could convey complex emotions and ideas that were difficult to express directly in words. According to Tolstoy, the communication of emotion was essential for art to have value and meaning, as it allowed the audience to experience a sense of shared humanity and a connection with the artist's vision. He believed that true art should be simple, honest, and accessible to all, and that its value lay in its ability to inspire and uplift the soul through the communication of universal emotions and moral truths.
