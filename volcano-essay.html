10. essay about geothermal energy and volcano​


Essay about Geothermal Energy:

Geothermal energy is the earth’s natural heat available inside the earth. This thermal energy contained in the rock and fluid that filled up fractures and pores in the earth’s crust can profitably be used for various purposes. Heat from the Earth, or geothermal — Geo (Earth) + thermal (heat) — energy can be and is accessed by drilling water or steam wells in a process similar to drilling for oil.

Geothermal resources range from shallow ground to hot water and rock several miles below the Earth’s surface, and even farther down to the extremely hot molten rock called magma. Mile-or-more-deep wells can be drilled into underground reservoirs to tap steam and very hot water that can be brought to the surface for use in a variety of applications.

This geothermal energy originates from the original formation of the planet, from radioactive decay of minerals, from volcanic activity and from solar energy absorbed at the surface. It has been used for bathing since Paleolithic times and for space heating since ancient Roman times, but is now better known for generating electricity.

Worldwide, about 10,715 megawatts (MW) of geothermal power is online in 24 countries. An additional 28 gigawatts of direct geothermal heating capacity is installed for district heating, space heating, spas, industrial processes, desalination and agricultural applications.

16. ESSAY. How different types of volcano vary in appearance and eruption?


Answer:

Volcanoes are natural phenomena that occur when molten rock, ash, and gas escape from the earth's crust. These geological formations come in different shapes and sizes, and their eruptions can range from peaceful to catastrophic. There are different types of volcanoes, each with unique characteristics that determine their appearance and eruption style. In this essay, we will discuss how different types of volcanoes vary in appearance and eruption.

The three main types of volcanoes are shield volcanoes, cinder cone volcanoes, and stratovolcanoes. Shield volcanoes are broad, gently sloping mountains formed by repeated lava flows. They are characterized by their low viscosity magma, which allows the lava to flow quickly and for long distances. Due to their broad base, shield volcanoes can be enormous and cover large areas. Examples of shield volcanoes include Mauna Loa in Hawaii and Galapagos Islands.

Cinder cone volcanoes are smaller than shield volcanoes and are usually only a few hundred meters high. They are formed by explosive eruptions that eject small pieces of lava into the air. As the lava falls back to the ground, it piles up around the vent, forming a cone-shaped mountain. Cinder cone volcanoes are typically found in clusters and can erupt several times over a short period. Examples of cinder cone volcanoes include Paricutin in Mexico and Sunset Crater in Arizona.

Stratovolcanoes, also known as composite volcanoes, are the most common type of volcano. They are tall, steep-sided mountains made of layers of ash, lava, and other volcanic debris. Unlike shield volcanoes, stratovolcanoes are formed by explosive eruptions that can be very violent. These eruptions can cause pyroclastic flows, which are fast-moving avalanches of hot ash, rock, and gas. Mount St. Helens in Washington State and Mount Fuji in Japan are examples of stratovolcanoes.

In terms of eruption style, volcanoes can be classified as effusive or explosive. Effusive eruptions are gentle and non-explosive, with lava flowing out of the volcano's vent and spreading slowly over the surrounding area. Shield volcanoes are known for their effusive eruptions, which can last for days or even years. Explosive eruptions, on the other hand, are violent and destructive. They occur when magma rises rapidly to the surface and mixes with gases and other volatile substances, causing the eruption to be explosive. Stratovolcanoes and cinder cone volcanoes are known for their explosive eruptions, which can send ash and debris high into the atmosphere and cause widespread damage.

In conclusion, the appearance and eruption style of a volcano are determined by its type. Shield volcanoes are broad, gently sloping mountains formed by repeated lava flows and have effusive eruptions. Cinder cone volcanoes are smaller and formed by explosive eruptions, while stratovolcanoes are tall, steep-sided mountains made of layers of ash, lava, and other volcanic debris and have explosive eruptions. Understanding the characteristics of different types of volcanoes can help scientists predict their behavior and mitigate the potential damage caused by their eruptions.

Answer:

Volcanoes are one of the most fascinating natural wonders on earth, but not all volcanoes are the same. There are different types of volcanoes, each with its unique appearance and eruption patterns. These differences are largely due to the type of magma, the location, and the underlying geological processes.

Firstly, let's consider the shield volcano. Shield volcanoes are typically broad and gently sloping with a convex shape, much like a warrior's shield. They are characterized by frequent and relatively gentle eruptions that produce fluid basaltic lava flows. These eruptions often create a caldera or depression at the summit of the volcano. Shield volcanoes are most commonly found at divergent plate boundaries or hotspots, such as Hawaii.

In contrast, stratovolcanoes or composite volcanoes are characterized by steep, conical shapes with a summit crater. These volcanoes are built up over time by layers of ash, lava, and other volcanic materials. The eruptions of stratovolcanoes are typically more explosive than shield volcanoes, producing ash, pumice, and other volcanic debris. They are often located at subduction zones, where one tectonic plate is forced beneath another.

Another type of volcano is the cinder cone, which is a small, steep-sided volcano that is composed of loose, fragmental volcanic material. Cinder cones are often found at the base of larger volcanoes and are typically only a few hundred meters high. They are formed by eruptions of small volumes of gas-rich lava that solidifies as it falls, creating the loose, cindery material that gives them their name.

Finally, there are supervolcanoes, which are massive volcanoes capable of producing the largest and most catastrophic eruptions on earth. These volcanoes are characterized by huge calderas, sometimes tens of kilometers in diameter, and can produce pyroclastic flows, ash clouds, and other destructive phenomena. Supervolcanoes are rare but can have devastating effects on the local and global environment.

In conclusion, the different types of volcanoes vary significantly in their appearance and eruption patterns. Shield volcanoes are broad and gently sloping, with frequent and relatively gentle eruptions, while stratovolcanoes are steep and conical, with more explosive eruptions. Cinder cones are small and composed of loose, fragmental material, while supervolcanoes are massive and capable of producing catastrophic eruptions. Understanding these differences is critical for scientists and policymakers in predicting and preparing for volcanic activity and its potential impacts.

19. essay about taal volcano​


Answer:

Taal volcano with its lake-filled 15x20 km wide Talisay (Taal) caldera is a beautiful caldera volcano, but also one of the most active and dangerous volcanoes of the Philippines. Taal has had some of the country's largest and deadliest eruptions: At least 6 eruptions during the recorded history of Taal since 1572 claimed fatalities, mostly from powerful pyroclastic flows, as well as tsunamis produced in the crater lake.

Background:

The Taal caldera is largely filled by Lake Taal, whose 267 sq km surface lies only 3 m above sea level. The maximum depth of the lake is 160 m, and contains several eruptive centers submerged beneath the lake. All historic eruptions took place from the 5-km-wide volcanic island in the northern-central part of the lake.

The island is formed by overlapping stratovolcanoes, cinder cones and tuff rings (maars). Historic eruptions have seen the constant change and growth of the island.

Taal caused one of the worst volcano disasters in history: its eruption in 1911 killed 1334 people and caused ash fall as far as Manila city. Due to its devastating potential, Taal was declared one of the "Decade Volcanoes" in the Decade Volcanoes program of the 1990s in order to incentive study and monitoring of the volcano. Taal is today one of the most closely monitored volcanoes in the region. An increase in seismic activity under Taal was recorded in November 2006, followed by an increase in hot water springs in the crater in April 2007.
