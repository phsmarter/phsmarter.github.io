8. 7. How are the key concepts of Marxism correlated with literature?​


The Key Concepts Of Marxism

Marxist literary theory views the work as a representation of the current class conflict. Literature is more than just a matter of taste or personal expression. It has some connection to the social and political climate of the day. According to Marxism, literature is a part of the superstructure that results from the foundational truths. Marxist analysis links literary works to the society, history, and social, political, and cultural frameworks in which they were produced. A literary work without a writer or the forces that shaped that writer is not taken into consideration.

Marx himself frequently viewed literature as straightforward propaganda for the privileged classes. Some of this is accurate. People in a feudal society, for instance, adored chivalric romances, tales of knights who fight for honor and win the affection of their ladies. The glamorous lifestyle of the modern gentleman, the lady's man who wears in fancy clothes and drives fast automobiles, is celebrated in James Bond movies, which are popular in today's capitalistic culture. In these cultural illusions, the aristocrat is the hero who comes to our aid and defends us against fictitious antagonists who want to upend the status quo. Marx's fairly simplistic classification of literature as propaganda, however, incensed a large number of later Marxists.

10. what is the difference between classical Marxism and modern marxism ?​


Marxism, as formulated by Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, is a XIX-century theory of how Capitalism, and the class struggle it creates, will inevitably lead to the victory of Communism.

Neo-Marxism is a bunch of modern day’s leftist theories that declare the classical Marxism outdated. Their main argument is that the modern society is no longer dominated by the confrontation between the pauperized (=poor) proletarians and the industrial/financial Capital. They are also deeply frustrated by the success of Communist countries in gaining power, building up the industrial base, educating illiterate peasants, giving women equality and better maternity care, only to yield all that away to the superior productivity of market forces (Russia, China, Vietnam, Poland etc).

Neo-Marxists try to shed the unfortunate association the USSR and PRC have created between Communism and totalitarianism (oppression, starvation, mass murder, police state). They declare this a deviation from the true path of Socialism. Neo-Marxists stress the new possibilities created by technology, universal education, and the hacker ethics of knowledge sharing for the sake of deconstructing the exploitative, divisive, nature-devastating Capitalism of the XXI century.

13. Strengths of marxism


LIST OF STRENGTHS OF MARXISM

1. It tends to create a system of true equality.
Although Marxism’s system of government is considered as communism, it places an emphasis on human rights, with its foundation encompassing equal gender roles, health care and access to education. As Marx believed, there should be equality before the law and societal services, where everyone has an equal stance and opportunity with no dominant gender. This means that every person would be able to get access to the most important things he needs regardless of whatever he does, wherever he lives or how much he makes to provide a better living for those depending on him.

2. It offers benefits to the society. 
If you look at the Marxist theory, it considers society as a whole, which means that it acknowledges all the social forces involved, including the power interests of different groups. Stressing the role of class struggle or conflict within society between the bourgeoisie and the proletariat, it is effective in explaining change in society. In essence, it organizes society under capitalism, where the bourgeoisie tends to maximize profit with the proletariat.

3. It helps with capitalism.
Ironically, when huge multi-nationals dominate the entire world economy, capital advocates would tell us that the future lies with small businesses or always state that “Small is beautiful”. However, we can consider that the youthful phase of capitalism is gone beyond recall. But as far as Marxism is concerned, free competition inevitably begets monopoly, where the struggle between big and small capitals always yields to the same result. In modern times, the vast power of multi-nationals and monopolies seems to exercise a total stranglehold on the world, holding access to economies of scale, staggering sums of money, ability to manipulate commodity prices and even the influence of government policies. Now, Marxism was able to predict the inevitable tendency towards monopolization, where free competition was a standard.

4. It reduces the tendency of debt. 
Under the Marxist philosophy, communities will be working together to achieve success, where all people would come together to provide for each other, with the help of the government distributing resources as required.

5. It protects the rights of unions. 
Rather than exploiting managers, Marxism encourages unions to stand up for personal rights, creating a system of checks and balances for a maximum production level to be achieved. As it is believed that this philosophy never exploits workers by management, followers believe that unions are definitely a great idea.

18. Explain the some examples of an example of marxism


Answer:

1. Class struggle: Marxists believe that the primary struggle in society is between the working class and the capitalist class and that all other struggles are secondary and/or derivatives of this one.

2. Dialectical Materialism: This is the belief that all phenomena in society are the result of the interaction between material forces and the ideas that are held by individuals.

3. Surplus Value: This is the idea that the capitalist class is able to extract more value from the labor of the working class than the wage that they pay the worker.

4. Alienation: This is the idea that the worker is alienated from the means of production and the labor that they perform.

5. Historical Materialism: This is the belief that all social change is the result of material conditions and the contradictions between them.
