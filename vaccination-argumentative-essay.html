STUDENT ESSAY: Is Compulsory COVID-19 Vaccination a Violation of Human Rights?

Aaron Chia

In response to the COVID-19 pandemic, the UK Government passed the Coronavirus Act 2020 (CA), which implemented a number of emergency powers, allowing public officials to take action in specific situations in order to contain and slow down the spread of the virus as well as ease the burden on frontline staff.[1] Examples of these emergency powers include: the capability for public officials to test, isolate, and detain a person where they have reasonable grounds to think that the person is infected; restrict or prohibit gatherings or events; and require the temporary closure of a school or registered childcare provider.[2]

In its efforts to support the public health benefit, the CA has arguably led to interference with individuals’ right to liberty under Article 5(1) of the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR).[3] Pugh’s 2020 article on the CA discusses how individuals may be deprived of their liberty to prevent the spread of infectious diseases, if that deprivation is “necessary and proportionate” and is in accordance with a procedure prescribed by law.[4] Pugh refers to this as “the public health exception”.[5] The importance of this approach directly relates to the consideration of a compulsory COVID-19 vaccine, specifically whether limitations on specific rights can be justified.

This essay analyzes whether a compulsory COVID-19 vaccine would be a violation of human rights law in the UK. For a comprehensive understanding of the implications of this topic, I provide background information and discuss perspectives regarding compulsory vaccinations in general. This discussion is followed by an outline of the specific rights under the ECHR that may be affected if a compulsory vaccination was to be implemented in the UK. I then discuss various interpretations of the elements required for state interference upon these rights and expand on them in regard to their application to compulsory vaccination. Through this analysis, it is concluded that a compulsory COVID-19 vaccination would not be a violation of human rights in the UK if the government sets out explicit parameters whereby a compulsory vaccination would be “necessary and proportionate” in the interests of health and safety, and if it was to ensure that the state is fulfilling its positive obligation to protect the right to life.[6]

18. Make an Argumentative Essay: Should Children Be Vaccinated?​


Answer:

Vaccination is a critical aspect of modern medicine that has contributed to saving many lives and controlling the spread of illnesses. Despite this, there is a growing group of people who oppose vaccination and question its safety and effectiveness. The debate of whether children should be vaccinated is complex and divisive, but the answer is straightforward: children should receive vaccines.

First, vaccines are secure and efficient. Numerous studies and clinical trials have demonstrated that vaccines are a safe and effective method of controlling infectious diseases. Organizations such as the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and World Health Organization (WHO) concur that vaccines are safe and have undergone thorough testing for their safety and effectiveness. Additionally, the safety of vaccines is continually monitored through surveillance systems and reporting of adverse events, allowing for rapid identification and resolution of any issues with vaccines.

Second, vaccinating children protects vulnerable groups. When a significant portion of the population is vaccinated, it creates herd immunity, making it more challenging for diseases to spread. This is especially important for individuals who cannot receive vaccines, such as newborns, elderly people, and those with certain medical conditions. By protecting these vulnerable groups, vaccinating children ensures that everyone has access to the protection that vaccines offer.

Third, not vaccinating children puts them and others in danger. Diseases that were once widespread and deadly, like measles and polio, can re-emerge as a threat if enough people stop getting vaccinated. This is already happening in some areas, with outbreaks of preventable diseases becoming increasingly frequent. Not only does this put unvaccinated individuals at risk, but it also increases the danger to vulnerable populations who cannot receive vaccines.

Finally, vaccinating children is a moral duty. Parents have a responsibility to protect their children from harm, and vaccines provide a safe and effective way to do so. By choosing not to vaccinate their children, parents are not only putting their own children at risk, but they are also putting others in their community in danger. Furthermore, vaccination ensures that future generations continue to have access to the protection that vaccines offer, preserving the progress made against preventable diseases.

In conclusion, vaccinating children is essential. Vaccines are safe and effective, protect vulnerable populations, prevent the spread of diseases, and are a moral obligation. By vaccinating children, we can help ensure a healthier and safer future for everyone.

One of the most compelling reasons to vaccinate children is to protect them against deadly diseases. The availability of vaccines has made it possible to eradicate many dangerous diseases that once claimed thousands of lives, such as smallpox and polio. By vaccinating children, parents can help ensure that their children are protected from diseases that could cause severe harm or even death. Furthermore, vaccines also provide protection against diseases that can cause serious long-term health problems, such as meningitis and chickenpox.

Body Paragraph 2: Community protection

Vaccination not only protects individuals, but it also protects the wider community. When a large number of people are vaccinated, it creates herd immunity, which makes it difficult for a disease to spread. This helps to protect those who are unable to receive vaccines, such as individuals with weakened immune systems, as well as newborns who are too young to receive certain vaccines.

Body Paragraph 3: Safety and efficacy of vaccines

Another common concern among parents is the safety and efficacy of vaccines. However, the vast majority of scientific evidence supports the safety and effectiveness of vaccines. In fact, vaccines are thoroughly tested and monitored by various agencies, such as the World Health Organization (WHO) and the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), to ensure their safety. Additionally, vaccines have been shown to be highly effective in preventing the spread of diseases and reducing their severity.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, vaccinating children is a crucial step in protecting their health and the health of others. Moreover, the safety and efficacy of vaccines have been well established, making them a safe and effective way to protect children against diseases. It is important that parents understand the benefits of vaccination and take the necessary steps to ensure that their children are properly protected.
