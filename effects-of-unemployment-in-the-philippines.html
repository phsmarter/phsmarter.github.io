INEQUALITY:

Causes: Unequal distribution of wealth, power, and resources, social and economic marginalization, limited access to education and job opportunities, historical and systemic discrimination based on ethnicity, gender, or social status.

Effects: Widening wealth gap, social unrest, poverty, limited social mobility, political instability, health disparities, and decreased economic growth.

EDUCATION:

Causes: Insufficient funding for public schools, inadequate teacher training and salaries, lack of access to education, cultural attitudes toward education, and a lack of quality learning materials.

Effects: Limited opportunities for social mobility, reduced economic growth, increased poverty rates, and limited access to opportunities.

HOUSING:

Causes: Lack of affordable housing, poor urban planning, insufficient government regulation, informal settlements, and natural disasters.

Effects: Increased homelessness, poor living conditions, health risks, limited economic opportunities, and social instability.

UNEMPLOYMENT:

Causes: Economic downturns, insufficient job creation, insufficient skills training, limited access to education, and poor economic policies.

Effects: Reduced economic growth, increased poverty rates, social unrest, limited opportunities for social mobility, and increased crime rates.

CRIME:

Causes: Poverty, inequality, unemployment, lack of access to education, social marginalization, corruption, and inadequate law enforcement.

Effects: Fear, loss of property and life, social instability, economic decline, and decreased investment.

CORRUPTION:

Causes: Inadequate regulation and transparency, political patronage, a lack of political will to combat corruption, and societal acceptance of corrupt practices.

Effects: Reduced public trust in government, decreased foreign investment, reduced economic growth, and increased poverty rates.

POVERTY:

Causes: Inequality, limited access to education, insufficient job opportunities, poor health, and inadequate government social services.

Effects: Reduced economic growth, increased crime rates, poor health outcomes, and limited opportunities for social mobility.

ENVIRONMENTAL DEGRADATION:

Causes: Overpopulation, industrialization, poor land use planning, lack of environmental regulation, and unsustainable development practices.

Effects: Loss of biodiversity, pollution, natural disasters, and adverse health effects.

HEALTHCARE:

Causes: Insufficient healthcare funding, lack of health education, and inadequate infrastructure and resources.

Effects: Poor health outcomes, limited economic opportunities, and social instability.

14. what are the effects of unemployment's pros and cons?


Answer:

All developed economies have unemployment benefit programs to protect workers against major income losses during spells of unemployment. By enabling unemployed workers to meet basic consumption needs, the programs protect workers from having to sell their assets or accept jobs below their qualifications. The programs also help stabilize the economy during recessions. If benefits are too generous, however, the programs can lengthen unemployment and raise the unemployment rate. The policy challenge is to protect workers while minimizing undesirable side effects.

21. give your stand about unemployment in the philippines​


Answer:

In the world today, there are a few common causes of unemployment. Let us look at some of these causes and compare them with some of the causes of unemployment in the Philippines.  

Common Causes of Unemployment

Structural Unemployment

Structural unemployment occurs when there are shifts in the demand among the industries. The workforce possesses skills for jobs that were previously high in demand. With the shift in demand for jobs, supply for the jobs that were previously high in demand outstrips the demand for these jobs. As a result, many people become unemployed until market equilibrium is reached, where supply equals demand. Moreover, the jobs that are now high in demand may require skills that the unemployed do not possess. Unless they invest in re-training, which is time consuming, they will find it difficult to find a place for themselves in these jobs because firms prefer employees with the necessary skills and experience.

Oversupply of graduates

The number of graduates have been increasing over the years. Getting a degree is all too common nowadays. However, the labour force does not require so many graduates. National Development Minister Khaw Boon Wan once said that Singapore, which has a significant number of graduates, could not be a nation entirely made up of them. “Can you have a whole country where 100% are graduates? I am not so sure. What you do not want is to create huge graduate unemployment.”

Economic Recession

Economic recession is one of the main causes of unemployment. During a recession, firms experience a decrease in revenue. As profit equals to revenue minus cost, firms would want to cut as much cost as possible. One of the ways to do so is through the retrenchment of employees, where employees deemed to be not productive enough lose their jobs. As this happens, unemployment rates go up.

Causes of Unemployment in the Philippines

Unemployment in the Philippines is attributed to reasons including overpopulation, oversupply of labour force on certain industries and the inability to take on available jobs.  

Overpopulation

Philippines has a high population growth rate at an average of 1.7% for the past few years, consistently higher than the world's population growth rate at an average of 1.2%. The high population growth rate which translates to a steady supply of graduates outstrips the rate at which jobs are created, leaving these graduates finding themselves unemployed.

Undergraduates are still taking up college courses that are popular but were previously high in demand. After graduation, these graduates possess skills that are not high in demand in the job market. As such, there is a skill mismatch. For example, in the healthcare industry, nurses were previously in demand abroad. Many undergraduates took up nursing and soon supply matches demand. In 2009, the United States faced its highest unemployment rates since 1982-1983 with rates reaching or exceeding ten percent in the last three months. The relationship between unemployment and poor health has been well documented [2–6]. The unemployed tend to have higher levels of impaired mental health including depression, anxiety, and stress, as well as higher levels of mental health hospital admissions, chronic disease (cardiovascular disease, hypertension, and musculoskeletal disorders), and premature mortality [2, 5–12]. Some longitudinal studies have shown that higher levels of depression and unemployment are not just correlated, but that higher levels of depression are a result of unemployment [11, 13, 14]. Other prospective studies have found that poor mental health contributes to unemployment [15]. A study by Montgomery et al. [11] showed that subjects who had recently become unemployed had an adjusted relative risk of 2.10 for depression and anxiety compared to those who had not recently become unemployed. When participants with preexisting depression were excluded from the study, those who had greater than thirty-seven months of accumulated unemployment were two times more apt to be depressed or anxious than were the employed (RR = 2.04). Additionally, unemployment is associated with unhealthy behaviors such as increased alcohol and tobacco consumption and decreased physical activity [9, 16, 17]. The survey includes a core component with questions that are asked to all participants in every state. 28. give some effects of unemployment and underemployment​


Answer:

Unemployment and underemployment can have several negative effects on individuals, families, and society as a whole, including:

Financial strain: Unemployment and underemployment can lead to financial stress, making it difficult for individuals to meet their basic needs such as food, housing, and healthcare. This can also lead to accumulating debt and financial instability.

Mental and physical health problems: Individuals who are unemployed or underemployed may experience higher levels of stress, anxiety, and depression. These mental health issues can lead to physical health problems such as hypertension, heart disease, and other stress-related illnesses.

Reduced self-esteem and confidence: Being unemployed or underemployed can erode an individual's self-esteem and confidence. This can lead to a sense of hopelessness and feelings of worthlessness.

Social isolation: Unemployment and underemployment can result in social isolation and a lack of social support, which can exacerbate mental health problems and increase the risk of substance abuse and other negative coping mechanisms.

Reduced economic growth: Unemployment and underemployment can slow down economic growth as it reduces consumer spending and lowers the overall demand for goods and services.

Increased crime rates: High rates of unemployment and underemployment can lead to an increase in crime rates, as individuals may resort to illegal means to make ends meet.

Reduced workforce productivity: Underemployment can lead to a reduction in productivity as individuals may not be utilizing their skills and education to their fullest potential. This can also result in a skills gap in the workforce.

Overall, unemployment and underemployment can have a range of negative effects on individuals, families, and society. It is important for policymakers to prioritize job creation and support systems for those who are unemployed or underemployed to mitigate these effects
