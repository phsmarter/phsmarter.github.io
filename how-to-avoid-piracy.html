1. how can we prevent piracy​


Remove the Incentive

One of the most-effective ways of dealing with piracy is by removing the incentive for the consumers to look for pirated content. Effectively this can be characterised by offering a good product and a good user experience at a fair price. It is interesting to note that the new Disney+ SVOD service launching in the fall is coming to market at a $6.99 monthly pricepoint. For good or bad, the bar is set somewhere between their offering and Netflix’s $12.99 at the high-end.

Price is not the only differentiator. The importance of the user experience cannot be understated; viewers want sympathetic interfaces that contain the usual sophisticated bells and whistles such as personal recommendations, and they want excellent picture quality with no buffering and/or latency. The more the industry can provide that at realistic cost, the less people will be driven towards pirate arms.

You are not going to stop everyone from watching pirated content, but this can definitely remove some of the more casual illegal consumers.

4. Differentiate plagiarism and intellectual piracy


Plagiarism is the illegal act of taking other’s words or ideas as your own instead of giving the credit to the real owner.  This is done to gain honor.

Usually, plagiarism takes place among students who wish to accomplish their literary assignments in the most convenient way.  The easy access to the internet can provide them with good essays that is used for their term paper and the like.

On the other hand, intellectual piracy is the illegal act of copying other’s works for reselling or redistribution in a much lower price.  As opposed to plagiarism, this involves earning of money by using other’s works.

One of the very common example is the free downloading of movies, music, and books online.  It seems free but the website earns from the advertisements.  And the reselling of pirated CDs or DVDs along the walkways.

5. What is the difference between piracy in criminal law and piracy special law​


Answer:

Piracy refers to the act of robbing or attacking a ship at sea. In the context of law, there are two different types of piracy: piracy under criminal law and piracy under special law.

Piracy under criminal law is governed by the general criminal laws of a country. In this context, piracy is considered a serious crime and is often punishable by imprisonment or even death. Piracy under criminal law is generally prosecuted by national authorities, and the penalties for piracy may vary depending on the laws of the country in question.

Piracy under special law, on the other hand, is a type of piracy that is specifically defined and regulated by international law. This type of piracy is covered by the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) and is therefore subject to international jurisdiction.

Under UNCLOS, piracy is defined as any illegal acts of violence or detention, or any act of depredation, committed for private ends by the crew or passengers of a private ship or aircraft against another ship or aircraft on the high seas or in a place outside the jurisdiction of any state. Piracy under special law is therefore subject to specific international penalties, which can include fines, imprisonment, and other sanctions.

In summary, while both types of piracy involve criminal acts committed at sea, piracy under criminal law is governed by the general criminal laws of a country, whereas piracy under special law is a type of piracy that is specifically defined and regulated by international law.
