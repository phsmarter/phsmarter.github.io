1. What is research?

—Research is "creative and systematic work undertaken to increase the stock of knowledge". It involves the collection, organization and analysis of information to increase understanding of a topic or issue. A research project may be an expansion on past work in the field.

2. Why do we need to repeat doing research? What is IPO?

— It is very important that research can be replicated, because it means that other researchers can test the findings of the research. Replicability keeps researchers honest and can give readers confidence in research. If the research is replicable, then any false conclusions can eventually be shown to be wrong. An initial public offering or stock launch is a public offering in which shares of a company are sold to institutional investors and usually also retail investors. An IPO is typically underwritten by one or more investment banks, who also arrange for the shares to be listed on one or more stock exchanges.

3. What are the two types of research? What is the difference of quantitative and qualitative?

— There are two main categories of research methods: qualitative research methods and quantitative research methods. Quantitative research methods involve using numbers to measure data. Researchers can use statistical analysis to find connections and meaning in the data. Quantitative research deals with numbers and statistics, while qualitative research deals with words and meanings.

4. What are the data collection method that is used in quantitative research? What are the data collection method that is used in qualitative research?

— Although there are many other methods to collect quantitative data, those mentioned above probability sampling, interviews, questionnaire observation, and document review are the most common and widely used methods either offline or for online data collection. Some of the most common qualitative data collection techniques include open-ended surveys and questionnaires, interviews, focus groups, observation, case studies, and so on.

24. enumerate the 10 parts of chapter 1 in research​


Answer:

The specific contents of Chapter 1 of a research paper can vary depending on the specific requirements and guidelines of the assignment or project. However, in general, Chapter 1 of a research paper is typically composed of the following parts:

Introduction: This section provides an overview of the research topic, including the background and context for the study.

Statement of the problem: This section identifies the main research question or problem that the study aims to address.

Purpose of the study: This section explains the specific goals and objectives of the research.

Significance of the study: This section discusses the potential contributions and implications of the research for the field or discipline.

Research questions: This section presents the specific research questions or hypotheses that the study aims to answer.

Scope and limitations: This section describes the boundaries and constraints of the study, such as the specific time period, geographic region, or population being studied.

Definition of terms: This section provides clear and concise definitions of key terms and concepts used in the study.

Review of the literature: This section provides a summary and analysis of relevant existing research on the topic.

Methodology: This section outlines the specific methods and procedures used to conduct the research, such as the research design, sample selection, data collection, and data analysis.

Organization of the study: This section provides an overview of the structure and organization of the rest of the research paper, including the chapters and sections that follow.

These are the general parts of Chapter 1 of a research paper, but the exact contents and organization may vary depending on the specific requirements and guidelines of the assignment or project.

27. when making the chapter one for a qualitative research, do you need to include a solution to your introduction?​


In qualitative research, the purpose of the introduction is to provide background information on the research topic, explain the importance of the research, and identify the research questions or objectives. It is not necessary to include a solution to the research problem in the introduction.

Qualitative research often focuses on exploring and understanding complex phenomena and experiences, rather than providing a specific solution to a problem. Therefore, the research questions or objectives are typically framed to guide the research process and to help the researcher gain a deeper understanding of the topic under investigation.

However, in some cases, qualitative research may aim to develop or test a theoretical framework, or to inform the development of interventions or policies. In these cases, the research may have a more practical or applied focus, and the introduction may include some discussion of potential solutions or implications of the research.

Overall, the content and structure of the introduction in a qualitative research study will depend on the specific research questions, objectives, and context of the study.
