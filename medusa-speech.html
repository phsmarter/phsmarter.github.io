Perseus

Greek mythology

BY The Editors of Encyclopaedia Britannica | View Edit History

Perseus, in Greek mythology, the slayer of the Gorgon Medusa and the rescuer of Andromeda from a sea monster. Perseus was the son of Zeus and Danaë, the daughter of Acrisius of Argos. As an infant he was cast into the sea in a chest with his mother by Acrisius, to whom it had been prophesied that he would be killed by his grandson. After Perseus had grown up on the island of Seriphus, where the chest had grounded, King Polydectes of Seriphus, who desired Danaë, tricked Perseus into promising to obtain the head of Medusa, the only mortal among the Gorgons.

Benvenuto Cellini: Perseus

Benvenuto Cellini: Perseus

Perseus, bronze sculpture by Benvenuto Cellini, 1545–54; in the Loggia dei Lanzi, Florence.

Alinari/Art Resource, New York

Aided by Hermes and Athena, Perseus pressed the Graiae, sisters of the Gorgons, into helping him by seizing the one eye and one tooth that the sisters shared and not returning them until they provided him with winged sandals (which enabled him to fly), the cap of Hades (which conferred invisibility), a curved sword, or sickle, to decapitate Medusa, and a bag in which to conceal the head. (According to another version, the Graiae merely directed him to the Stygian Nymphs, who told him where to find the Gorgons and gave him the bag, sandals, and helmet; Hermes gave him the sword.) Because the gaze of Medusa turned all who looked at her to stone, Perseus guided himself by her reflection in a shield given him by Athena and beheaded Medusa as she slept. He then returned to Seriphus and rescued his mother by turning Polydectes and his supporters to stone at the sight of Medusa’s head.

The Story of Medusa

The most well-known example of a gorgon in Greek mythology is Medusa. She was often portrayed as a winged female figure with a snake-covered head of hair, yet she was occasionally painted as being extraordinarily attractive, unlike the Gorgons. Perseus was able to kill Medusa because she was the only Gorgon who could die by having her head severed. Chrysaor and Pegasus, her two Poseidon-born kids, were born from the blood that spurted from her neck. The severed head was sent to Athena and placed in her shield; according to another legend, Perseus buried it in the Argos marketplace. The severed head had the power to turn anybody who looked at it into stone.

The town of Tegea is said to have been protected by Hercules by receiving a lock of Medusa's hair from Athena, which was said to have the same powers as the head, and giving it to Sterope, the daughter of Cepheus. When the lock was displayed, it was said to unleash a storm that drove the enemy away.

The Gorgon's Head

The story of Perseus, who was foretold to kill his grandfather and is renowned for killing the Gorgon Medusa, is told in this Greek myth. Acrisius, a king, was told by a prophecy that his grandson would kill him, leading him to put her lovely daughter Danae in prison. Since Zeus, the king of Olympus, fell in love with her and gave birth to a son named Perseus, she was kept away from people but not from the gods.

King Acrisius imprisoned Danae and Perseus inside a casket and sent it sailing on the sea after learning of the birth of his grandson. She was discovered by a fisherman whose brother had married Danae after falling in love with her. The now-husband wanted Perseus to kill the Gorgon Medusa since he didn't like him.

The gods' guidance for Perseus, he combated the gorgon. He came across a lovely woman imprisoned by the water during the struggle and freed her from the monster. Everyone, even the maiden's anticipated husband, disagreed when he requested her hand in marriage. Perseus used Gorgon's head in the ensuing fight, which he easily won.

He returned to confront his stepfather, who did not think Perseus killed Medusa. He made a statue out of his stepfather. When he eventually went back to his grandfather's country and unintentionally killed him during a throwing competition, the prophecy was realized.
