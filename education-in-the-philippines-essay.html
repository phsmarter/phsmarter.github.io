Is Online Education Relevant to the Philippine System?

Philippines is not prepared for online learning. According to the Department of Science and Technology, the Philippines ranks 83rd out of 183 countries in terms of digital readiness. This indicates that the Philippines is not technologically sophisticated (DOST).

During the quarantine in most of the Philippines, education officials have advocated using online platforms for the 2020-2021 school year to continue the education of millions of students. Online education offers virtual classes that a student can take at home alone or with a group via phone call or video conference using software such as Messenger, Google Zoom, or Skype. Online education necessitates the use of suitable technology, such as a smartphone that supports video calls and a stable internet connection.

However, the Philippines cannot support online education for the majority of its pupils, therefore the attempt will fail for the majority of the country. Due to poverty in the Philippines, not every family has the means to acquire the necessary equipment for online learning. These families simply cannot adapt to the needs of online learning and will be left behind or forced to find alternative means of education for their children if they are not provided with an alternative. In addition, internet speed in the Philippines is still bad, as our country's more than 7,000 islands make it impossible to build fixed networks. In addition, the process of constructing cell towers is arduous and time-consuming.

However, Online Education allows both the teacher and the student to set their own learning rate, as well as a schedule that accommodates everyone's schedule. In addition, it can assist us in being highly organized, self-motivated, and time-management savvy. Consequently, employing an online educational platform allows for a better work-study balance.

In conclusion, the Philippines requires a clear set of regulations and norms based on an innovative educational system. This necessitates a thorough and serious evaluation of the nation's readiness to offer learning programs with rigorous criteria. This comprises instructor capability, the learner's position and context, and the effectiveness of the learning environment. These are in addition to the more obvious difficulties of internet speed, material cost, and delivery method. The greatest approach to advance is to take a step back and develop a strategy that involves instructors, students, parents, school officials, and technology-based corporations.

I strongly believe that home economics has an extremely important place in our educational system today. No other academic discipline incorporates in its curriculum as many pertinent life skills that will help students succeed independent of their chosen career paths. Referring back to past studies and my own personal beliefs, I hope to help the reader understand the benefits of receiving an education in home economics and how the knowledge obtained through this program will prove valuable throughout the lifespan.

In my opinion, the most important aspect of a home economics education is that students not only learn about subject matter that has relevance to their present lives, but will constantly be of use as they continue to grow. One area of home economics that is considered to be among the most essential is the emphasis on personal development, decision making and intrapersonal skills. According to a study done in Japan, students’ personal initiatives play an extremely large role in determining how they’ll react to their changing work situations (1). Those who lack the ability to make effective personal decisions are more at risk for experiencing hardships in the instabilities found in the real world. It was also found in the study that students do not feel they have the proper means to learn these valuable life skills at home (1). Taking courses in home economics at school allows students to acquire the necessary decision making, social, and communications skills deemed critical for occupational success.

In addition to the development of valuable intrapersonal skills, the home economics curriculum also introduces the students to a wide variety of potential career paths. Students become aware of all the career opportunities relating to each domain, as well as being taught the skills associated with them. Those who find themselves intrigued by course material may begin to consider an occupation in a related area. The home economics discipline has led individuals to the fields of education, nutrition, social service, and hospitality management, to name a few (8). It is hard to find a single subject taught in schools today that incorporates as many topics of interest as home economics, helping students to become more well-rounded individuals.

Consumer-related material covered in home economics courses is another area that provides students with information pertinent to their lives as adults. These programs help supply the students with an understanding on how economic, social, and cultural factors personally affect them and their behaviors in consumerism (5). While other academic courses may cover theoretical aspects personal finance, home economics gives a more practical, everyday application of the subject matter. However, there have been some criticisms to how useful learning about consumerism as adolescents actually is. Research on Finnish students showed that motivation to study consumerism in school was not particularly high, because they felt true consumerism started at adulthood (2). Despite the attitudes of this sample, I do believe children in home economics programs can benefit from learning purchasing practices at a young age. Because of its real-world applications, students may find these techniques conducive to their everyday lives.

20. Write a short essay on the following: 1. Condition of science education in the Philippines.


Answer:

Science education in the Philippines is in a state of disrepair. The high-stakes testing culture and lack of resources for teaching science have resulted in poor performance on standardized tests and a lack of interest in the subject among students. There are also issues with the curriculum and teacher training. Schools lack the proper equipment and materials to effectively teach science, and there is a large gap between what is taught in the classroom and what is needed in the workplace. Additionally, the Philippines has one of the lowest student-to-teacher ratios and the lowest spending per student in the entire ASEAN region.

Overall, the condition of science education in the Philippines is dismal. With limited resources and inadequate teaching methods, students have difficulty understanding the concepts they need to be successful in their future careers. It is imperative that the government invest in science education in order to create an educated, productive workforce that can compete with the rest of the world. Only then will the Philippines be able to move forward and reach its full potential.
