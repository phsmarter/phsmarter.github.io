8. CANCY B. Using the table below, think of other types of barriers that you have encountered while communicating. Site an example situation and give a specific solution. BARRIER EXAMPLE SOLUTION​


Answer:

Here are some examples of barriers to communication and possible solutions:

Barrier: Physical distance

Example: You are trying to communicate with a colleague who is working remotely and you are having difficulty hearing them over the phone.

Solution: Use a video conferencing platform to allow for visual communication and to help with auditory clarity.

Barrier: Language differences

Example: You are trying to communicate with a client who speaks a different language and you do not have a common language in which to communicate.

Solution: Use a translation app or hire a translator to facilitate the conversation.

Barrier: Disagreements or conflicts

Example: You are trying to communicate with a coworker about a project, but you are not seeing eye to eye on certain aspects of the work.

Solution: Take a step back and try to understand each other's perspectives, use active listening techniques to clarify misunderstandings, and consider finding a compromise or a different approach that works for both parties.

Barrier: Noise or distractions

Example: You are trying to communicate with a client in a busy coffee shop and it is difficult to hear each other over the background noise.

Solution: Find a quieter location to continue the conversation or use earplugs or noise-cancelling headphones to reduce the distractions.

Barrier: Lack of attention or engagement

Example: You are giving a presentation to a group of people, but some of the attendees are not paying attention or are multitasking on their phones.

Solution: Use engaging visual aids and try to involve the audience in the discussion, ask questions and encourage participation, and consider breaking up the presentation into shorter, more interactive segments to keep everyone's attention.

answer

Metals:

1. Metals are located on the left of the periodic table, and nonmetals are located on the upper right.

2. Metals are lustrous, good conductors, high melting points, high density, malleable, ductile, and usually solid.

3. Aluminium, copper, and iron are among the examples of metals.

Nonmetals:

1. The non-metals or non-metallic elements; hydrogen (H), carbon (C), nitrogen (N), oxygen (O), phosphorus (P), sulphur (sulfer) (S), selenium (Se), (Uuo may belong here) and the noble gases form a relatively small group with a step like pattern towards the left-hand side of the periodic table.

2. Nonmetals are usually dull, poor conductors, non-ductile, brittle, and may be solids, liquids or gases at room temperature.

3. Hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen are nonmetals.

Metalloids:

1. Metalloids are located to the right of the post-transition metals and to the left of the non-metals.

2. Metalloids tend to be shiny like metals, but brittle like nonmetals.

3. The commonly recognised metalloids are boron, silicon, germanium.

30. Directions: fill the concept web with at least three facts about metals, non metals, and mettaloids.Put in the first line their position in the periodic table, the secod line their physical property and in the third line three specific examples​


Explanation:

METALS

A metal is a material that, when freshly prepared, polished, or fractured, shows a lustrous appearance, and conducts electricity and heat relatively well. Metals are typically ductile and malleable. These properties are the result of the metallic bond between the atoms or molecules of the metal.

NONMETALS

In chemistry, a nonmetal is a chemical element that generally lacks a predominance of metallic properties; they range from colorless gases to shiny solids.

METALLOIDS

A metalloid is a type of chemical element which has a preponderance of properties in between, or that are a mixture of, those of metals and nonmetals. There is no standard definition of a metalloid and no complete agreement on which elements are metalloids.
