Essay Example - The Philippines as a Developing CountryA country is classified as part of developing countries when they fall under a high rate of population growth, low per capita income, primary sector dependence, high unemployment rates, and dependency on exports of primary commodities. That being said, most countries listed as developing countries belong to Asia, such as China, South Korea, Taiwan, India, Singapore, Indonesia, Thailand, the Philippines, and Malaysia.Even if they are on their way to growth, developing countries face more challenges along the way. The most difficult are decreasing poverty rates, access to quality education, and a good healthcare system for all its citizens. Third-world countries, especially those in Southeast Asia, are experiencing these challenges. One of them is the Philippines. The roots of the problems in the Philippines economy can be traced back to World War II, when soldiers from Japan occupied the country. The Philippines' worst inflation was then recorded in history.The Philippines struggled again with its economic growth in recent years during the Marcos era of Martial Law due, stemming from the negative image attached to the country. Marcos aimed to lead the country for consecutive terms and use the country's resources following his agenda. Oppositions against the administration then rose. This included Benigno Aquino Jr, a Filipino journalist, and politician who was assassinated right as he landed at Manila International Airport (now named Ninoy Aquino International Airport or NAIA). His death ignited the distrust of its citizens to its government. The Philippines' debt in the world bank increased from $600 million to $26 billion during the Marcos regime. The debt has caused macroeconomic effects in the Philippines, with the country continuing to pay for this debt using the current national income and remittances. At present, the Philippines is continuously facing a high population rate and low gross domestic product (GDP). Millions of Filipinos are still suffering from poverty and struggling with exporting their products up where inflow is greater than outflow of commodities within the country.

4. 3. What makes Philippines a vicinity of different kinds of calamities every year?A. Philippines is an archipelago. C. Philippines is a developing country.B. Philippines is a small country. D. Philippines is facing the Atlantic Ocean.​


What makes Philippines a vicinity of different kinds of calamities every year? A. Philippines is an archipelago. C. Philippines is a developing country. B. Philippines is a small country. D. Philippines is facing the Atlantic Ocean.

The answer is letter A. Philippines is an archipelago.   The Philippines is an archipelago territory with roughly 7,100 islands and islets and a land area of approximately 300,000 km2. The nation is divided into three groups or vast islands: the Luzon region, which includes Luzon, Mindoro, and Palawan in the north and west;  the Visaya group, which includes Bohol, Cebu, Leyte, Masbate, Negros, Panay, and Samar in the center; and  Mindanao in the south. The country's most populated cities, Manila and neighboring Quezon City, are part of the National Capital Region (NRC or Metro Manila), which is situated on the largest island, Luzon (Cullinane, 2019). As of 2018, the islands and groups are split into four major administrative divisions: 17 autonomous regions, 81 provinces, 1,489 municipalities, and the smallest political entities, 42,044 Barangays (PSA, 2018). The Philippines is governed by a presidential system of government in which authority is split among three juridical branches: administrative, legislative, and judiciary, all of which pursue democracy and balance by carrying out their evenly weighted duties to defend justice, freedom, and people's interests (GoP, 2019). According to the World Risk Report 2018, the Philippines ranked third among all countries with the highest catastrophe risk, with an index value of 25.14 percent (World Economic Forum, 2018). At least 60% of the country's total land area is vulnerable to multiple hazards, This is primarily due to the area and geological background, when coastal threats such as typhoons, storm waves, and increasing sea levels pose a significant danger. Furthermore, since the islands are situated on the “Ring of Fire” between the Eurasian and Pacific tectonic plates, earthquakes and volcanoes pose significant threats to the populace's survival. Flooding, landslides, droughts, and tsunamis add to the risk of natural disasters (CFE-DM, 2018). Hydro-meteorological phenomena, such as typhoons and flooding, accounted for more than 80% of natural disasters in the world over the last half-century (Jha, 2018).

18. is improved infrastructure in developing countries achievable in the Philippines?​


Is improved infrastructure in developing countries achievable in the Philippines?

Yes, improved infrastructure in developing countries like the Philippines is achievable. However, it requires a significant effort from the government, private sector, and the people. The government must prioritize infrastructure development by allocating sufficient funds and creating policies that promote public-private partnerships. The private sector must also invest in infrastructure projects and collaborate with the government to ensure their success.

Moreover, the people must support infrastructure development by paying their taxes, following regulations, and participating in community initiatives. They must also hold their leaders accountable and demand transparency and accountability in infrastructure projects.

Some of the key areas that need improvement in the Philippines include transportation, water and sanitation, energy, and telecommunications. Addressing these issues will require a sustained effort over several years, but the benefits will be significant, including improved economic growth, job creation, and better quality of life for Filipinos.

30. Explain how food security will help the Philippines develop as a country.


Answer:

According to the FAO, food security exists when all people at all times have physical, social and economic access to sufficient, safe and nutritious food to meet their dietary needs and food preferences for an active and health life.

People living in poverty often cannot produce or buy enough food to eat and so are more susceptible to disease. ... Food security is also seen as a prerequisite for economic development. Losses in labour productivity due to hunger can cause 6-10% reduction in per capita gross domestic product (GDP)

Food security is often defined in terms of food availability, food access and food utilization (USAID 1995). Improved access to food-thr ough increased agricultural productivity and incomes-is essential to meet the food needs of the world's growing population.
