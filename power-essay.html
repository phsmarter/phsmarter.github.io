The EDSA People Power Revolution is a significant event in the history of the Philippines. It was a peaceful revolution that took place from February 22-25, 1986, which led to the ousting of President Ferdinand Marcos and the restoration of democracy in the country.

The revolution was sparked by the results of the snap presidential elections held on February 7, 1986. The results showed that Marcos had won the elections, but allegations of fraud and irregularities led to massive protests from opposition groups, including the Catholic Church, civil society organizations, and the military.

The protests culminated in a historic event that became known as the EDSA People Power Revolution. Hundreds of thousands of people gathered along EDSA, the main thoroughfare in Metro Manila, to protest against Marcos and his regime. The protesters came from all walks of life, including religious leaders, students, workers, and the middle class.

The protests were peaceful, and the military, under the leadership of Defense Minister Juan Ponce Enrile and General Fidel Ramos, joined the protesters in their call for change. The military's support was crucial, as it provided protection for the protesters and helped to ensure that the demonstrations remained peaceful.

After four days of protests, Marcos was forced to flee the country, and Corazon Aquino, the widow of opposition leader Benigno Aquino Jr., was installed as the new president. The EDSA People Power Revolution became a symbol of the power of nonviolent resistance and the triumph of democracy over authoritarianism.

The EDSA People Power Revolution has had a lasting impact on the Philippines and the world. It served as an inspiration for other peaceful revolutions in other parts of the world, including in Eastern Europe and Latin America. It also paved the way for the restoration of democracy in the Philippines, leading to the drafting of a new constitution and the establishment of democratic institutions.

In conclusion, the EDSA People Power Revolution is a testament to the power of ordinary people to effect change and the importance of upholding democracy and human rights. It is a reminder that through peaceful and nonviolent means, people can work together to create a better future for themselves and for their country

The process of transmitting electric power involves the conversion of mechanical energy to electrical energy, which is then transported through a network of power lines and transformers to homes and businesses. In this essay, we will explore the fundamental principles of electromagnetism and their application in the transmission of electric power.

Electricity is generated at the power plant by rotating turbines that are driven by steam or water. These turbines are connected to generators, which convert the mechanical energy of the turbines into electrical energy. The generators consist of a rotating coil of wire, called the rotor, and a stationary coil of wire, called the stator. The stator is surrounded by a series of powerful magnets, which create a magnetic field that induces an electrical current in the rotor coil. As the rotor rotates, it produces a changing magnetic field, which in turn generates an alternating current in the stator coil.

The alternating current produced by the generator is then transported to the high-voltage transmission lines, which carry the power to substations located closer to the consumer. These transmission lines are typically made of aluminum or copper and are designed to minimize energy loss during the transfer of power. The flow of electricity through the transmission lines creates a magnetic field around the wires. This magnetic field is responsible for the interaction between the transmission lines and the surrounding environment, which can lead to power losses and other issues.

At the substations, the high-voltage electricity is transformed into lower-voltage electricity, which is then distributed to the consumer. This process involves the use of transformers, which are devices that use the principles of electromagnetism to change the voltage of the electricity. Transformers work by inducing a magnetic field in a coil of wire, which is then used to transfer energy to a second coil of wire. The voltage of the electricity is determined by the ratio of the number of turns in the two coils. For example, a transformer with a primary coil of 100 turns and a secondary coil of 10 turns would reduce the voltage of the electricity by a factor of 10.

Once the voltage of the electricity has been reduced, it is distributed to homes and businesses through a network of power lines and transformers. The electricity is consumed by various devices, such as lights, refrigerators, and computers, which convert the electrical energy into other forms of energy, such as light, heat, and motion.

In conclusion, electromagnetism is a crucial component of the transmission of electric power from the power plant to the consumer. The EDSA People's Power Revolution, also known as the EDSA Revolution or simply People Power, was a series of non-violent protests and demonstrations that took place in the Philippines from February 22 to 25, 1986. The revolution resulted in the overthrow of the authoritarian regime of President Ferdinand Marcos, and the restoration of democracy in the Philippines.

The EDSA Revolution was triggered by the disputed results of the presidential election held on February 7, 1986, in which Marcos claimed victory over his opponent, Corazon Aquino. However, the opposition accused Marcos of cheating and electoral fraud, and massive protests erupted in the streets of Manila and other major cities.

The protests quickly grew in size and intensity, and soon included a broad coalition of civil society groups, religious organizations, and ordinary citizens from all walks of life. The protesters used a variety of creative tactics to express their discontent, such as forming human chains, singing patriotic songs, and waving flags and banners.

One of the most iconic moments of the EDSA Revolution was the "Yellow Revolution," in which thousands of people wore yellow ribbons to show their support for Aquino, whose campaign color was yellow. This symbol of unity and hope became a powerful tool for the protesters to rally around and express their desire for change.

Despite the peaceful nature of the protests, the Marcos regime responded with violence and repression. The military and police were ordered to disperse the protesters and suppress the demonstrations, leading to several instances of brutality and bloodshed.

However, the courage and determination of the protesters eventually proved too much for the regime to handle. On February 25, 1986, Marcos fled the country with his family and close allies, ending his 21-year rule over the Philippines.

The EDSA Revolution was a remarkable achievement for the Filipino people, who showed the world that change can be achieved through peaceful means. It was a triumph of democracy over dictatorship, and a testament to the power of collective action and solidarity.

24. ESSAY1. Differentiate animal power from tractor power. (10 points)217hot on 1​


Answer:

The environmental impacts of moving to different traction power sources have not been well studied in either developing countries or modern agriculture. This study assesses the environmental impact of the transition from animal to tractor power and vice versa. Three different scenarios are analyzed: a developing country context where draught animal power (DAP) is actually in use; a similar context where DAP is replaced by mechanical traction; and a developed country scenario where mechanical traction is replaced by DAP. The impact assessment focuses on global warming (GW) and primary energy consumption (PEC). The DAP system refers to an Indonesian case study where Peranakan Ongole cattle are used for work, together with the production of milk and meat, and fed with two forage-supplying alternatives. The mechanical traction system considers the cases of a two-wheel tractor (2WT) and a rear-wheel-drive tractor (4WT). In the first scenario, assessing DAP impacts for plowing through process subdivision and indirect impacts allocation to co-products, DAP has a consistent advantage regarding energy consumption, while for CO2eq emissions, performance is better only in the case of 2WT. In the second scenario, considering product equivalent systems, substituting DAP with tractor power and maintaining the same DAP livestock farming more than doubles the impact; only with the introduction of modern, specialized livestock farming is a reduction achieved. In the third scenario, replacing mechanical traction with DAP results in a greater than three-fold increase in impact for PEC and more than 17 times increase for GW. Further work on the influence of system definition and the socio-economic background would improve the estimates produced in this study.
