4. What are the specific clues that most clearly differentiates a Darwinian explanation from a Lamarckian explanation?​


Darwin’s theory was that species are the result of natural selection acting on the variation of phenotypes in a species. Finches might have beaks from skinny to strong, but if thick shelled seeds are the most available food, then over time there will be a species of finches with strong beaks, and the finches with skinny beaks might disappear. Darwin argued that based on how all life appeared to be related in how their bodies were structured, it’s conceivable that natural selection acting on variation in a species could have produced all of the variety of life on Earth.

Lamarck held with an older idea, that it was the needs of individuals of a species that caused their offspring to change. An animal that needed to constantly stretch its neck to reach higher leaves would have offspring with longer necks, and eventually there’d be giraffes.

So the clear differentiation between Lamarck and Darwin is that Lamarck believed that evolution was the result of individuals striving to survive, while Darwin argued (and had the specimen collection to illustrate it) that evolution was the result of the environment acting on the entire population of a species.

The modern theory of evolution has a lot more information that neither Lamarck nor Darwin had, including genetics, and more recently epigenetics.

Genetics makes variability possible, and it is also what results in selecting for the best characteristics as individuals mix genes and produce offspring.

But epigenetics sounds almost as if Lamarck might have at least had a small point. It turns out that some genes can be turned on or off due to a parent’s environment, and that gene, turned on or off, will be passed along to the offspring. It’s not quite what Lamarck was thinking, but I’m willing to give him a nod.

7. diffenation process of evolution (lamarckian Darwinian evolution)​


Answer:

Lamarckian Darwinian evolution refers to the two major theories regarding the process of evolution. Lamarckian evolution, proposed by Jean-Baptiste Lamarck, suggests that organisms can acquire new traits or characteristics during their lifetime and pass them on to future generations. This theory suggests that environmental factors can directly influence an organism's evolution. On the other hand, Darwinian evolution, proposed by Charles Darwin, states that evolution occurs through natural selection, where organisms with advantageous traits have a higher chance of survival and reproduction, leading to the gradual accumulation of beneficial traits in a population over time. This process is driven by random genetic variations and the pressures of the environment.

Compare Lamarckian vs. Darwinian Evolution

The key distinction between "Darwinian" and "Lamarckian" evolutionary mechanisms is that the former emphasizes random, undirected variation, whereas the latter is based on variation induced directly by an environmental signal and resulting in a specific reaction to that cue. Neither Lamarck nor Darwin understood the mechanisms of heritable variation emergence and fixation. As a result, it was very easy for Lamarck to entertain the notion that phenotypic variation translates directly into heritable (what we now refer to as genetic or genomic) alterations. We now understand that the strict Lamarckian scenario is extremely difficult to achieve since a molecular mechanism must exist for the influence of a phenotypic alteration to be channeled into a genomic modification (mutation).

Lamarck's theory of evolution was based on how organisms (such as animals and plants) change through time and pass those changes down to their descendants. For example, Lamarck argues that the giraffe had a long neck because it stretched its neck to reach leaves in high-up trees during its lifetime, implying that each generation of giraffe had a longer neck than previous generations.

Natural selection, Darwin's theory, held that organisms had variation (each individual being slightly different from the next), and that these variations led to some organisms being more likely to survive and reproduce than others. Features that let an organism survive or reproduce are more likely to exist in subsequent generations.
