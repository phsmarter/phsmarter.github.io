Primary Emotions and Secondary Emotions

1.Cheerful-Secondary emotion2.Suffering-Secondary emotion3.Irritabilty-Secondary emotion4.Fear-Primary emotion5.Nervousness-Secondary emotion

Emotions are a person's feelings when they are in a situation or are involved in an interaction. Therefore, emotions are not just feelings that suddenly appear, but emotions arise because of an interaction.

Robert Plutchick divides emotions into primary and secondary emotions. The understanding of primary emotions is the same as that proposed by Santrock, except that Robert Plutchick added secondary emotions which are a combination of more than one basic emotion. Robert Plutchick also put forward the wheel of emotions.

By knowing the types of human emotions, the term emotion regulation appears, which is the ability to monitor, evaluate, and modify emotional reactions to achieve goals. Someone who is able to express emotions appropriately can change himself and even the environment for the better.

Providing an understanding of the types of emotions and regulation to the target community (participants) aims to understand more deeply the importance of being aware of and concerned about controlling individual emotions.

Apart from that, there is also a need for knowledge so that people are able to understand this for the benefit of psychological health, such as excessive anxiety. This is very vulnerable, especially during this pandemic. As is known, in the current situation, with various problems that arise, it is not uncommon for someone to be more easily aroused by emotions and less able to think rationally.

23. It is the type of camera shot where the subject’s smallest details are closely seen. a. Medium shot b. Close-up shot c. Wide shot d. Extreme close-up


Answer:

d. EXTREME CLOSE-UP

An extreme close-up shot frames a subject very closely, typically to the point where the subject's margins are cut off by the frame's edges. This is typically used on actors to show certain parts of their bodies, such as the face or hips, but it can also be used to display simply the actor's mouth or even a single eye.

Inanimate objects can also be framed in an extreme close-up photograph, but the scale and size of the object must be considered. If you were to photograph a steaming teapot up close, you might choose to focus on the spout or the base. The concept is that you can't see the whole thing, so you're forced to concentrate on a little part of it, hopefully for the best impact.

Extreme close-up shots are employed for b-roll, inserts, and the occasional establishing shot in addition to performers. To put it another way, there are a lot of ways to use this shot, and having it in your director's toolbox will help you be a better filmmaker.
