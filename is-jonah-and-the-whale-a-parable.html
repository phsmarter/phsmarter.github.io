Answer and explanation

B. the parable of Christ's death and resurrection

Some early Christian interpreted the story of Jonah as a prelude to the death and resurrection of Jesus Christ. The use of Jonah and the sea creature was often a motif for tombs and sarcophagus of rich Christians, along with other prophets of the old testaments like Daniel and the Lions, the three Hebrews in a Furnace and, Moses hitting the Rock.

Additional Information

However, this isn't a parable to the Death and Resurrection of Jesus Christ. The account of Jonah and the people of Nineveh is all about the mercy and grace of God. The Lord is willing to give a second chance to those who have truly repented.

Synopsis of Jonah's account

Jonah was a prophet who disobeyed God by running away from God's presence. God sent Jonah to go to Nineveh and warn the people there to repent their sins because of God's upcoming judgment on them. Jonah disobeyed God because he didn't like the Assyrians and he thought that the people of Nineveh deserve God's Judgement so, He fled on a ship to Tarshish. God caused a storm on the seas and the ship's crew blamed Jonah for it. So, they threw Jonah into the sea and He was swallowed by a huge sea creature. While Inside the stomach of the sea beast, Jonah prayed and repented to God asking for a second chance. Three days later, the sea creatures spat out Jonah into dry land. Eventually, the people of Nineveh (including the King) turned away from their wicked culture and repented. Thus, they were spared from destruction and judgment from the Lord.

8. Can we really live inside a whale like jonah did on the bible?


From a scientific perspective, the anatomy and behavior of whales make it impossible for a human to survive inside them for any length of time. Whales are marine mammals that have powerful digestive systems and are not designed to carry passengers. The stomach of a whale is a highly acidic environment, which would quickly kill a human if they were swallowed. Additionally, the lack of oxygen and fresh water would make it impossible for a human to survive for more than a few minutes inside a whale's body.

16. God's word for my world to study the life of the prophet Jonah in Jonah 1-2 ​


Answer:

Jonah 1 – Jonah Runs from God

A. Jonah’s attempted escape.

1. (1-2) God’s call to Jonah.

Now the word of the LORD came to Jonah the son of Amittai, saying, “Arise, go to Nineveh, that great city, and cry out against it; for their wickedness has come up before Me.”

a. The word of the LORD came to Jonah: God spoke to Jonah in His own unique and powerful way and He told Jonah to do two things. First, go to Nineveh; second, cry out against it – that is, to rebuke them for their sin and call them to repentance.

b. Go to Nineveh: The city of Nineveh was the capital of the Assyrian Empire and was a large and prominent city in its day. It was not a city of Israel at all; God called Jonah to go to a pagan, Gentile city and call them to repentance.

i. Ancient historians say that Nineveh was the largest city in the world at that time. It was the large, important capital of a dominating empire – surely an intimidating place to go.

c. For their wickedness has come up before Me: God wanted Jonah to go because He saw their wickedness. None of man’s wickedness is hidden before God. He sees it all, and it may come to a point where it demands the specific warning and judgment of God.

2. (3) Jonah’s attempt to flee from God’s call.

But Jonah arose to flee to Tarshish from the presence of the LORD. He went down to Joppa, and found a ship going to Tarshish; so he paid the fare, and went down into it, to go with them to Tarshish from the presence of the LORD.

a. But Jonah arose to flee: Jonah was a reluctant prophet. He didn’t want to do what God told him to do. Several reasons for this have been suggested.

The Parable of the Sower (sometimes called the Parable of the Soils) is a parable of Jesus found in Matthew 13:1–23, Mark 4:1–20, Luke 8:4–15 and the extra-canonical Gospel of Thomas.[1]

Jesus tells of a farmer who sows seed indiscriminately. Some seed falls on the path (wayside) with no soil, some on rocky ground with little soil, some on soil which contains thorns, and some on good soil. In the first case, the seed is taken away; in the second and third soils, the seed fails to produce a crop; but when it falls on good soil, it grows and yields thirty-, sixty-, or a hundred-fold
