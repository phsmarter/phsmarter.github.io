3. what point of view is used in the poem telephone conversation?


This poem by Wole Soyinka is in the point of view of the African American man who is inquiring about a lease for a house. The person he is speaking with is the landlady of the said house that he wanted to lease.

 At the beginning, he is already interested in renting the house, so he is thinking of going there to see it for himself. In the poem, he says, “I hate a wasted journey - I am African.” He says this to admit that he is not Caucasian and that he does not want to travel to visit the house only to be rejected. 

The landlady asks if he is dark or very light which shows her prejudice towards darker-colored Africans. He replies that he is West African Sepia that confuses the landlady and shows her impatience by asking him what it means. He says that he is dark, but his palms and soles were white, or as he mentions in the poem, peroxide blonde. Sensing that the landlady plans to hang up the phone, he pleads that she should see him to see his skin color herself.

This poem’s theme is racism against the Africans by the Caucasians.  Unfortunately, even now, there is still discrimination based on the color of one’s skin.

13. What is the attitude of the landlady towards the speaker in the poem telephone conversation?


The poem ‘Telephone Conversation’ by Wole Soyinka is a satirical look on racism, particularly against Africans with a dark skin color.


The attitude of the landlady in the poem telephone conversation towards the speaker was normal from the first to the fifth line of the poem, normal in the sense that it is a typical business conversation of strangers. The landlady answered questions about the apartment’s location and rental price directly or to the point. She even sweetened the deal by promising less interferance from her because she does not live in the building. But by the sixth line, after the reveal of her potential renter’s race, her attitude quickly changed to prejudice against blacks. It can be argued that her discrimination is for the benefit of her other tenants, people who may have wanted no black neighbors, but she makes the rules for all tenants and only her decision matters. Her skin color questions in the succeding lines are all in uppercase, which suggests shouting or an increase in volume at the least. These telephone conversation questions and answers reveal how cold and exacting the landlady’s attitude became. She will not hesitate to refuse rental just on the basis of exact skin color. By line 25, her accent changed to probably annoyance at the expectation that this business opportunity will not happen. At the end, the speaker’s joke about his “bottom raven black” may have increased the irritation of the landlady to the point that she hangs up the phone. That is maybe the author’s intention on ending the poem in this manner, besides the humorous intention.
