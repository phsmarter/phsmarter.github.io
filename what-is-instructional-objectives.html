6. What is the difference between instructional objectives and learning targets?​


Answer:

How is a Learning Target different that an Objective? Learning Targets convey to students the destination for the lesson – what to learn, how deeply to learn it, and exactly how to demonstrate their new learning. Objectives are for teachers; learning targets are for students.

Answer:

This experiment is quite similar to the hide-and-seek game. The difference is that instead of people, you will going to find a missing object.

It's like, I hid an object, let's say, a ping-pong ball in a certain part of the house. Now, I instructed you to find it. To help you, I gave you a knife board.

Now, this comes the fun part. You have to guess where I put the ping-pong ball with the knife board as a hint.

An idea came to you. The KITCHEN! You went to the kitchen and tried to look for the mischievous ball. Then, you remembered that your mom usually uses the knife board near the kitchen sink. So, you proceed to look in there and surprisingly find the ping-pong ball in a small plastic basket together with the dishwashing paste and sponge!

After this little experiment, go back to your activity/homework and answer the questions.

Q1. Were you able to find the object? Was it easy or difficult?

The answer here may vary depending on your experience. You may answer this as "Yes, I was able to find it, and it's easy" or "Yes, I was able to find it but it was difficult" or "No, I could not find it at all. It was too difficult."

Q2. Is the instruction clear and easy to follow? What made it so?

The answer here may vary. Just honestly tell your experience here.

In the next part... #3, you were asked to revise the instructions to find the object, the ping-pong ball in our example. You can make it more exciting by putting it in your backyard just behind those sweet-smelling pink flowers. Be creative in writing the instructions. If it were me, I will probably write "Location: Behind the pots of greens and flours". The "greens" here pertains to the leaves that are green in color and the "flours" is the homophone of flowers.

When you and your family members are done looking for the ball, you can proceed answering the other questions.

Q3. Were they successful in finding the object? Was it easy for them or difficult?

The answer may vary. Just write your experience.

Q4. What other details or information was included in your instruction that made it clearer and easier to follow?

The answer may vary depending on the information in the instruction you have given to your family member. Did you provide...

Q5. In your own words, what is a point of reference? How important it is?​

This is the conclusion of your game experiment. In the first part (#1 and #2), you used an object to locate the ping-pong ball. This was your reference point to find the missing ball. In #3, your reference point is the instruction you have given for your family member to locate the missing object. In my case, the example I had given, my reference point there is the pots of flowers. From that, we could say that a point of reference is a point (or something/object) we could use to compare with or describe the position of an object.

Additional Info

This is an example of a reference point.

Suppose you are on the earth looking up to the sun while standing. You would say, "The sun is moving." You could say that because your reference point is the earth. But, if your reference point is the sun (it means it's like you are standing on it), you would be saying, "The earth is moving". (see attached pic)
