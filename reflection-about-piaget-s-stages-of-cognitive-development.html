Piaget’s theory of cognitive development:

According to Jean Piaget's theory of cognitive development, children's intellect evolves with age.

A kid's cognitive growth involves more than simply knowledge acquisition; the youngster also has to create or develop a mental picture of the world.

The teacher, according to Piaget, should encourage pupils to think critically by giving them engaging learning opportunities and tools. Individual and student-centered learning, formative assessment, active learning, discovery learning, and peer interaction have all been impacted by his approach.

Our knowledge of children's intellectual development has been aided by Piaget's theory of cognitive development. It also emphasized the fact that kids weren't just passive sponges for information.

Instead, as they develop their awareness of how the world functions, children are continually trying and discovering.

You may assist students who are struggling with learning and adjustment by using developmental knowledge. Developmental psychology may assist you in recognizing the various factors that influence behavior in your students as well as growth, maturation, learning, and development.

11. What is the 3rd stage of Piaget's Cognitive Development Theory?​


Answer:

Background and Key Concepts of Piaget's Theory

Sensorimotor stage: birth to 2 years. Preoperational stage: ages 2 to 7. Concrete operational stage: ages 7 to 11. Formal operational stage: ages 12 and up.

Concrete operational stage. The concrete operational stage is the third stage of Piaget's theory of cognitive development. This stage, which follows the preoperational stage, occurs between the ages of 7 and 11 (middle childhood and preadolescence) years, and is characterized by the appropriate use of logic.

Piaget has identified four primary stages of development: sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, and formal operational. In the sensorimotor stage, an infant's mental and cognitive attributes develop from birth until the appearance of language.

21. Differentiate Piaget and Vygostsky's views on cognitive development.​


Answer:

Vygotsky believed that the child is a social being, and cognitive development is led by social interactions. Piaget, on the other hand, felt that the child was more independent and that development was guided by self-centered, focused activities.
