On their banks flowers soon grew, and to the streams people came to fetch water for their various needs. Source: The Origin of Tapuy and Other Cordillera Tales, Rosella Camte-Banhi,et.al pp.42-43​


Answer:

This Ibaloy tale is structured in a traditional folktale format, beginning with the introduction of two blind women who are poor and must beg for food. The plot then follows their journey to the next village, where they encounter a magical rock that opens up and reveals an old woman who gives them a sack of rice and bottle of water that will never be empty or drained dry. The story ends with the two women sharing their good fortune with others by sowing some of the rice and pouring out some of the water, which turns into small streams.

The point of view is third person omniscient, as it allows readers to understand what is happening from both characters' perspectives. The major characters are the two blind women, while minor characters include the old woman from within the rock and those in their village who benefit from their generosity.

The actions taken by these characters include walking to another village, bumping into a large rock, being given food by an old woman inside it, receiving gifts from her (a sack of rice and bottle of water), returning home happily together afterwards, sowing some grain from one's sack and pouring out some water from one's bottle so that it turns into small streams for others to use.

Literary devices used in this story include imagery (the hilly paths they walk on) as well as personification (the rock opening magically). These devices help create meaning by providing vivid descriptions that bring life to this tale about how kindness can be rewarded through generosity towards others.
