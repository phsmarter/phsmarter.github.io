15. What is ontological truth and cite one example.​


Answer:

Ontology is the study of being. It focuses on several related questions:

What things exist? (stars yes, unicorns no, numbers . . . yes?)

What categories do they belong to? (are numbers physical properties or just ideas?)

Is there such a thing as objective reality?

What does the verb “to be” mean?

Some of these questions may seem painfully abstract and not very useful, but they are and always have been enormously important to some philosophers, especially to those who believe in foundationalism.

22. Similarities and differences between anselm and descarte


Answer:

The greatest similarity is that both start from ideally conceived notions, as opposed to arguments from reality like those by Aristotle and Aquinas. The main difference is that Anselm abided by the scholastic premise that the human mind comes into existence as a blank slate (tamquam tabula rasa), and therefore, the idea of a supremely perfect being enters the mind a posteriori, even though I don't believe he explains how that happens. Descartes instead, discards the blank slate scholastic idea and decides, with very insufficient explanation, that the human mind comes into existence with certain self-evident innate ideas, and one of them is the idea of a supremely perfect being. To be sure, Descartes was a man of faith, of religious faith, but with a pathological need for absolute certainty (probably a type of certainty that only a perfect, divine being can possess), and probably because he was gifted with a brilliant mind, he thought he could conceive of free of error conclusions using the philosophical method of doubting of absolutely everything (la doute méthodique) allowing himself to doubt even his own existence, thus giving life to the skeptic school of philosophy that still endures, with catastrophic consequences. Once he allowed to doubt of his own existence, the door was wide open to doubt the existence of God. He solved the problem of skepticism about his own existence by resorting to the famous “I think, therefore I am” (cogito, ergo sum). But he could not apply the same conclusion to God and that's how he comes up with the concept of innate ideas, God being one of them. His skeptic followers caught the glitch in his thought, and that's why they are all atheists

Aquinas's first demonstration of God's existence is the argument from motion. He drew from Aristotle's observation that each thing in the universe that moves is moved by something else. Aristotle sometimes called this prime mover “God.” Aquinas understood it as the God of Christianity.
