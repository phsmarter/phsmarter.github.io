Beowulf and Grendel’s Tale

Beneath the legend lies the tale

Why was it harder for Beowulf to kill Grendel's mother than Grendel himself?

Grendel's mother was a monster that belonged in an undersea marsh. Grendel's mother seeks retribution because of the death that resulted from the loss of his arm.

Grendel and his mother are both evil; they are both products of hell. Grendel's mother is a stronger monster than Grendel as well, as Grendel may be slain by Beowulf's sword but not Grendel's mother.

Beowulf finds a huge magic sword in the cave and uses it to slay the mother, despite the fact that his sword, Hunting, which Unferth had loaned to him, was unable to pierce the mother's hide. Beowulf wields the sword to decapitate the corpse of Grendel that lies nearby, and the sword melts to its hilt.

Beowulf is defended this time by his armor as she attempts to stab him to death with a dagger. After managing to knock her off of himself, Beowulf spots a huge sword and seizes it. This sword features a lovely embellished handle and a blade that give away the fact that it is not your typical sword.

Along the route, Beowulf and his thanes advance. They run into the thane Wulfgar when they arrive at Hrothgar's fortress. Beowulf introduces himself, and Wulfgar informs Hrothgar of the details. Beowulf announces himself as a hero who, among other things, can slay water sprites when he arrives at Hrothgar's throne.

Message of the tale:

Everybody dies, the narrator likes to point out in his narrations. Most people are aware of this, thus I believe I have noticed a pattern. Death was a distinct possibility because Vikings enjoyed killing both other people and themselves. Given the possibility that each battle could be one's last, bravery becomes increasingly crucial in combat. Yes, even the powerful Beowulf must perish, which only serves to highlight how important the concept of honor in both war and life is.
