23. sample problem of chain discount


The term "chain discount" comes from the practice of chaining a series of discounts together. Suppliers and wholesalers often do this with seasonal items or items that may soon be discontinued. Let's explore chain discounts by working through an example.
Al from Al's Fireplaces orders 6 wood stoves with a list price of $1,000 each from his supplier, Binford Fire. The supplier offers a chain discount of 5/10. What is the net price of the 6 fireplaces?
The chain discount of 5/10 means that 5% is given as a trade discount, followed by another 10% discount on the remaining balance. It is very important to NOT add the discounts together in a chain discount. Assuming a discount of 15% would be wrong!
To solve this problem we calculate the Net Price Equivalent Rate, then multiply the net price equivalent by the list price.
To calculate the Net Price Equivalent Rate, follow these two steps.
Step 1 Subtract each discount from 100%.
Step 2 Multiply them together.
(Do not round off decimals since this number is the Net Price Equivalent Rate).
Now apply the steps to find the Net Price Equivalent Rate for a chain discount of 5/10.
Step 1 100% - 5% = 95%
100% - 10% = 90%
Step 2 95% x 90%
0.95 x 0.90 = 0.855 (Net Price Equivalent Rate)
Net Price = List Price x Net Price Equivalent Rate
= ($1000 x 6) x 0.855 (6 fireplaces at $1000 each)
= $5,130
Therefore the net price is $5,130.
