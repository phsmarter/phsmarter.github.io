5. How does an RRL for quantitative differ from an RRL for qualitative?​


Answer:

Qualitative Research: an operational description

Purpose: explain; gain insight and understanding of phenomena through intensive collection and study of narrative data

Approach: inductive; value-laden/subjective; holistic, process-oriented

Hypotheses: tentative, evolving; based on the particular study

Lit. Review: limited; may not be exhaustive

Setting: naturalistic, when and as much as possible

Sampling: for the purpose; not necessarily representative; for in-depth understanding

Measurement: narrative; ongoing

Design and Method: flexible, specified only generally; based on non-intervention, minimal disturbance, such as historical, ethnographic, or case studies

Data Collection: document collection, participant observation, informal interviews, field notes

Data Analysis: raw data is words/ ongoing; involves synthesis

Data Interpretation: tentative, reviewed on ongoing basis, speculative

8. As a student researcher, what are your thoughts about related literature? What are the major approaches in ordering or sequencing the topics and concepts you need to include in a RRL?​


Answer:

A review of related – and preferably recent – literature is meant to set your research in the context of what is currently known about the topic and to establish that what you have to offer is novel, something different from what has been already attempted. The review also reassures the referees that you are familiar with current developments in your field: if all of the papers that you cite in the review are, for example, at least 3–5 years old, the referees are bound to notice!

Coming to the second part of your query, a good review is not a chronological inventory. Do not simply list all the papers in the order in which they were published; a thematic organization is more effective. For example, in one paragraph, you may cite papers related to what is currently known about the topic; in another, you can mention papers that specifically relate to the method you have chosen. Another possibility is to group the papers by region: global studies forming one group, for example, and regional studies and national-level studies forming two more groups. Yet another possibility is by results: all the papers with similar results forming one group and those proposing a different hypothesis forming a different group, and so on.

10. The influence of mass media to moral development of grade 11 students RRL​


Answer:

The influence of mass media on moral development has been a topic of much discussion and debate among researchers, educators, and parents. In recent years, concerns about the negative impact of media on the moral development of young people have grown. This literature review will examine relevant research on the influence of mass media on the moral development of grade 11 students.

According to a study by the Kaiser Family Foundation, adolescents in the United States spend an average of over seven hours a day using media, including television, video games, music, and the internet. Given this high level of exposure, researchers have become increasingly interested in understanding the ways in which media influences the moral development of young people.

Studies have found that exposure to violent and sexually explicit media can have a negative impact on moral development. A study by Paik and Comstock (1994) found that exposure to violent media was associated with lower levels of empathy and higher levels of aggression in children. Similarly, research by Ward and Friedman (2006) found that exposure to sexual content in media was associated with earlier sexual behavior and less positive attitudes toward sexual relationships among adolescents.

However, not all media exposure has negative effects. Positive portrayals of pro-social behavior, such as helping others or standing up for what is right, can have a positive impact on moral development. A study by Johnson and colleagues (2002) found that exposure to media featuring positive role models was associated with higher levels of empathy and pro-social behavior among adolescents.

In addition to the content of media, the context in which it is consumed can also impact its influence on moral development. For example, research by Huesmann and Moise-Titus (1998) found that the impact of media violence on aggression was stronger when the violence was portrayed as justified or unpunished.

In conclusion, while the influence of mass media on the moral development of grade 11 students is complex and multifaceted, research suggests that exposure to violent and sexually explicit media can have a negative impact on moral development, while positive portrayals of pro-social behavior can have a positive impact. It is important for parents, educators, and media producers to be aware of these findings and to take steps to promote positive media content and responsible media use among young people.

15. is there a similarity between traditional rrl and systematic rrl​


Answer:

Traditional reviews

Authors: One or more authors usually experts in the topic of interest

Study protocol: No study protocol

Research question: Broad to specific question, hypothesis not stated

Search strategy: No detailed search strategy, search is probably conducted using keywords

Sources of literature: Not usually stated and non-exhaustive, usually well-known articles. Prone to publication bias

Selection criteria: No specific selection criteria, usually subjective. Prone to selection bias

Critical appraisal: Variable evaluation of study quality or method

Synthesis: Often qualitative synthesis of evidence

Conclusions: Sometimes evidence based but can be influenced by author’s personal belief

Reproducibility: Findings cannot be reproduced independently as conclusions may be subjective

Update: Cannot be continuously updated

Systematic reviews

Authors: Two or more authors are involved in good quality systematic reviews, may comprise experts in the different stages of the review

Study protocol: Written study protocol which includes details of the methods to be used

Research question: Specific question which may have all or some of PICO components (Population, Intervention, Comparator, and Outcome). Hypothesis is stated

Search strategy: Detailed and comprehensive search strategy is developed

Sources of literature: List of databases, websites and other sources of included studies are listed. Both published and unpublished literature are considered

Selection criteria: Specific inclusion and exclusion criteria

Critical appraisal: Rigorous appraisal of study quality

Synthesis: Narrative, quantitative or qualitative synthesis

Conclusions: Conclusions drawn are evidence based

Reproducibility: Accurate documentation of method means results can be reproduced

Update: Systematic reviews can be periodically updated to include new evidence

The impact of mass media on the moral development of grade 11 students can be significant. Mass media refers to any form of media that reaches a large audience, such as television, movies, music, social media, and video games. It's important for grade 11 students to be aware of the potential impact of mass media on their moral development and to be critical consumers of media.
