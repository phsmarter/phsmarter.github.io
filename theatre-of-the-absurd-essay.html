6. Compare the characteristics of Greek Theatre and Shakespearean Theatre.Greek TheatreShakespearean Theatre.​


Answer:

THE classic stage and Shakespeare's theatre have, at first sight, nothing in common; for the first was dedicated to unity, the second to variety. The great size of the antique stage made unity essential. A play had but three or four characters and involved but one or two ideas, which were hammered upon during the entire performance. When the heroes ceased speaking, the Chorus took up the thread of the argument. A Greek tragedy, moreover, was of national origin and of religious import. The plot was always taken from a familiar myth; and only great personages, heroes, kings and princes were allowed upon the stage.

Since its inception, Children’s Theatre, has had a major impact in the lives of children in promoting creativity, communication and teamwork skills, in addition to inspiring children to explore their imaginations and challenges. Children’s theatre, popular for its inclusion of classic characters such as Peter Pan and Alice in Wonderland, can help children develop these important life-long skills.

Peter Pan and Alice In Wonderland, two literature classics very often found in children’s theatre, have been part of childhood for ages. Peter Pan, a character introduced in the novel “Peter Pan and Wendy” by J. M. Barrie, has become a timeless figure in both children’s theater and their imaginations. As the archetypical character of unbounded childhood and eternal youth, Peter Pan is often associated with the infinite possibilities of children’s imagination. Similarly, Lewis Carroll’s Alice in Wonderland, with its surreal space and characters, has enchanted readers with its fantastic adventures and has left an everlasting influence on children’s theatre.

The presence of Peter Pan and Alice in Wonderland in children’s theatre has allowed children to enter into a fantasy world and engage with their imaginations. For example, the popular musical adaptation of Alice in Wonderland, entitled “Alice in Wonderland, Jr” features children in a musical voyage down the rabbit hole. Furthermore, interactive workshops are often included in children’s theatre experiences which helps the young actors learn how to work together as a team and understand the importance of communication while putting on a production.

Children’s theatre provides important tools for children to explore and unravel their inner-selves. Friendships and creative experiences can be formed between production members on projects such as Peter Pan and Alice in Wonderland. With its interesting storytelling, the development of imagination and its collaboration of teamwork, children’s theatre can develop important life skills in children.

17. what is the proof by reductions to absurdity?​


Answer: in logic, reductio ad absurdum (Latin for '"reduction to absurdity"'), also known as argumentum ad absurdum (Latin for "argument to absurdity"), apagogical arguments, negation introduction or the appeal to extremes, is the form of argument that attempts to establish a claim by showing that the opposite scenario would lead to absurdity or contradiction.[1][2] It can be used to disprove a statement by showing that it would inevitably lead to a ridiculous, absurd, or impractical conclusion,[3] or to prove a statement by showing that if it were false, then the result would be absurd or impossible.[4][5] Traced back to classical Greek philosophy in Aristotle's Prior Analytics[5] (Greek: ἡ εἰς τὸ ἀδύνατον ἀπόδειξις, lit. "demonstration to the impossible", 62b), this technique has been used throughout history in both formal mathematical and philosophical reasoning, as well as in debate.[6]

1.Traditional Asian Theatre encompasses a diverse range of cultural and artistic expressions, each with their unique styles, themes, and techniques. In this essay, we will compare some of the major traditional Asian theatres and highlight their differences.

1. Chinese Theatre - Chinese theatre is one of the oldest and most well-established forms of theatre in Asia. It includes a wide range of genres, including opera, puppetry, and acrobatics. Chinese theatre is known for its elaborate costumes, makeup, and stylized movements. It also emphasizes a strong connection to Chinese mythology and history. Some of the most popular Chinese theatrical forms include Peking Opera, Kunqu Opera, and Cantonese Opera.

2. Japanese Theatre - Japanese theatre has a rich history dating back to the medieval period, and its most well-known form is Noh theatre. Noh theatre is characterized by its minimalist sets, stylized movements, and use of masks. The performances are often slow-paced and highly ritualized, with a strong emphasis on poetic language and symbolism. Another important form of Japanese theatre is Kabuki, which is known for its elaborate costumes and makeup, as well as its exaggerated acting style.

3. Indian Theatre - Indian theatre has a long and rich history, dating back over two thousand years. It includes a wide range of genres, including Sanskrit drama, puppetry, and dance theatre. Indian theatre is characterized by its elaborate costumes, music, and dance, as well as its emphasis on religious and mythological themes. Some of the most popular forms of Indian theatre include Kathakali, Bharatanatyam, and Koodiyattam.

4. Korean Theatre - Korean theatre has a history dating back to the Three Kingdoms period, and it is known for its highly stylized movements and use of masks. One of the most popular forms of Korean theatre is Pansori, which features a solo singer accompanied by a drummer. Another important form of Korean theatre is Talchum, which involves mask dances performed at festivals.

While each of these traditional Asian theatres has its unique characteristics, they share some commonalities, such as their emphasis on storytelling, symbolism, and ritual. Theatrical forms in Asia have evolved over time and have been influenced by various cultures and art forms. d) Indonesian Wayang Kulit: Wayang Kulit is a traditional Indonesian shadow puppet theatre that dates back to at least the 10th century. It is characterized by its use of intricate, leather puppets, which are manipulated by a puppeteer behind a screen. Wayang Kulit plays often explore spiritual and mythological themes.

e) Korean Mask Dance Drama: Korean Mask Dance Drama is a traditional Korean theatre form that combines dance, music, and masked performances. It is known for its use of colorful, expressive masks, as well as its use of physical movements and gestures to convey emotions and actions.

30. How can human being transcend the absurdity of life?​


Answer:

Albert Camus

First published Thu Oct 27, 2011; substantive revision Mon Apr 10, 2017

Albert Camus (1913–1960) was a journalist, editor and editorialist, playwright and director, novelist and author of short stories, political essayist and activist—and, although he more than once denied it, a philosopher. He ignored or opposed systematic philosophy, had little faith in rationalism, asserted rather than argued many of his main ideas, presented others in metaphors, was preoccupied with immediate and personal experience, and brooded over such questions as the meaning of life in the face of death. Although he forcefully separated himself from existentialism, Camus posed one of the twentieth century’s best-known existentialist questions, which launches The Myth of Sisyphus: “There is only one really serious philosophical question, and that is suicide” (MS, 3). And his philosophy of the absurd has left us with a striking image of the human fate: Sisyphus endlessly pushing his rock up the mountain only to see it roll back down each time he gains the top. Camus’s philosophy found political expression in The Rebel, which along with his newspaper editorials, political essays, plays, and fiction earned him a reputation as a great moralist. It also embroiled him in conflict with his friend, Jean-Paul Sartre, provoking the major political-intellectual divide of the Cold-War era as Camus and Sartre became, respectively, the leading intellectual voices of the anti-Communist and pro-Communist left. Furthermore, in posing and answering urgent philosophical questions of the day, Camus articulated a critique of religion and of the Enlightenment and all its projects, including Marxism. In 1957 he won the Nobel Prize for literature. He died in a car accident in January, 1960, at the age of 46.
