18. Iraya mangyan weavers at work ​


Answer:

The Iraya people of the Philippines are known primarily for their traditional weaving techniques. They use a type of backstrap loom, which is a weaving frame held in place by the weaver's body. The loom is made out of bamboo and other natural materials, and it is used to weave fabrics for clothing and home items like mats and hammocks. The weavers make use of various materials, such as cotton, abaca, and pineapple fibers. In addition to creating their own unique designs, Iraya weavers also incorporate traditional motifs from the Philippines, such as flowers, stars, and geometric shapes.

The Iraya are also well known for their intricate hand-dyeing techniques. The weavers obtain their dyes from plants and other natural sources. They use a variety of methods to create the desired shades and patterns, such as tie-dye, batik, and dye-resist. The resulting fabrics are vibrant and eye-catching.

Iraya weaving is an important part of the Iraya people's cultural identity, and it is a source of pride and a way of connecting with their ancestors. The Iraya weavers are highly skilled artisans, and their work is highly sought after. Their products can be found in museums, galleries, and markets around the world.

25. describe the following: mangyan- _______________________________________________________________________________________________________________ mariones festivals- _____________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________ marble product- _____________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________ nito weaving- _____________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________


Answer:

Mangyan—comprises the indigenous peoples of Mindoro Island. ... Also known in literature as Mangianes, Manghianes, Manguianes, Tiron, Lactan, Buquit, Barangan, Tagaydan, Pula, Nauhan, and Buid) The term is a generic one to refer to the indigenous peoples of the island of Mindoro.

Mariones Festival—The Moriones is a lenten festival held annually on Holy Week on the island of Marinduque, Philippines. The "Moriones" are men and women in costumes and masks replicating the garb of biblical Imperial and Royal Roman soldiers as interpreted by locals.

Marble Product—Construction marble is a stone which is composed of calcite, dolomite or serpentine that is capable of taking a polish.

Nito weaving—The nito is a forest vine abundant in their environment which they use to weave the traditional baskets, hats, trays and jars of different shapes and sizes. Their creations have also evolved to various modern home ornaments and fashionable accessories.

28. What are the possible reasons why the iraya mangyans no longer speak the iraya language?


Answer:

Although there remains several number of Iraya speakers to this day, certain alarming developments could jeopardize the future of the language in some parts of Mindoro. A specific example would be the Irayas of Baclayán. Many of the children can only muster a smattering of Iraya, as Tagalog is slowly replacing their native language. And few show interest in preserving their language, as perhaps it is no longer useful to them.

Explanation:

The Iraya language is an Austronesian language belonging to the northern Mindoro group. The known dialects of Iraya are Abra-de-Ilog, Alag-Bako, Pagbahan, Palauan-Calavite, Pambuhan, and Santa Cruz. Each dialect is distinct, but the basic vocabulary and grammar of everyday speech are the same. Tagalog words are also used by the Irayas living in the border areas between the foothills.
