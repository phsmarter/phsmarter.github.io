6. How can one imitate the example of St. Augustine as Philosopher? Explain


St. Augustine a Philosopher

Since he preached that loving one's neighbor was the best way to serve God, Saint Augustine is sometimes shown with a heart that is on fire. The Order's crest serves as a reminder to Augustine's disciples that they must live out and teach charity toward God and their neighbor.

Like the majority of ancient philosophers, Augustine believes that the human being is a composite of body and soul and that the soul—conceived as the source of life and the epicenter of awareness, perception, and thought—is, or should be, the governing portion within this composite.

The City of God, On Christian Doctrine, and Confessions are only a few of his many influential writings.

The idea that philosophy is "love of wisdom" (Confessiones 3.8; De civitate Dei 8.1), or an attempt to pursue happiness—or, as late-antique thinkers, both pagan and Christian, liked to put it, salvation—by seeking understanding of the true nature of things and living, was passed down to Augustine from ancient thought.

The human race is unified in sin and disobedience against God, and Augustine has taught us that they cannot rescue themselves.

Those who have seen Christ have discovered that to be saved, they must entirely trust him and not depend on their abilities, traits, or heredity.

As Augustine builds a theology of God that would come to rule Western thought, he also develops a brand-new notion of personal identity known as the idea of the self. Self-presentation and self-realization are the two steps that lead to this identity.
