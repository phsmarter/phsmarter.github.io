19. Write a letter to the President of the Philippines proposing measures for how the youth could help mitigate disaster in the country.​


Dear President of the Philippines,

As the youth of your country, we are deeply concerned about the impact of natural disasters on our communities and the need for effective measures to mitigate their effects. We believe that our generation has the energy, passion, and creativity to play a key role in addressing this important issue, and we would like to propose some ideas for how we can help.

First, we believe that education is a critical tool in building resilience to disasters. We propose the creation of a national program to educate young people on the risks and impacts of natural disasters, as well as the steps they can take to prepare for and respond to them. This could include training in first aid, emergency response, and disaster preparedness, as well as opportunities for youth to participate in disaster drills and simulations.

Second, we believe that youth can play a key role in disaster risk reduction and management. We propose the establishment of a national youth network focused on disaster mitigation and response. This network could provide a platform for young people to share ideas, best practices, and resources, as well as to participate in disaster response efforts.

Third, we believe that youth can be powerful advocates for disaster preparedness and response. We propose the creation of a national campaign to raise awareness about the importance of disaster preparedness and the role that young people can play in mitigating its effects. This could include the use of social media, public events, and other channels to engage young people in the conversation and encourage them to take action.

We believe that these proposals have the potential to make a real difference in the fight against natural disasters in the Philippines. We stand ready to work with your administration to develop and implement these ideas, and to do our part to build a safer and more resilient future for our country.

Sincerely,

23. statement of problem. about, Out Of School Youth​


Out-of-school youth are individuals who are not currently enrolled in formal education, such as primary, secondary, or higher education. This can be due to a variety of factors, including poverty, lack of access to education, or personal or family circumstances. The problem of out-of-school youth is significant because it can have negative impacts on individuals and communities. Out-of-school youth may have difficulty acquiring the knowledge and skills necessary for employment and may be at higher risk for poverty, social exclusion, and other negative outcomes. In addition, communities with high rates of out-of-school youth may experience economic and social problems due to a lack of skilled labor and a lack of investment in human capital. Therefore, addressing the problem of out-of-school youth is important for promoting individual and societal well-being.

Out-of-School Children and Youth Prevalence in the Philippines

The 2013 Functional Literacy, Education and Mass Media Survey (FLEMMS) covered around 36 million population aged 6 to 24 years. According to the survey, one in every ten or about 4 million Filipino children and youth was out-of-school in 2013. Out-of-school children is defined in the FLEMMS as persons aged 6 to 14 years who are not attending school while out-of-school youth as persons aged 15 to 24 years who are not attending school, have not finished any college or post secondary course, and are not working.

Out-of-school children and youth in ARMM comprised 14.4 percent, which is the highest across regions. Six regions have proportion of out-of-school children and youth higher than the national figure at 10.6 percent, namely, SOCCSKSARGEN, Davao, Central Luzon, Zamboanga Peninsula, Caraga and MIMAROPA (ranging from 11.2 to 12.3 percent). Meanwhile, the proportion of out-of-school children and youth was lowest in CAR at 7.1 percent.
