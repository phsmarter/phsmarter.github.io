The hypothesis regarding the effects of using a smartphone on the sleeping habits of students can vary depending on the specific research question and context. However, a possible hypothesis is:

"Excessive smartphone use among students can negatively impact their sleep quality and quantity, leading to an increased risk of sleep disorders, such as insomnia and sleep deprivation. This effect may be caused by the blue light emitted by smartphones, which suppresses the production of melatonin, a hormone that regulates the sleep-wake cycle. Additionally, smartphone use before bedtime may increase cognitive stimulation and arousal, making it harder for students to fall asleep and stay asleep."

It's important to note that this is just one possible hypothesis, and other factors may also contribute to the relationship between smartphone use and sleep habits. The effects of this widespread use on the sleeping habits of students is a statement problem that has a number of potential consequences. Studies have shown that the use of smartphones at night significantly affects a person's sleep quality, circadian rhythm, and overall quality of life. It has been demonstrated that even brief exposure to the lit screen of a smartphone in the evening and during the night can disrupt the individual's internal biological clock, which can adversely affect their ability to fall asleep and stay asleep during the desired hours. As students’ evening and late night activities increasingly involve the use of smartphones and other technology, it is important to understand the effects of such activities and their resulting effects on sleeping habits. Furthermore, the manner in which these devices are used can lead to long-term changes in mental health, such as decreased focus, decreased alertness, and decreased memory. Therefore, it is essential to gain a better understanding of the statement problem of the effects of using smartphone to the sleeping habits of students in order to improve the students’ well-being, safety, and academic performance.
