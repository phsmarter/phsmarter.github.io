14. What is The Influence of the Catholic Church on the issue of Abortion and Reproductive Health Law? Please provide the sources of your research regarding the topic itself.​


Answer:

The Catholic Church has a long history of opposing abortion, as it believes that life begins at conception and that taking a human life is wrong. The Catholic Church's influence on the issue of abortion and reproductive health law is seen both in its vocal opposition to abortion and its active involvement in politics. In 2012, the Church openly urged Catholics to consider the Church’s position when electing political leaders, and in 2013, the Pope declared abortion “intrinsically evil” during a public mass.

In most Catholic countries, abortion is illegal except in cases of a medical emergency or risk to the health of the mother. Catholic Church leaders such as U.S. Knights of Columbus Supreme Knight Carl Anderson have cited Pope Francis’ statement in 2013 to argue against abortion access in the U.S. The Church also plays a key role in the push for laws limiting contraception access and has filed amicus briefs in related court cases.

In terms of the influence of the Church on reproductive health law, it is important to note that the Catholic Church has a long history of opposing contraception, sterilization, and in vitro fertilization, and its views on the issue are widely reflected in the laws of many Catholic countries. In a strongly Catholic society such as Mexico, abortion remains illegal except in very specific cases despite the fact that abortion has been decriminalized in other Latin American countries.

ABORTION AS CONTROVERSY

Abortion is a highly controversial and divisive issue in the Philippines. It is currently illegal in the country, with exceptions only allowed in cases where the pregnancy poses a serious threat to the life or health of the woman, or if the fetus is severely malformed.

The main argument for making abortion illegal in the Philippines is based on the belief that human life begins at the moment of conception, and that abortion is therefore the taking of human life. Proponents of this view believe that all human beings have the right to life and that this right should be protected by law.

Opponents of abortion, however, argue that the decision to have an abortion should be left up to the woman who is pregnant and that the government should not be involved in making such personal medical decisions. They point out that women have the right to control their own bodies, and that denying them the ability to have an abortion can have serious consequences for their health and well-being.

In terms of evidence, studies have shown that making abortion illegal does not actually reduce the number of abortions that take place. Instead, it often leads to women seeking out unsafe, illegal abortions, which can put their lives at risk. Additionally, denying women the right to abortion can have negative economic consequences, as women may be forced to miss work or school in order to carry unplanned pregnancies to term.

Overall, the debate over abortion in the Philippines is complex and multifaceted, with valid arguments on both sides. Ultimately, the decision on whether or not to allow abortion in the country will likely be made by lawmakers and the courts.

24. write a position paper about abortion​


ABORTION IN PHILIPPINES

In the Philippines, abortion has been criminalized for almost a century. The penal provisions regarding abortion do not contain any exceptions for abortion, such as saving the pregnant woman's life or protecting her health. Under Spanish colonial control, the Penal Code of 1870 criminalized abortion, and the criminal provisions were included in the Revised Penal Code passed in 1930 during the U.S. colonization of the Philippines.

The prohibition of abortion has not halted abortion but has made the practice hazardous and potentially fatal for the more than half a million women who attempt to terminate pregnancies annually. In 2008 alone, the Philippines' criminal abortion ban was projected to have caused at least 1,000 fatalities and 90,000 complications.

Filipino women with life-threatening pregnancies have little choice except to endanger their lives, either through unsafe abortion or by continuing high-risk pregnancies, due to the absence of access to safe abortion. As they encounter obstacles in acquiring efficient family planning methods and lack access to reproductive health services, poor women are especially susceptible to unsafe abortions and their complications. Hemorrhage, sepsis, peritonitis, and injuries to the cervix, vagina, uterus, and abdominal organs are common physical problems resulting from the use of such crude and risky procedures.
