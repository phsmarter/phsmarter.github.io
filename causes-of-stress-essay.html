Answer:

Stress is a common experience for many students. There are several causes of stress that can affect a student's physical and mental health. In this essay, I will discuss three causes of stress and their effects on students.

Firstly, academic pressure is a significant cause of stress among students. The pressure to succeed academically can lead to anxiety, sleep disorders, and depression. The pressure to maintain good grades, the pressure to excel in extracurricular activities, and the pressure to get accepted into prestigious colleges can all contribute to academic stress. The effect of academic pressure can be manifested through physical symptoms like headaches, stomach aches, and fatigue.

Secondly, personal relationships can also cause stress among students. Relationship problems with friends or family members can lead to emotional distress, anxiety, and depression. Students who have difficulty balancing their relationships with their schoolwork often experience stress. The effect of relationship stress can lead to poor academic performance and a lack of motivation.

Lastly, financial issues can also be a cause of stress among students. The cost of tuition fees, textbooks, housing, and other academic expenses can create financial stress. Financial stress can lead to emotional distress, sleep disorders, and an inability to concentrate on academic work. Students who work part-time jobs to pay for their expenses can experience stress from balancing their work and school schedules.

In conclusion, stress is a common experience for many students. Academic pressure, personal relationships, and financial issues are some of the main causes of stress. The effects of stress on students can lead to physical symptoms, emotional distress, poor academic performance, and a lack of motivation. It is important for students to find ways to manage their stress and seek support from friends, family, and school resources.

Learn to recognise the signs that you are becoming stressed

There are a wide range of possible signs and symptoms that may be associated with stress. These include headaches, stomach upsets and indigestion, and sleep problems. Many people also find they become very emotional and have trouble regulating their emotions.

Unfortunately, most of these signs are fairly non-specific: that is, they may be associated with many different illnesses and conditions. It can therefore be hard to identify when your symptoms are the result of stress.

Look after yourself physically

When you are physically fit and well, it is much easier to cope with stress.

When you become stressed, it is harder to motivate yourself to care about what you eat, or whether you exercise. However, not doing so can also make you more stressed. Get into good habits while your stress levels are fairly low, and you may find that they never go up again. These ‘good habits’ include:

Taking regular exercise

Stressful situations increase the level of stress hormones such as adrenaline and cortisol in your body.

These are the “fight or flight” hormones that evolution has hard-wired into our brains and which are designed to protect us from immediate bodily harm when we are under threat.  However, stress in the modern age is rarely remedied by a fight or flight response. Physical exercise can be used as a surrogate to metabolise the excessive stress hormones and restore your body and mind to a calmer, more relaxed state.

Try to incorporate some physical activity into your daily routine on a regular basis, either before or after work, or at lunchtime. Regular physical activity will also improve the quality of your sleep.

(You may use another sheet of paper for your answer) STRESS AND STRAIN In engineering, stress and strain describe the conditions of a material under varying circumstances Stress is the material's resistance to external forces, measured in terms of the force exerted per unit of area, and strain is the material's change resulting from those external forces. Every force that acts on a material creates a type of stress, and each stress has an accompanying type of strain. The three basic types of stress are tensile, compressive, and shearing. Tensile stress occurs when a pair of forces acts on opposite ends of the material and attempt to pull it apart. The resistance the material offers to this action is known as the tensile stress in the material Tensile stress is most easily demonstrated by pulling on the ends of a rubber band. The type of stretching (strain) taking place in the rubber band occurs to some degree in all other materials which are this type of force. Compressive stress results from forces pushing in on the ends of a material. A simple example of a compressive force is pressure exerted to push an accordion together. An accordion has only a small compressive stress because the accordion bag offers little resistance to being forced together Reduction is the length of the material (strain) accompanies a compressive stress Shearing stress occurs when a force acts downward on a material, causing a piece of it to bend on break off. This type of force, known as shearing force, is resisted by shearing stress Shearing force and stress be demonstrated by slicing a loaf of bread. Although amplied by the knife's cutting edge, the force can still be considered a shearing force. Like all other materials the bread offers resistance (stress) but tends to break (strain) under such force. ​
