28. A story about regretting being stubborn and having ignored your parents' instructions


Answer:

If there was ever a time I didn’t listen to a person of authority (e.g. parents, guardians, teachers, mentors, etc), I’d feel very guilty about it.

If you meant not following their advice, commands, directions, etc… no. I have no regrets about disobedience.

Why? because I grew up in an environment where everything was suggested rather than commanded. Mostly because I was the only male in the household, I was groomed to take over and/or to take charge. Consequently, I was trained to pay attention and listen to all data points before making a decision. I was also trained to explain what the course of action is before doing anything.

Wrong or right, bad or good, after the decision has been made, all concerned has to follow through. Including me. If anyone involved in that action has any reservations, these need to be aired out before the decision is made. Not before, during, or after the action or plan is implemented.

Please understand that I started early… like even before I went to school. It’s always been like that, and it drives my wife crazy. The good news is, I do not hesitate to apologize if things don’t work out as planned. And I am very vocal and grateful to everyone when things go well.

Also, my children already know that I am responsible for all the mistakes they make because I didn’t give them all the life lessons needed to deal with all the nuances that life brings. But all their successes are theirs to claim because they’re the ones who made the decisions and took responsibility for those decisions they made.

Because we all understand that there’s no real wrong or right, bad or good. We choose a course of action and take responsibility for it.

Jack was feeling an overwhelming sense of remorse as he reflected on his latest venture. He had been an optimist, buoyed by the potential success, but had found himself unable to act on the opportunities he had come across. He was growing increasingly annoyed at his own blunt refusal to move forward and boldy take necessary chances. The longer he stayed in a state of feigned activity, covering up his lack of action with pretense, the more frustrating he found himself becoming.

It was clear that something would have to alter if he wanted to break free from this cycle of self-sabotaging inaction. So he decided to push himself to take at least one action each day and not to refrain from striving towards success. He was surprised to find how easy it was to make progress when he was no longer resisting his efforts and was terrified of the immense satisfaction he felt when he gave ample energy to his newly found ambitions.
