About phenomenology

Phenomenology is a study in terms of philosophy that studies human as a phenomenon. The science of phenomonology in philosophy is usually associated with the science of hermeneutics, namely the science that studies the benefits of this phenomenon.

This term was first introduced by Johann Heinrich Lambert (1728 - 1777), a German philosopher. In his book Neues Organon (1764). he wrote about unreal knowledge.

In the literary approach, phenomenology uses the intuitive experience of phenomena, something that has a phenomenological reflection, as a starting point and effort to get to the nature of experience and the nature of what we experience. G.W.F. Hegel and Edmund Husserl are two important figures in the development of this philosophical approach.

The phenomenological tradition concentrates on personal experience including the realm of individuals who actually share experiences with one another. Communication is seen as a round of sharing experiences or information between individuals through discussion. Good relations between individuals have a high position in this tradition. In this tradition, it is said that language represents a meaning for objects. So, just one word can give meaning to something that will be interpreted.

Fundamentally, phenomenology is a tradition of study used to explore human experience. As stated by Littlejohn that phenomenology is a tradition to explore human experience. In this context, the assumption is correct that humans actively understand the world around them as a life experience and actively interpret that experience.

The main assumption of phenomenology is that humans actively interpret their experiences by providing a formula for something they experience. Therefore interpretation is an active process to provide a formula for something that is experienced by humans. In other words understanding is a creative action, namely action towards meaning.
