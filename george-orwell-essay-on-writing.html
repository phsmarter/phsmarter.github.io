7. "politics and English language' by George Orwell . What type of academic writing is being describe​


Answer:

Politics and the English Language" is an essay by George Orwell that criticised the "ugly and inaccurate" written English of his time and examines the connection between political orthodoxies and the debasement of language

Answer:

The essay focuses on political language, which, according to Orwell, "is designed to make lies sound truthful and murder respectable, and to give an appearance of solidity to pure wind". Orwell believed that the language used was necessarily vague or meaningless because it was intended to hide the truth rather than express it. This unclear prose was a "contagion" which had spread to those who did not intend to hide the truth, and it concealed a writer's thoughts from himself and others.[1] Orwell encourages concreteness and clarity instead of vagueness, and individuality over political conformity.

17. Literalization of the story shooting an elephant by George Orwell​


Answer:

Once upon a time, there was a British police officer stationed in a Burmese town. One day, he was informed that a rogue elephant had been causing trouble in the town. He had a duty to protect the townspeople, so he got his rifle and set out to find the elephant.

When he finally located the elephant, it was wreaking havoc on the town. The officer was faced with a difficult dilemma: he wanted to protect the townspeople, but he didn't want to hurt the elephant. After much deliberation, he decided the only way to protect the townspeople was to shoot the elephant.

The officer was filled with sorrow as he raised his rifle and fired. The bullet pierced the elephant's side, and it crumpled to the ground with a loud thud. The townspeople were relieved and thankful for the officer's brave act, but the officer was left with a heavy heart. He had just taken the life of a magnificent creature, and he would never forget the sorrow of that day.

25. four things that you can infer about george orwell in his book "books vs cigarrets"?


Answer:

George Orwell wrote his ‘Books versus Cigarettes‘ in 1946, a year later than his Animal Farm. This piece was published in The Tribune on February 8th in the same year. Orwell became the literary editor of The Tribune in 1943. He wrote several essays on problems affecting British society. This one is an interesting and thought-provoking essay about reading books versus smoking cigarettes. Smoking is addictive and costly. It burns your pockets and your heart too. On the other hand, reading costs less and can be profitable in many ways. Orwell has picked a topic for analysis that looks generic but has a very deep meaning. He presents a cost analysis of reading books over a lifetime and its advantages. Then he compares it with the costs of smoking and compared the benefits and harms of the two. Both are habits and while one makes you wise and noble, the other is an addiction that burns millions every year. Orwell also talks of poverty in his essay and how it is related to smoking. His work is full of creative humor and sarcasm. Orwell presents his own reading expenses; a rough estimate of all he has spent on acquiring the books in his library as well as the ones he received as gifts and the specimen copies. Then he compares reading expenses with smoking to provide readers an estimate of the value they earn from reading or smoking in their life. Orwell recommends reading over smoking. Reading will make you a better person and give you the wealth of your life. Smoking and other recreations do not offer the same potential returns.
