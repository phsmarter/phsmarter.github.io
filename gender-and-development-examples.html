3. Gender and developmentand Example?Care​


Gender and development is an interdisciplinary field of research and applied study that implements a feminist approach to understanding and addressing the disparate impact that economic development and globalization have on people based upon their location, gender, class background, and other socio-political identities. A strictly economic approach to development views a country's development in quantitative terms such as job creation, inflation control, and high employment – all of which aim to improve the ‘economic wellbeing’ of a country and the subsequent quality of life for its people.[1] In terms of economic development, quality of life is defined as access to necessary rights and resources including but not limited to quality education, medical facilities, affordable housing, clean environments, and low crime rate.[1] Gender and development considers many of these same factors; however, gender and development emphasizes efforts towards understanding how multifaceted these issues are in the entangled context of culture, government, and globalization. Accounting for this need, gender and development implements ethnographic research, research that studies a specific culture or group of people by physically immersing the researcher into the environment and daily routine of those being studied,[2] in order to comprehensively understand how development policy and practices affect the everyday life of targeted groups or areas.

Examples of these policies and programs include Structural Adjustment Programs (SAPs), microfinance, outsourcing, and privatizing public enterprises,[4] all of which direct focus towards economic growth and suggest that advancement towards gender equality will follow.

16. Choose two (2) of the six (6) theories of gender development. Explain each theory in 5-10 sentences. And give atleast two (2) concrete examples.​


Answer:

The social learning theory and the cognitive development theory.

Social Learning Theory:

The social learning theory suggests that gender roles and behaviors are learned through observation, imitation, and reinforcement from the people and environment around us. Children learn from observing others and imitating the behaviors and attitudes they see. They also receive reinforcement or punishment for their behaviors and learn from the consequences of their actions. This theory suggests that gender roles are not innate but are learned through socialization.

For example, a child may learn to exhibit stereotypical gender roles by observing their parents' behaviors. If a child sees their mother performing housework and taking care of the children, while their father works outside the home, the child may learn that these are the appropriate gender roles. Similarly, if a child is praised for exhibiting gender-appropriate behaviors, such as a girl being praised for being nurturing or a boy being praised for being tough, they may continue to exhibit these behaviors.

Cognitive Development Theory:

The cognitive development theory suggests that children actively construct their own understanding of gender through their experiences and interactions with the environment around them. Children develop schemas, or mental representations, of what it means to be male or female based on their observations and experiences. As children develop, they refine these schemas and integrate new information into their understanding of gender.

For example, a child may develop a schema for what it means to be a boy based on their observations of other boys around them. As they interact with more people and gain new experiences, they may refine their schema to include more complex ideas about gender, such as the idea that boys can also have emotions and be nurturing. Another example is a child who may question why they cannot wear dresses if they are a boy, challenging the idea that clothing is gendered.

Explanation

both the social learning theory and cognitive development theory offer insights into how children develop an understanding of gender. The social learning theory emphasizes the importance of environmental factors, while the cognitive development theory emphasizes the role of the child's own thinking and experiences.
