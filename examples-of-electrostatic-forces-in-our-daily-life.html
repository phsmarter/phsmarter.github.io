18. How are gravitational, magnetic, and electrostatic forces different from mechanical, frictional, and spring forces?


When the distance between atoms approaches the nanometer range, the charge clouds or wave functions of the atoms become distorted due to perturbations. As a result, some atomic states are pushed lower, while others are pushed higher. There is binding between the atoms if the net effect is a decrease in energy. This interatomic force is proportional to the interatomic distance.

Now, in the case of friction, the atoms of two surfaces attract each other. When we try to slide one surface over another, we are attempting to change interatomic distances, which is opposed by attractive forces. This is where the conflict arises.

When we extend or compress a spring, we also change the distances between the spring's atoms. The distances are changed from equilibrium interatomic distances to other distances. This is opposed by attractive forces, and we have quantities such as the spring(force) constant.

When we try to change the distance between the atoms in a string, we get a restoring (tensional)force.
