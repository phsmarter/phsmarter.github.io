29. Can someone smart enough make me an essay that addresses this prompt: "My Interpretation of Life"​


My Interpretation of Life

(or if you need an original title: "Light of Life")

    When people attempt to interpret life, most turn to philosophical insights, searching for a deeper meaning in all things. Some say understanding the meaning of life means attaining the ultimate wisdom, which is to say, the meaning of life cannot be found at all. That's because life has no clear-cut meaning other than that given in dictionaries. It is something openly interpreted. For me, the true interpretation of life is found not in defining it, but in valuing it.

    Plants, animals, and humans alike have the essence of life, the ability to live. Every single life is precious because it came from God. To be given the breath of life, something no one in this world can impart, is both a gift and a burden, because every living being is responsible for one another. In more civilized societies, however, like that of humans, this responsibility can be forgotten. The capability of humans to decide freely also makes them capable of selfishness.

    This is why so many people also think of life as useless, as an unnecessary necessity. Many people find life to be meaningless because there is no clear definition for it. The world can seem so when ruled by the wrong people, and it can ultimately feel empty. People might feel they are pushed around by forces beyond their control, like a tumbleweed with no aim nor direction.  Life, as John Berryman puts in one of his poems, can seem so boring, and nothing in the world can satisfy the emptiness one feels.

   But nothing about life should be meaningless. Certainly the world can feel like a drag sometimes, but that isn't it's own fault. It is because we refuse to make the world worth living in. When the entire world is in darkness, we believe we are blind, and come to terms with this empty reality. But we aren't blind. It's just that no one has decided to shine a light in our lives. The black silhouettes and shadows around us are boring because we can't see it for what it truly is: beautiful mountains wrapped in lush green leaves, antelopes skipping across endless yellow-green fields, or even smiling faces, seeming so ominous in darkness, but so caring in reality.

   This is what I see life as. Life, for me, is not finding meaning, but valuing. Life is about living for others and making the best of every moment, leaving a legacy of joy. The emptiness of life comes from the lack of value we place on it. When we, collectively, start caring and collaborating, we raise our candles high and become a beam for others. We give meaning to others' lives by showing them that we value them. And, ultimately, we find meaning in our own lives, because we see the truth: life is a gift that you cannot enjoy if you don't share it. This world is worth living for if we value our lives, and those of others.

Brief explanation:

For prompts that are more or less personal, this is a typically helpful format:

an introduction with a thesis statement (or your main stand/topic on the prompt given to you, in one sentence), a basic reinforcement of the thesis, a contradicting viewpoint with the thesis,another reinforcement that proves your thesis is better than the contradicting viewpoint,and a conclusion to sum everything up.

Also, to help with the flow, think of a metaphor and stick with it. Notice the uses of "darkness" and "light": these are used in the body paragraphs (paragraphs 2, 3, and 4) and then again in the conclusion. This helps make your essay more cohesive, or glued together.
