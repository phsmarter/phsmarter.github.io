1. explain.A MAJOR CHANGE IN PASSION: THE EXPERIENCES OF COLLEGE STUDENTS WHO SHIFTED COURSE​


A major change in passion refers to a situation where a college student decides to change their course of study, despite having had a strong interest in their previous major. This could be due to a variety of reasons such as a lack of fulfillment in their current field, difficulty finding employment, or discovering a new area of interest.

The experiences of college students who have shifted course can vary greatly, depending on the individual's situation and the reasons behind the change. Some students may feel a sense of relief and excitement at the prospect of pursuing a new field, while others may feel anxious or uncertain about their decision.

In many cases, switching majors may require students to take additional coursework, extend their time in college, or even transfer to a different institution. This can result in added stress, financial burden, and social adjustment.

However, for some students, changing majors can be a positive and transformative experience. It may allow them to pursue a new passion, discover new opportunities, and develop new skills. Additionally, the process of changing majors can teach students valuable lessons about decision-making, perseverance, and adaptability.

Overall, a major change in passion can be a significant and life-changing experience for college students, with both positive and negative consequences. It is important for students to carefully consider their options and seek advice from advisors, mentors, and peers before making such a decision.

Choosing your course

There are a range of subjects and courses available which can help you reach new careers, so it's important to research what's right for you.

The Careers Service provides an impartial, all-age careers information, advice and guidance service to help young people and adults make informed choices about their future career paths. Find out how you can contact the Careers Service below or speak to your school's Careers Adviser.

Careers

Many courses are vocational and work-related, and lead straight into a career such as nursing or accountancy. Others are academic and range from subjects you may have studied before in school such as French or geography, to less familiar ones like social policy.

You’ll need to look beyond the course title because courses with exactly the same name may differ enormously. You should look carefully at the differences between courses within your subject before deciding which to apply for.

For example, if you’re interested in construction but wish to use your creative skills, you may be better suited to a building design management course than a building project management course.

Course differences and entry requirements
