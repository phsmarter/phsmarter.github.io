The film was featured by The United Nations at the 2015 Climate Conference in Paris, has won 3 international film festivals, and was awarded the prestigious Vimeo Staff Pick. 11. reflection regarding photographic films​


Answer:

Photographic Films

Photographic film is a type of image recoding material that is used in cameras. It's made of transparent plastic in the shape of a strip or sheet with light-sensitive silver halide crystals in a gelatinous solution on one side. When a photographic film is exposed to light by a photographic camera, the amount of light absorbed by each crystal causes chemical reactions. These modifications result in the emulsion forming an invisible latent picture, which is then fixed and developed into a visible photograph. Color film has three layers, each sensitive to a different color, whereas the black and white film has only one layer of silver halide crystals.

A bit of history...

Early attempts at photography used silver-plated copper sheets, paper, and sometimes leather covered with light-sensitive chemicals rather than film. Because early transparent polymers could not attain the opaqueness of glass and were still much more expensive than glass, glass plates became standard about the middle of the nineteenth century. George Eastman created the first flexible roll film in 1885, although it was made of paper rather than synthetic.

In 1889, the first transparent plastic roll film (made of highly flammable nitrocellulose) was produced. In 1908, Kodak introduced "safety film." It was created as a replacement for hazardous nitrate film and was made of cellulose acetate. Because nitrate film was significantly stronger, somewhat more clear, and less expensive, it wasn't totally replaced until 1951.

Photographic Film Types

Print, color reversal film, and black-and-white reversal film are the three most common types of photographic film.

When print film, also known as negative film, is developed, the colors are inverted, resulting in negatives. These photographic films must be printed by either magnifying them with a lens or placing them in direct contact with photographic paper as light shines through. Print-type photographic films are accessible in color or black-and-white.After being processed, color reversal photographic films, also known as slide films, are known as transparencies and are viewed using a loupe or projector. These films, sometimes known as slides, are often placed on plastic or cardboard for projection. They're frequently employed to create digital sans serif or color separations for mass-market printing.Print and color reversal films are more frequent than black-and-white reversal films. They reverse-process traditional black-and-white material to produce black-and-white slides.

What is reflection photography.

Reflection photography, also referred to as mirror photography, is when you use reflective surfaces to create an artistic echo of a scene. This type of photography can add an interesting spin to locations that are hotspots for photographers such as oceans, lakes, puddles, and even rain drops.

30. reflection regarding photographic films​


Answer:

Photographic film

is a strip or sheet of transparent film base coated on one side with a gelatin emulsion containing microscopically small light-sensitive silver halide crystals. The sizes and other characteristics of the crystals determine the sensitivity, contrast, and resolution of the film.[1]

The emulsion will gradually darken if left exposed to light, but the process is too slow and incomplete to be of any practical use.

Instead, a very short exposure to the image formed by a camera lens is used to produce only a very slight chemical change, proportional to the amount of light absorbed by each crystal.

This creates an invisible latent image in the emulsion, which can be chemically developed into a visible photograph.

In addition to visible light, all films are sensitive to ultraviolet light, X-rays and gamma rays, and high-energy particles.

Unmodified silver halide crystals are sensitive only to the blue part of the visible spectrum, producing unnatural-looking renditions of some colored subjects.

This problem was resolved with the discovery that certain dyes, called sensitizing dyes, when adsorbed onto the silver halide crystals made them respond to other colors as well.

First orthochromatic (sensitive to blue and green) and finally panchromatic (sensitive to all visible colors) films were developed. Panchromatic film renders all colors in shades of gray approximately matching their subjective brightness. By similar techniques, special-purpose films can be made sensitive to the infrared (IR) region of the spectrum.[2]
