1. Reaction paper based on ROTC modules​


ROTC

Students can make the transition from college to the military and receive both academic and military training through the Reserve Officers' Training Corps (ROTC). Cadets agree to serve eight years in the military in exchange for employment as a commissioned officer following graduation. The chance for cadets to apply for and receive an ROTC scholarship, which pays for all of their tuition, frequently attracts students interested in a career in the armed forces.

The two-, three-, and four-year scholarships offered by ROTC programs are based on how much of your coursework you still have to complete. Most ROTC scholarships fully pay all college expenses, including tuition, board, and books. All majors are allowed to join ROTC and to apply for an ROTC scholarship, but not all universities offer the program, and some fields—such as commerce, engineering, and public policy—are given preference when it comes to funding provided through ROTC scholarships.

In addition to their degree-related coursework, ROTC cadets study military organization, military tactics, and military technology to prepare for work as commissioned officers. They also receive training in the military. Some graduates choose to work in the armed forces in specialized positions as analysts, nurses, or teachers. ROTC has disadvantages, namely:

1. Service commitment in the military

Only if you are convinced that you want to join the military after graduation and believe you can manage an eight-year military service commitment should you commit to ROTC.

2. Additional responsibility

You will need to dedicate yourself to physical training, rising early, and attending additional classes and meetings in addition to finishing your degree requirements. These obligations will be as significant to your academic obligations.

3. Access barriers for people with disabilities

People with certain disabilities cannot participate in ROTC because it needs a particular level of physical fitness.

4. Scholarship requirements

High academic and personal standards are imposed for ROTC scholarships, and if you don't meet them, you risk losing your scholarship.

5. Indefeasible commitment

Before you sign the contract, you must be confident that you want to be in ROTC. You may be subject to legal repercussions and be required to repay your scholarship money if ROTC dismisses you or if you drop out.

Position paper:

The inclusion of Reserve Officer Training Corps (ROTC) in the curriculum of senior high school (SHS) students is a matter that requires serious consideration. The purpose of this paper is to take a stance on the issue and argue that the inclusion of ROTC in the SHS curriculum is necessary.

First and foremost, ROTC provides students with valuable leadership skills, self-discipline, and a sense of responsibility. These skills are crucial not only for success in military careers but also for success in various other fields and aspects of life. ROTC programs also provide students with opportunities to engage in physical fitness activities, which promote healthy habits and wellbeing.

Moreover, ROTC programs are a great way for students to learn about the military, its role in society, and the history of our country. By exposing students to the military, they will gain a deeper understanding of the sacrifices made by the men and women who serve in our armed forces. This knowledge will also help them to become more informed citizens who can make informed decisions about military policies and their impact on society.

Another advantage of including ROTC in the SHS curriculum is that it provides students with a clear path to a military career. The program prepares students for the rigors of military life and provides them with the tools and skills they need to succeed in this demanding field. Furthermore, ROTC programs offer students the opportunity to earn scholarships and other financial benefits, which can make a military career more accessible and affordable.

In conclusion, the inclusion of ROTC in the SHS curriculum is not only necessary but also highly beneficial for students. It provides them with the skills and knowledge they need to succeed in life, exposes them to the military, and offers them a clear path to a military career. Therefore, it is recommended that the Department of Education seriously consider including ROTC in the SHS curriculum.

7. What are the disadvantages of ROTC?​


Answer:

Joining ROTC—especially if you receive a scholarship—is a serious commitment. You must sign a legally-binding contract that could last up to 12 years. Under some circumstances, you must make this decision before even beginning the program.

 

If you drop out or are expelled from the program, you will face serious consequences, including potential legal action. You will also be required to pay back your scholarship if you have received one. (However, keep in mind that many of these consequences only apply to students who do receive ROTC scholarships.)

 

If you commit any violations of ROTC’s standards or your school’s, or drop below ROTC’s academic requirements, you may be asked to leave the program, and you will still face consequences and potential legal action.

 

While individual branch requirements vary, federal law requires that ROTC members must commit to a four-year active duty, usually directly after college, and an additional 8-year military service. These requirements may be slightly lower for non-scholarship students. Ultimately, this means you need to be certain that serving in the military is something you really want to do. Remember that this also means you’ll need to delay other career or education aspirations until your active service is complete, though you can pursue them while you are fulfilling non-active service requirements.
