20. Write the similarities, differences, uses and example ( 1 only ) of ductility and malleability


Answer:

Similarities:

Both ductility and malleability are physical properties of metals.

Both properties describe the ability of a material to deform without breaking or fracturing.

Both properties are important for manufacturing and shaping metal objects.

Differences:

Ductility refers to the ability of a material to deform by stretching under tensile stress, while malleability refers to the ability of a material to deform by compression without cracking or breaking.

Ductility is typically measured by the percentage of elongation or reduction in cross-sectional area, while malleability is typically measured by the ability of a material to be rolled or hammered into thin sheets without breaking.

Ductility is more commonly associated with wire drawing, while malleability is more commonly associated with sheet metal forming.

Uses:

Ductility is important for the manufacture of wires, cables, and other thin structures that require a high degree of tensile strength and flexibility.

Malleability is important for the manufacture of sheet metal products, such as roofs, gutters, and other building components.

Example:

One example of ductility in action is the manufacture of electrical wires, which are typically made from highly ductile metals such as copper, aluminum, or silver. These metals are drawn into thin wires by pulling them through a series of progressively smaller dies until they reach the desired diameter.

An example of malleability in action is the manufacture of aluminum cans, which are made from sheets of aluminum that are rolled and shaped into the familiar cylindrical form. The malleability of aluminum allows it to be easily formed into a variety of shapes and sizes, making it a versatile material for many different types of products.

Ductility and malleability

Similarities:

Both ductility and malleability are physical characteristics that are associated with metals, and they both refer to a metal's ability to be deformed or shaped under the application of force or stress without breaking or cracking.

Differences:

Ductility and malleability are different physical properties of metals that describe their ability to undergo plastic deformation under stress. Ductility specifically pertains to a metal's capacity to be drawn into thin wires or elongated shapes without breaking or cracking, while malleability relates to a metal's capability to be flattened, rolled, or shaped into thin sheets or other forms without fracturing.

Uses:

The properties of ductility and malleability are both essential for the industrial applications of metals. Ductility is particularly important for metals used in electrical wiring, as it enables them to be elongated and drawn into thin wires, which is crucial for effective conduction of electricity. On the other hand, malleability is critical for metals used in construction and manufacturing, as it permits them to be shaped, molded, or flattened into various forms and structures that are required for different applications.

Examples:

Gold is an example of a metal that possesses the property of malleability, which means that it can be molded or flattened into thin sheets or other shapes without cracking or breaking. This quality makes gold a preferred material for jewelry making and decorative arts, where the metal needs to be shaped or formed into intricate designs and patterns.

Copper is an instance of a metal that exhibits the property of ductility. This means that it can be elongated into thin wires without breaking or fracturing, making it a suitable material for electrical wiring applications where the metal is required to conduct electricity efficiently.

24. What are the similar species example with different characteristics​


Answer:

The designation of species originates in taxonomy, where the species is the fundamental unit of classification recognized by the International Commission of Zoological Nomenclature. Every species is assigned a standard two-part name of genus and species. The genus is the generic name that includes closely related species; the gray wolf, for example, is classified as Canis lupus and is a close relative of the coyote found in North America and designated as Canis latrans, their systematic relation indicated by their sharing the same genus name, Canis. Similarly, genera that have shared characters (or traits) are classified in the same taxonomic family; related families are placed in the same order; related orders are placed in the same class; and related classes are placed in the same phylum. This classification system is a hierarchy applied to all animals and plants, as originally set forth by the Swedish naturalist Carolus Linnaeus in the 18th century.

Organisms are grouped into species partly according to their morphological, or external, similarities, but more important in classifying sexually reproducing organisms is the organisms’ ability to successfully interbreed. Individuals of a single species can mate and produce viable offspring with one another but almost never with members of other species. Separate species have been known to produce hybrid offspring (for example, the horse and the donkey producing the mule), but, because the offspring are almost always inviable or sterile, the interbreeding is not considered successful.

Interbreeding only within the species is of great importance for evolution in that individuals of one species share a common gene pool that members of other species do not. Within a single pool there is always a certain amount of variation among individuals, and those whose genetic variations leave them at a disadvantage in a particular environment tend to be eliminated in favour of those with advantageous variations. This process of natural selection results in the gene pool’s evolving in such a way that the advantageous variations become the norm. Because genetic variations originate in individuals of a species and because those individuals pass on their variations only within the species, then it is at the species level that evolution takes place. The evolution of one species into others is called speciation.

9. Indulgence vs. Restraint

Indulgence orientated societies encourage pleasure-seeking. Members pursue fun activities for the sake of personal enjoyment.

In contrast, Restraint societies believe that hedonistic pleasure needs to be curbed and regulated by strict social norms.

The danger in stereotyping

No cultural group is homogenous: there are individual differences in the thoughts and behaviours of members of every cultural group.

Cultural values do not allow one to predict the behaviour and responses of individuals with certainty, yet a working knowledge of how members of a cultural group, in general, think and behave provides a useful starting point for navigating intercultural interactions.

As we interact with diverse others using Cultural Intelligence, we test and reflect on the appropriateness of our cultural assumptions and experiment with alternative responses, actively constructing a cultural profile for each unique exchange.
