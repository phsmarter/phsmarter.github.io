1. What is components of an opera


An opera is a form of theater that has a combination of different theatrical elements of drama such as music, dance, costume, scenery, lighting, and a lot more.

The opera has different components. These are Overture, Recitative, Aria, Chorus, and Ballet. Each of them has a significant function in the play as a whole.

The Overture is the introduction of the opera. This component arranges the music and prepares the audience for ready. The Recitative is the part where the actors' speech takes place.The Aria is the component in which singers express a song.The Chorus is where the opera is in complete rhythm. Finally, the Ballet. It is the last part where the actors finish their story and the performance ends.

17. Give the components of an opera and fine it.​


COMPONENTS OPERA

I'm not sure what you mean by "they fine it," but here are the components of an opera:

1. Overture: An introductory musical piece played by the orchestra at the beginning of an opera.

2. Libretto: The text of the opera, which is written by the librettist and contains the lyrics for the vocal pieces.

3. Aria: A solo vocal piece sung by one of the lead characters, often expressing their emotions or motivations.

4. Recitative: A style of singing in which the singer partially speaks the text to move the story along, often used for dialogue.

5. Ensemble: A vocal piece sung by multiple characters on stage at the same time, often used for group scenes.

6. Chorus: A group of singers who sing together in harmony, often used to depict a larger group of people such as soldiers or peasants.

7. Set design: The physical scenery and props used to create the world of the opera, designed by the set designer.

8. Costume design: The clothing and accessories worn by the characters, designed by the costume designer.

9. Lighting design: The use of different types of lighting to create mood and highlight important moments in the opera, designed by the lighting designer.

10. Orchestration: The arrangement of music for the orchestra, composed by the composer of the opera.

A triumphal march is a musical form generally reflecting a triumph, victory or great joy.

Many composers have written a triumphal march, with maybe the best known one being by Italian opera composer, Giuseppe Verdi for his 1871 grand opera, Aida, where, in the second act, Radames leads the Egyptian army on its return following their victory over the Ethiopians. The triumphal scene gives directors the opportunity for elaborate spectacle typical of the grand opera of the period in the nineteenth century.
