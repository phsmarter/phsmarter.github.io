23. The Philippine Government during the American Regime.


Answer:

The history of the Philippines from 1898 to 1946 describes the period of the American colonialization of the Philippines. It began with the outbreak of the Spanish–American War in April 1898, when the Philippines was still a colony of the Spanish East Indies, and concluded when the United States formally recognized the independence of the Republic of the Philippines on July 4, 1946.

With the signing of the Treaty of Paris on December 10, 1898, Spain ceded the Philippines to the United States, thereby beginning the era of American colonialization.[1] The interim U.S. military government of the Philippine Islands experienced a period of great political turbulence, characterised by the Philippine–American War.

Beginning in 1901, the military government was replaced by a civilian government—the Insular Government of the Philippine Islands—with William Howard Taft serving as its first Governor-General. Also, a series of insurgent governments that lacked significant international and diplomatic recognition existed between 1898 and 1904.[a]

Following the passage of the Philippine Independence Act in 1934, a Philippine presidential election was held in 1935. Manuel L. Quezon was elected and inaugurated second President of the Philippines on November 15, 1935. The Insular Government was dissolved and the Commonwealth of the Philippines, intended to be a transitional government in preparation for the country's full achievement of independence in 1946, was brought into existence.[2]

After the World War II Japanese invasion in 1941 and subsequent occupation of the Philippines, the United States and Philippine Commonwealth military recaptured the Philippines in 1945. The United States formally recognised the independence of the Republic of the Philippines on July 4, 1946, according to the terms of the Philippine Independence Act.[2]

30. What was the American regime in the Philippines?​


Answer:

The American regime in the Philippines was a period of time during which the Philippines was under the control of the United States. It began in 1898, when the United States defeated Spain in the Spanish–American War, and ended in 1946, when the Philippines gained its independence.

During the American regime, the Philippines underwent a number of changes. The United States established a new government in the Philippines, and it also introduced a number of reforms, including the establishment of a public school system, the development of infrastructure, and the promotion of economic development.

The American regime was not without its critics. Some Filipinos felt that the United States was exploiting the Philippines, and they wanted independence. Others felt that the United States was not doing enough to promote the welfare of the Filipino people.

Despite the criticisms, the American regime had a number of positive effects on the Philippines. The United States helped to modernize the Philippines, and it also helped to promote democracy and human rights. The Philippines also benefited from the economic development that took place during the American regime.

The American regime in the Philippines was a complex period of time. It was a time of change and progress, but it was also a time of conflict and controversy. The American regime had a lasting impact on the Philippines, and it continues to be a source of debate and discussion today.

The American regime in the Philippines refers to the period of time from 1898 to 1946, when the United States governed the Philippines as a colonial power. This period began when the United States acquired the Philippines from Spain as part of the Treaty of Paris, which ended the Spanish-American War.

During this period, the United States established a colonial government in the Philippines, which was headed by an American Governor-General. The colonial government was tasked with modernizing and developing the Philippines, which was seen as a "civilizing mission" by the American government.

The American regime brought about many changes in the Philippines, including the establishment of a modern education system, the construction of infrastructure such as roads and bridges, and the development of the country's economy. However, the regime was also marked by widespread corruption, repression, and violence, particularly during the Philippine-American War, which lasted from 1899 to 1902.

The American regime in the Philippines ended in 1946, when the Philippines gained independence from the United States.
